10.01 Introduction

Effective business correspondence reflects professionalism and clarity. This chapter provides comprehensive guidance for writing letters, memorandums, and electronic communications in the Canadian government context.

Types of Business Correspondence

üìÑ Formal Letters

External communications requiring official letterhead and signatures

üìã Memorandums

Internal communications for information sharing and decision-making

üìß Email Communication

Electronic correspondence for routine business matters

üì® Transmittal Letters

Cover letters accompanying reports, documents, or materials

Universal Principles

  • Clarity: State your purpose clearly and concisely
  • Courtesy: Maintain professional and respectful tone
  • Completeness: Include all necessary information
  • Correctness: Ensure accuracy in facts, spelling, and grammar
  • Consistency: Follow established formatting standards

10.02 Business Letters

Business letters follow established conventions for structure, tone, and formatting. They represent your organization and must maintain professional standards.

Standard Letter Components

  1. Letterhead: Organization name, logo, and contact information
  2. Date: Full date in Canadian format
  3. Inside Address: Recipient's name, title, and address
  4. Salutation: Appropriate greeting
  5. Body: Main message in logical paragraphs
  6. Complimentary Close: Professional closing phrase
  7. Signature Block: Name, title, and contact information
  8. Reference Information: Initials, enclosures, copies

10.04 Memorandums

Memorandums (memos) are internal communications used for information sharing, policy announcements, and decision documentation within organizations.

Memo Structure

  • Header: MEMORANDUM or organization name
  • TO: Recipient(s) - name and title
  • FROM: Sender - name and title
  • DATE: Full date
  • SUBJECT: Clear, descriptive subject line
  • Body: Purpose, background, discussion, action required
  • Attachments: Supporting documents (if any)

10.05 Formatting Standards

Consistent formatting ensures professional appearance and improves readability. Follow these standards for all business correspondence.

üìè Layout Standards

Page Setup

  • Paper: 8.5" √ó 11" (216 mm √ó 279 mm)
  • Margins: 1 inch (25 mm) all sides
  • Font: Times New Roman 12pt or Arial 11pt
  • Line spacing: Single space within paragraphs
  • Paragraph spacing: One blank line between paragraphs

Letter Spacing

  • Date: 3-4 lines below letterhead
  • Inside address: 3-4 lines below date
  • Salutation: 2 lines below address
  • Body: 2 lines below salutation
  • Closing: 2 lines below body
  • Signature: 4 lines space for handwritten signature

Alignment & Style

  • Alignment: Left-aligned (standard business style)
  • Paragraphs: No indentation, block format
  • Page numbers: Bottom center or top right
  • Continuation pages: Include name and page number
  • Letterhead: Only on first page

üé® Visual Elements

Typography Guidelines

‚úì Preferred fonts:

  • Times New Roman 12pt (formal letters)
  • Arial 11pt (memos and informal correspondence)
  • Calibri 11pt (electronic documents)

‚úó Avoid:

  • Decorative fonts (Script, Comic Sans)
  • Very small fonts (under 10pt)
  • All capitals for body text
  • Multiple fonts in one document

Professional Presentation

  • White paper: Use high-quality white paper for printed correspondence
  • Clean printing: Ensure clear, dark text with no smudges
  • Proper folding: Fold letters in thirds for standard envelopes
  • Envelope addressing: Match letterhead style and include return address
  • Electronic formatting: Ensure compatibility across different systems

üìù Multi-page Documents

Continuation Pages

  • Use plain paper (no letterhead) for second and subsequent pages
  • Include recipient name, page number, and date at top
  • Leave at least two lines of a paragraph on continuation page
  • Never start a continuation page with signature block alone

10.07 Transmittal Letters

Transmittal letters accompany reports, proposals, or other documents, providing context and highlighting key points for the recipient.

Transmittal Letter Purpose

  • Introduction: Present the accompanying document
  • Context: Explain why the document was prepared
  • Highlights: Draw attention to key findings or recommendations
  • Action: Specify what the recipient should do
  • Authorization: Provide official approval or endorsement

üí° Transmittal Letter Best Practices

Content Guidelines

  • Keep the letter concise (1-2 pages maximum)
  • Focus on what's most important to the reader
  • Use specific details and quantifiable results
  • Avoid repeating information from the document
  • Include clear action items or next steps

Professional Presentation

  • Use official letterhead and formal format
  • Include complete enclosure descriptions
  • Copy relevant stakeholders appropriately
  • Provide contact information for follow-up
  • Ensure consistent tone with document content

10.08 Electronic Correspondence

Email has become the primary means of business communication. Apply professional standards to ensure clarity and maintain your organization's reputation.

Email Best Practices

  • Subject Line: Clear, specific, and informative
  • Professional Tone: Formal language for external communications
  • Concise Content: Get to the point quickly and clearly
  • Proper Formatting: Use paragraphs and white space effectively
  • Signature Block: Include complete contact information
  • Security: Protect sensitive information appropriately

Email Communication Guidelines

‚úÖ Professional Standards

  • Use descriptive subject lines with keywords
  • Start with appropriate greeting
  • Keep paragraphs short (3-4 lines)
  • Use bullet points for multiple items
  • Include clear action items with deadlines
  • End with professional closing
  • Use complete signature block

‚ùå Common Email Mistakes

  • Vague subject lines ("FYI," "Important")
  • No greeting or too casual greeting
  • Wall of text without breaks
  • Unclear action items or deadlines
  • Reply-all when not necessary
  • Forwarding without context
  • Missing or incomplete signatures

üîí Security Considerations

  • Mark sensitive emails "CONFIDENTIAL"
  • Use encryption for classified information
  • Verify recipient addresses before sending
  • Be cautious with "Reply All" function
  • Don't send personal information via email
  • Use secure file sharing for large attachments
  • Follow organizational IT policies

üì¨ Email Response Etiquette

Response Timing

  • Urgent requests: Within 4 hours during business hours
  • Standard business emails: Within 24 hours
  • Complex requests: Acknowledge receipt within 24 hours, provide full response timeline
  • Auto-replies: Use when away for more than one business day

Thread Management

  • Keep related emails in the same thread
  • Update subject line if topic changes significantly
  • Quote relevant portions when responding
  • Start new threads for new topics
  • Remove unnecessary recipients as threads evolve
