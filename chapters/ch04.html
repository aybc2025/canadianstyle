4.01 General Principles

üìè Foundation of Good Writing

Proper capitalization enhances readability and shows attention to detail in professional writing.

Basic Capitalization Rules

Always Capitalize

  • First word of a sentence
  • Proper nouns (specific names)
  • First word of a direct quotation
  • Major words in titles

Generally Don't Capitalize

  • Common nouns (general categories)
  • Articles (a, an, the) in titles
  • Prepositions under 5 letters in titles
  • Generic references to titles or positions

The Context Matters

‚úÖ Specific The Prime Minister announced new policies.
‚ùå Generic A prime minister has many responsibilities.

üí° Quick Test

Ask yourself: "Am I referring to a specific, unique person, place, or thing?" If yes, capitalize. If it's a general category, use lowercase.

4.02 Proper Nouns

üåç Names of Specific Things

Proper nouns name specific, unique entities and are always capitalized.

Categories of Proper Nouns

People

  • Sarah Johnson
  • Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
  • Prime Minister Trudeau

Places

  • Canada, Ontario, Toronto
  • Lake Superior
  • Rocky Mountains

Organizations

  • University of Toronto
  • Canadian Broadcasting Corporation
  • Parliament of Canada

Events & Periods

  • World War II
  • Renaissance
  • Canada Day

Geographic Names

Capitalize all parts of official geographic names:
  • Lake Ontario (not Lake ontario)
  • British Columbia (not british Columbia)
  • St. Lawrence River (not St. lawrence River)
Don't capitalize general geographic references:
  • the lake (when referring generally)
  • the province
  • the river

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake

Don't capitalize directional words unless they're part of a proper name or refer to a specific region: "drive north" vs. "the North" (Arctic region).

4.03 Titles and Positions

üëë When Titles Get Capital Letters

The capitalization of titles depends on their use and position in the sentence.

Title Capitalization Rules

‚úÖ Capitalize When:

  • Before a name: President Smith, Dr. Johnson
  • As direct address: "Thank you, Prime Minister."
  • In formal contexts: The President announced...
  • In signatures/letterheads: John Smith, Director

‚ùå Don't Capitalize When:

  • Used generically: "The prime minister's role..."
  • After a name: "John Smith, the director, said..."
  • Plural forms: "Several presidents attended"
  • Former positions: "the former mayor"

Examples in Context

Government Positions
‚úÖ Prime Minister Trudeau announced ‚ùå The Prime Minister of canada
‚úÖ The prime minister's duties ‚ùå Several Prime Ministers met
Professional Titles
‚úÖ Dr. Sarah Wilson ‚ùå Sarah Wilson, The Doctor
‚úÖ Professor Martinez teaches ‚ùå She is a Professor of history

If you can replace the title with the person's name and the sentence still makes sense, capitalize it. "President Smith arrived" ‚Üí "John arrived" ‚úÖ

4.04 Organizations

üè¢ Institutional Names

Organization names are proper nouns and follow specific capitalization patterns.

Types of Organizations

Government Bodies

  • Parliament of Canada
  • House of Commons
  • Department of National Defence
  • Statistics Canada

Capitalize all significant words in official government names.

Educational Institutions

  • University of British Columbia
  • McGill University
  • York University
  • Centennial College

Include articles and prepositions as written in official names.

Corporations & NGOs

  • Canadian Red Cross
  • Royal Bank of Canada
  • Air Canada
  • United Nations

Follow the organization's official capitalization.

Generic vs. Specific References

Specific (Capitalize)
  • The University of Toronto announced
  • Parliament voted today
  • The Department issued guidelines
Generic (Lowercase)
  • She attends university
  • The parliamentary system
  • Each department has policies

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For

  • Shortened forms: "the University" (if clear from context)
  • Informal references: "the feds" for federal government
  • Acronyms: CBC, RCMP, UN (always capitalized)

4.05 Academic Subjects

üìö School Subject Capitalization

Academic subjects follow specific rules based on whether they're proper nouns or general fields.

When to Capitalize Academic Subjects

‚úÖ Always Capitalize

Languages
  • English, French, Spanish
  • Mandarin, Arabic, Hindi
Specific Course Titles
  • Introduction to Psychology 101
  • Advanced Calculus
  • Canadian History Since 1867
Proper Nouns in Subject Names
  • American literature
  • Shakespearean drama
  • Newtonian physics

‚ùå Generally Don't Capitalize

General Subject Areas
  • mathematics, chemistry, biology
  • psychology, sociology, philosophy
  • history, geography, economics
Academic Fields
  • She studies engineering
  • He majors in computer science
  • The physics department

Context Examples

Course Registration

‚úÖ "I'm taking English 201 and advanced chemistry this semester."

Course title gets capitals; general subject doesn't.

Academic Discussion

‚úÖ "She has a degree in mathematics with a minor in French."

General field lowercase; language capitalized.

Department References

‚úÖ "The English Department offers many literature courses."

Department name is capitalized as proper noun.

üí° Quick Rule

Languages are always capitalized. Other subjects are capitalized only when they're part of a specific course title or contain proper nouns.

4.06 Special Cases

üéØ Unique Situations

Some capitalization situations require special attention and memorization.

Religious Terms

‚úÖ Capitalize

  • Names of deities: God, Allah, Buddha
  • Religious texts: Bible, Quran, Torah
  • Specific religions: Christianity, Islam, Judaism
  • Religious figures: Jesus Christ, Prophet Muhammad

‚ùå Don't Capitalize

  • General religious terms: prayer, worship, faith
  • Religious concepts: heaven, hell (unless in specific theological context)
  • General references: a god, the gods

Holidays and Events

Capitalize All Holiday Names

Canadian Holidays
  • Canada Day
  • Victoria Day
  • Thanksgiving Day
  • Remembrance Day
International Holidays
  • Christmas Day
  • New Year's Day
  • Mother's Day
  • Father's Day

Seasons and Time Periods

‚ùå Don't Capitalize Seasons

spring, summer, fall/autumn, winter

"We'll review this in the spring."

‚úÖ Capitalize Historical Periods

Renaissance, Middle Ages, Great Depression

"The Renaissance brought many changes."

Brand Names and Trademarks

Always Capitalize Brand Names

  • Coca-Cola (not coca-cola)
  • iPhone (following company styling)
  • Microsoft Word
  • Tim Hortons

Note: Follow the company's official capitalization, even if it seems unconventional (like iPhone or eBay).

üéâ Capitalization Mastery

With these rules mastered, your writing will demonstrate professional attention to detail. Proper capitalization enhances credibility and readability.
