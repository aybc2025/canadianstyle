Capitalization

Master the rules for capitalizing words in Canadian government writing

4.01 Introduction

Capitalization rules help readers distinguish between different types of words and understand their importance. Consistent capitalization enhances clarity and follows established conventions in Canadian government writing.

Key Principle

Capitalize proper nouns (specific names) and the first word of sentences. When in doubt, use lowercase unless you have a specific reason to capitalize.

Basic Capitalization Rules

  • First words: Sentences, quotations, lines of poetry
  • Proper nouns: Names of specific people, places, things
  • Proper adjectives: Adjectives derived from proper nouns
  • Official titles: When used before names
  • Important words in titles: Books, documents, headings

Proper vs. Common Nouns

Common Noun (lowercase) Proper Noun (capitalized)
the minister Minister Johnson
the department Department of Justice Canada
the city Ottawa
the university University of Toronto
canadian literature Canadian literature

When NOT to Capitalize

  • Generic references: "the department announced" (not specific)
  • Common nouns: "government employees" (general group)
  • Seasons: spring, summer, fall, winter
  • Directions: north, south, east, west (unless part of proper name)
  • Academic subjects: mathematics, english (unless language or specific course)

⚠️ Overcapitalization

Avoid unnecessary capitalization. Government writing should not capitalize words simply for emphasis or perceived importance. Use capitalization rules consistently.

4.02 Initial Words

The first word of sentences, quotations, and certain other constructions should be capitalized according to specific rules.

Sentence Beginnings

  • First word of sentence: Always capitalize
  • After periods: New sentence begins
  • After colons: Capitalize if complete sentence follows
  • After question marks: New sentence begins
  • After exclamation marks: New sentence begins

Colon Usage

✓ Complete sentence after colon (capitalize):

The report reached one conclusion: The current system is inadequate.

✓ Fragment after colon (lowercase):

The report identified three problems: budget constraints, staff shortages, and outdated equipment.

Quotations

  • Direct quotations: Capitalize first word if complete sentence
  • Partial quotations: Don't capitalize unless proper noun
  • Interrupted quotations: Don't capitalize resumption
  • Block quotations: Follow original capitalization

Quotation Examples

Complete sentence:

The Minister said, "This policy will benefit all Canadians."

Partial quotation:

The Minister described the policy as "beneficial to all Canadians."

Interrupted quotation:

"This policy," said the Minister, "will benefit all Canadians."

Lists and Bullet Points

  • Sentence-style lists: Capitalize first word of each item
  • Fragment lists: Usually lowercase unless proper noun
  • Numbered lists: Follow same rules as bullet points
  • Consistency: Be consistent within each list

List Capitalization

Complete sentences:

The committee will consider three options:

  • The department can hire additional staff.
  • Current employees can receive overtime pay.
  • External contractors can supplement the team.

Fragments:

The committee identified three needs:

  • additional staff
  • overtime funding
  • external support

After Dashes and Parentheses

  • Em dashes: Don't capitalize unless new sentence
  • Parenthetical sentences: Capitalize if independent
  • Parenthetical fragments: Don't capitalize

💡 Consistency Rule

Whatever style you choose for lists, headings, or other elements, maintain it consistently throughout your document.

4.03 Personal Names

Personal names and related elements require careful attention to capitalization, especially in formal government documents where accuracy and respect are essential.

Names and Titles

  • Full names: Capitalize all parts (John Smith)
  • Surnames only: Capitalize (Mr. Smith)
  • Given names only: Capitalize (John)
  • Titles before names: Capitalize (Dr. Smith, Prime Minister Trudeau)
  • Titles after names: Usually lowercase (John Smith, prime minister)

Title Usage

✓ Titles before names (capitalize):

  • Prime Minister Trudeau announced the policy
  • Minister Johnson will attend the meeting
  • Dr. Smith presented the research
  • Professor Brown teaches at the university

✓ Titles after names or alone (lowercase):

  • Justin Trudeau, prime minister of Canada
  • Sarah Johnson, minister of health
  • The prime minister announced the policy
  • The minister will attend the meeting

Honorific Titles

  • The Honourable: Before names of certain officials
  • The Right Honourable: Prime Minister and certain others
  • His/Her Excellency: Governor General, ambassadors
  • His/Her Majesty: Monarchs

Canadian Official Titles

Examples of formal titles:

  • The Right Honourable Justin Trudeau, Prime Minister of Canada
  • The Honourable Chrystia Freeland, Deputy Prime Minister
  • Her Excellency Mary Simon, Governor General of Canada
  • The Honourable Justice Smith (Supreme Court)

Academic and Professional Designations

  • Academic degrees: Capitalize when abbreviated (Ph.D., M.A.)
  • Professional titles: Capitalize before names (Professor Smith)
  • Generic references: Lowercase (the professor, the doctor)
  • Department affiliations: Capitalize official names

Academic Examples

Specific references (capitalize):

  • Professor Sarah Brown, Department of Economics
  • Dr. Michael Johnson, Chief Medical Officer
  • Dean Elizabeth Wilson

Generic references (lowercase):

  • The professor presented the research
  • A doctor reviewed the proposal
  • The dean approved the program

Cultural and Religious Considerations

  • Names from other cultures: Follow individual preferences
  • Religious titles: Capitalize before names (Father Smith, Rabbi Cohen)
  • Nobility titles: Capitalize when part of name (Sir John)
  • Indigenous names: Respect traditional naming conventions

Special Cases

Various naming conventions:

  • Jean-Pierre Dubois (hyphenated French name)
  • Mary Two Eagles (Indigenous name)
  • Li Wei (Chinese name order)
  • van der Berg (Dutch particles)
  • O'Brien (Irish prefix)

💡 Verification

When writing about specific individuals, verify the correct spelling and capitalization of their names and titles. Check official biographies or government directories.

⚠️ Respectful Usage

Always use names and titles respectfully and accurately. When uncertain about preferences, ask the individual or consult official sources.

4.04 Government

Government entities, institutions, and bodies have specific capitalization conventions that reflect their official status and help distinguish between general and specific references.

Government Institutions

  • Specific institutions: Capitalize official names
  • Generic references: Lowercase general terms
  • Parliament: Capitalize when referring to Canadian Parliament
  • Government: Capitalize when referring to specific administration

Parliamentary References

✓ Specific institutions (capitalize):

  • Parliament of Canada
  • House of Commons
  • Senate of Canada
  • Supreme Court of Canada
  • Federal Court of Canada

✓ Generic references (lowercase):

  • The parliamentary committee met yesterday
  • Federal and provincial governments
  • The supreme court of the province
  • Municipal governments across Canada

Federal Departments and Agencies

  • Full official names: Always capitalize
  • Short forms: Capitalize if commonly recognized
  • Generic references: Lowercase "the department"
  • Crown corporations: Capitalize official names

Department Examples

Full Name Short Form Generic Reference
Department of Justice Canada Justice Canada the department
Health Canada Health Canada the health department
Employment and Social Development Canada ESDC the employment department
Canada Revenue Agency CRA the revenue agency

Provincial and Municipal Government

  • Official names: Government of Ontario, City of Toronto
  • Generic references: provincial government, municipal council
  • Legislatures: Legislative Assembly of Ontario
  • Courts: Ontario Court of Justice

Provincial Examples

✓ Specific (capitalize):

  • Government of British Columbia
  • Legislative Assembly of Alberta
  • Assemblée nationale du Québec
  • City of Vancouver
  • Region of Peel

✓ Generic (lowercase):

  • The provincial government announced
  • Municipal governments across Ontario
  • The legislative assembly voted
  • Regional and municipal councils

International References

  • Foreign governments: Government of the United States
  • International bodies: United Nations, European Union
  • Treaties and agreements: North American Free Trade Agreement
  • Generic references: foreign governments, international organizations

Legal and Constitutional Terms

Constitutional documents and concepts:

  • Constitution Act, 1982
  • Charter of Rights and Freedoms
  • British North America Act
  • Crown copyright (the Crown as institution)
  • The Crown in Right of Canada

Committees and Boards

  • Standing committees: Standing Committee on Finance
  • Special committees: Special Committee on Electoral Reform
  • Boards and tribunals: Immigration and Refugee Board
  • Advisory bodies: Economic Advisory Council

💡 Official Names

Always verify official names of government bodies. They may change over time or have specific formal designations that differ from common usage.

4.05 Titles

Titles of publications, documents, artworks, and other works follow specific capitalization conventions. These rules help distinguish titles from regular text and show respect for creative works.

Title Case Rules

Capitalize:

  • First and last words always
  • All nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs
  • Subordinating conjunctions (because, although, since)
  • Prepositions of 5+ letters (between, through, without)

Don't capitalize:

  • Articles: a, an, the
  • Short prepositions: of, on, in, at, by, for
  • Coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or, nor, yet, so

Title Capitalization Examples

✓ Correct capitalization:

  • The Canadian Style: A Guide to Writing and Editing
  • Report on the Impact of Climate Change in Northern Communities
  • Guidelines for Writing Effective Policy Documents
  • Building a Better Future: Strategic Plan 2024-2029
  • What Every Canadian Should Know About Digital Privacy

✗ Incorrect capitalization:

  • The canadian style: a guide to writing and editing
  • Report On The Impact Of Climate Change In Northern Communities
  • Guidelines For Writing Effective Policy Documents
  • Building A Better Future: Strategic Plan 2024-2029
  • what every canadian should know about digital privacy

Government Document Titles

  • Acts and legislation: Use official capitalization
  • Policy documents: Follow title case rules
  • Reports: Capitalize as published
  • Regulations: Use official names

Legal and Policy Documents

Legislation and regulations:

  • Privacy Act
  • Access to Information Act
  • Criminal Code
  • Official Languages Regulations
  • Immigration and Refugee Protection Act

Policy and strategy documents:

  • Federal Sustainable Development Strategy
  • Digital Operations Strategic Plan
  • Policy on Service and Digital
  • Directive on Automated Decision-Making

Academic and Scientific Works

  • Books: Title case, italicized
  • Journal articles: Title case, in quotation marks
  • Reports: Title case, usually italicized
  • Conferences: Title case for formal names

Academic Citation Examples

Various publication types:

  • Public Administration in Canada: Selected Readings (book)
  • "The Role of Technology in Modern Governance" (article)
  • Annual Report on the Access to Information Act (report)
  • Canadian Public Administration Conference (conference)

Media and Entertainment

  • Newspapers: The Globe and Mail, Toronto Star
  • Magazines: Maclean's, Canadian Geographic
  • TV shows: The National, Marketplace
  • Movies: Atanarjuat: The Fast Runner

Special Cases

  • Hyphenated words: Capitalize both parts if both are important
  • Subtitles: Capitalize first word after colon
  • Series titles: Follow individual volume capitalization
  • Quotations in titles: Maintain original capitalization

Special Capitalization Cases

Hyphenated and complex titles:

  • Twenty-First Century Challenges in Public Administration
  • E-Government: Digital Transformation in the Public Sector
  • French-English Relations: A Historical Perspective
  • Post-Secondary Education: Trends and Opportunities

💡 Consistency Check

When citing the same work multiple times, ensure consistent capitalization. Keep a style sheet for frequently referenced documents.

⚠️ Official vs. Common Usage

Some titles may be commonly written with different capitalization than their official form. Always check the official version for formal documents.

4.06 Geography

Geographic names and directional terms follow specific capitalization rules that help distinguish between proper place names and general geographic references.

Place Names

  • Countries: Canada, United States, France
  • Provinces/States: Ontario, British Columbia, California
  • Cities: Ottawa, Toronto, Vancouver
  • Geographic features: Hudson Bay, Rocky Mountains, Lake Ontario
  • Regions: Atlantic Canada, the Prairies, the Maritimes

Canadian Geographic Names

Provinces and territories:

  • British Columbia, Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba
  • Ontario, Quebec, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia
  • Prince Edward Island, Newfoundland and Labrador
  • Northwest Territories, Nunavut, Yukon

Major cities:

  • Ottawa, Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver
  • Calgary, Edmonton, Winnipeg, Halifax
  • Quebec City, Victoria, Regina, Charlottetown
  • St. John's, Whitehorse, Yellowknife, Iqaluit

Directions and Regions

  • Specific regions: Capitalize when referring to recognized areas
  • General directions: Lowercase compass directions
  • Adjectives: Usually capitalize regional adjectives
  • Part of proper name: Capitalize when part of official name

Directional Terms

✓ Specific regions (capitalize):

  • Western Canada, Eastern Canada
  • Northern Ontario, Southern Alberta
  • the North (referring to northern Canada)
  • the West (referring to western provinces)
  • the Maritimes, the Prairies

✓ General directions (lowercase):

  • Travel north on Highway 401
  • The wind is blowing from the west
  • Turn south at the intersection
  • The office is located east of downtown

Natural Features

  • Named features: Lake Superior, Mount Logan, Fraser River
  • Generic terms: the lake, the mountain, the river
  • Multiple features: the Great Lakes, the Canadian Rockies
  • Unofficial names: Follow local usage

Natural Features

Type Specific Name (capitalize) Generic Reference (lowercase)
Lakes Lake Ontario, Great Bear Lake the lake, northern lakes
Rivers St. Lawrence River, Mackenzie River the river, major rivers
Mountains Mount Logan, Rocky Mountains the mountain, mountain ranges
Bays Hudson Bay, Georgian Bay the bay, arctic bays

Streets and Addresses

  • Street names: Capitalize all parts of official names
  • Abbreviations: Follow postal conventions
  • Directions in names: Capitalize when part of street name
  • Generic terms: Lowercase when not part of specific name

Address Examples

Street addresses:

  • 123 Main Street
  • 456 Queen Street West
  • 789 Rue Saint-Jacques
  • 321 Broadway Avenue
  • 654 Parliament Hill Circle

International Locations

  • Country names: Follow official English names
  • Major cities: Use established English forms
  • Foreign terms: Capitalize according to local conventions
  • Translated names: Follow accepted English translations

International Examples

Countries and cities:

  • United Kingdom, United States, People's Republic of China
  • London, New York, Beijing, Mexico City
  • Côte d'Ivoire, São Paulo, München (when using local names)
  • Ivory Coast, Sao Paulo, Munich (anglicized forms)

💡 Official Sources

For Canadian place names, consult the Canadian Geographical Names Database. For international locations, use official government sources or established references.

⚠️ Changing Names

Geographic names can change over time. Verify current official names, especially for international locations and Indigenous place names that may have been updated.

4.07 Publications

Publications, media, and creative works have specific capitalization and formatting conventions that help distinguish different types of content and show proper attribution.

Books and Major Publications

  • Titles: Use title case capitalization
  • Formatting: Italicize book titles
  • Subtitles: Capitalize first word after colon
  • Series: Capitalize series names

Book Examples

Canadian books and publications:

  • The Canadian Style: A Guide to Writing and Editing
  • Two Solitudes by Hugh MacLennan
  • A Short History of Canada by Desmond Morton
  • The Handmaid's Tale by Margaret Atwood
  • Who Has Seen the Wind by W.O. Mitchell

Periodicals

  • Newspapers: Italicize, include "The" if part of official name
  • Magazines: Italicize titles
  • Journals: Italicize, use full official titles
  • Newsletters: Italicize or use quotation marks consistently

Periodical Examples

Type Examples Notes
Newspapers The Globe and Mail, Toronto Star, La Presse Include "The" if official
Magazines Maclean's, Canadian Geographic, L'actualité Always italicized
Academic Journals Canadian Public Administration, Policy Options Use full titles
Government Publications Canada Gazette, Parliamentary Debates Official publication names

Articles and Shorter Works

  • Journal articles: Title case, quotation marks
  • Newspaper articles: Title case, quotation marks
  • Book chapters: Title case, quotation marks
  • Reports: Usually italicized if standalone

Article Citation Examples

Various article types:

  • "Digital Government Transformation in Canada" (Policy Options)
  • "Budget 2024 Highlights" (The Globe and Mail)
  • "Public Service Reform" in Canadian Public Administration
  • "Indigenous Reconciliation Efforts" (Maclean's)

Digital and Electronic Media

  • Websites: Italicize site names
  • Blog posts: Quotation marks for individual posts
  • Podcasts: Italicize series names
  • Videos: Quotation marks for individual episodes

Digital Media Examples

Online content:

  • Canada.ca (website)
  • "Digital Services Updates" on Canada.ca (blog post)
  • The House (CBC Radio podcast)
  • "Budget Analysis" episode of The House
  • The National (TV program)

Government Documents

  • Acts and legislation: Italicize when referring to the document
  • Regulations: Follow legislative format
  • Policy documents: Title case, usually italicized
  • Reports: Title case, italicized if major publication

Government Document Examples

Official government publications:

  • Privacy Act (legislation)
  • Federal Sustainable Development Strategy (policy document)
  • Public Accounts of Canada (annual report)
  • Report of the Auditor General (annual publication)
  • Budget 2024: Fairness for Every Generation (budget document)

Creative Works

  • Films: Italicize titles
  • TV shows: Italicize series titles
  • Songs: Quotation marks for individual songs
  • Albums: Italicize album titles
  • Artworks: Italicize titles

Canadian Creative Works

Films and television:

  • Atanarjuat: The Fast Runner (film)
  • Corner Gas (TV series)
  • Anne of Green Gables (various adaptations)
  • The Red Green Show (TV series)

Music and art:

  • "The Weight" by The Band (song)
  • Music from Big Pink by The Band (album)
  • The Jack Pine by Tom Thomson (painting)

💡 Consistency is Key

Choose a consistent style for formatting publications and stick with it throughout your document. The most important thing is consistency rather than perfect adherence to a particular style guide.

4.08 Headings

Headings organize documents and guide readers through content. Consistent capitalization in headings improves readability and creates a professional appearance.

Heading Capitalization Styles

  • Title case: Capitalize major words (recommended for formal documents)
  • Sentence case: Capitalize only first word and proper nouns
  • All caps: Use sparingly, can be hard to read
  • Initial caps: Capitalize first letter of each word (avoid)

Heading Style Comparison

Title case (recommended):

  • Implementation of Digital Services Strategy
  • Budget Analysis and Financial Projections
  • Recommendations for Policy Development
  • Next Steps and Timeline for Action

Sentence case (acceptable):

  • Implementation of digital services strategy
  • Budget analysis and financial projections
  • Recommendations for policy development
  • Next steps and timeline for action

All caps (use sparingly):

  • IMPLEMENTATION OF DIGITAL SERVICES STRATEGY
  • BUDGET ANALYSIS AND FINANCIAL PROJECTIONS

Title Case Rules for Headings

Capitalize:

  • First and last words always
  • All nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs
  • Pronouns (I, you, he, she, it, we, they)
  • Subordinating conjunctions (because, although, if, when)

Don't capitalize:

  • Articles: a, an, the
  • Short prepositions: of, in, on, at, by, for, with
  • Coordinating conjunctions: and, but, or, nor, yet, so

Government Document Headings

✓ Correct title case:

  • Analysis of Current Economic Conditions
  • Report on the Status of Digital Transformation
  • Guidelines for Effective Public Consultation
  • Review of Policies and Procedures
  • Implementation Plan for the New System

✗ Incorrect capitalization:

  • Analysis Of Current Economic Conditions
  • Report On The Status Of Digital Transformation
  • Guidelines For Effective Public Consultation
  • review of policies and procedures
  • IMPLEMENTATION PLAN FOR THE NEW SYSTEM

Numbered and Lettered Headings

  • Numbers and letters: Follow with period or parenthesis
  • Decimal system: 1.1, 1.2, 1.2.1, 1.2.2
  • Traditional outline: I. A. 1. a.
  • Consistency: Use same system throughout document

Numbered Heading Examples

Decimal numbering system:

  • 1. Executive Summary
  • 2. Current Situation Analysis
  •   2.1 Background and Context
  •   2.2 Key Challenges Identified
  •     2.2.1 Budget Constraints
  •     2.2.2 Staffing Issues
  • 3. Recommendations and Next Steps

Special Heading Situations

  • Questions as headings: Capitalize as complete sentences
  • Acronyms in headings: Keep original capitalization
  • Hyphenated words: Capitalize both parts if both are significant
  • Proper nouns: Always capitalize regardless of position

Special Cases

Various special situations:

  • What Are the Benefits of Digital Transformation? (question)
  • Implementation of AI-Based Solutions (hyphenated term)
  • Compliance with GDPR Requirements (acronym)
  • Collaboration Between Canada and the United States (proper nouns)
  • Twenty-First Century Challenges in Governance (hyphenated number)

Table of Contents Considerations

  • Exact replication: TOC should match heading capitalization
  • Page references: Ensure accuracy
  • Indentation: Show heading hierarchy clearly
  • Dot leaders: Connect headings to page numbers

Table of Contents Format

💡 Automated Tools

Most word processors can automatically generate tables of contents from your headings. Use heading styles consistently to take advantage of these features.

⚠️ Accessibility

Proper heading structure isn't just about appearance—it's essential for accessibility. Screen readers use heading hierarchy to help users navigate documents.
