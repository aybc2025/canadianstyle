4.01 Introduction

Capitalization helps readers distinguish between common and proper nouns and emphasizes certain words. Canadian style tends toward minimal capitalization‚Äîwhen in doubt, use lowercase.

General Principles

  • Proper nouns: Capitalize names of specific people, places, and things
  • Common nouns: Use lowercase for general categories
  • Consistency: Apply rules uniformly throughout your document
  • Down style: Canadian government writing favours minimal capitals
Key Principle

Capitalize words that name something unique and specific. Use lowercase for words that describe general categories or concepts.

4.02 Initial Words

Always capitalize the first word of:

Sentences and Fragments

  • The meeting begins at 2:00 p.m.
  • Yes. No. Really?
  • Approved.

Direct Quotations

Example 4.02a

The minister said, "This policy will benefit all Canadians."

BUT: The minister said the policy would "benefit all Canadians."

(No capital when quotation is integrated)

Lines of Poetry

Capitalize the first word of each line (unless the poet doesn't):

Example 4.02b

In Flanders fields the poppies blow
Between the crosses, row on row

Items in Vertical Lists

Capitalize the first word if items are complete sentences:

  • The project has three phases.
  • Each phase takes six months.
  • Budget approval is required.

Use lowercase if items complete the introductory statement:

The project requires:

  • budget approval
  • staff allocation
  • timeline confirmation

After a Colon

  • Complete sentence: Capitalize ‚Üí The rule is simple: Always proofread your work.
  • Fragment or list: Lowercase ‚Üí Buy these items: paper, pens, and folders.
  • Multiple sentences: Capitalize ‚Üí Remember: Check your facts. Cite your sources.

4.05 Governments and Government Bodies

Government capitalization follows specific patterns:

Full Formal Names (Capitalize)

  • Government of Canada
  • Province of Ontario
  • City of Toronto
  • Parliament of Canada
  • House of Commons
  • Supreme Court of Canada

Shortened Forms

Capitalize when specific:

  • the Government (meaning Government of Canada)
  • the Province (in Ontario context)
  • the House (meaning House of Commons)
  • the Court (meaning a specific court)

Lowercase when general:

  • federal government
  • provincial governments
  • the government's policy
  • many governments worldwide

Departments and Agencies

Example 4.05

Capitalize: Environment and Climate Change Canada

Lowercase: the department, the ministry


Capitalize: Canada Revenue Agency

Lowercase: the agency, federal agencies

Special Rules

  • Crown: Capitalize when referring to the state
  • Parliament: Capitalize when referring to a specific parliament
  • Cabinet: Capitalize when referring to a specific cabinet
  • Acts and Bills: Income Tax Act, Bill C-45

Don't capitalize "government" in "the federal government" or "the provincial government" unless it starts a sentence.

4.08 Titles of Office or Rank

Capitalization of titles depends on how they're used:

Capitalize Titles When:

1. Directly before a name

Example 4.08a

Prime Minister Trudeau

Professor Smith

Chief Justice Wagner

Dr. Patel

2. In direct address

Example 4.08b

"Thank you, Prime Minister."

"Yes, Professor, I understand."

"Your Honour, I object."

3. Referring to a specific officeholder

Example 4.08c

The Prime Minister will address Parliament. (current PM)

The President of the company resigned. (specific person)

Use Lowercase When:

1. The title follows the name

Example 4.08d

Justin Trudeau, prime minister of Canada

Mary Smith, professor of history

John Brown, vice-president of sales

2. Used as a common noun

Example 4.08e

All prime ministers face challenges.

The professors met yesterday.

Many presidents attended.

3. Modified by a possessive

Example 4.08f

our prime minister

their professor

the company's president

Special Cases

  • Former titles: former prime minister Chr√©tien
  • Acting/Deputy: acting director Smith
  • Multiple titles: Finance Minister and Deputy Prime Minister Jones
Note: Some organizations capitalize all titles for internal documents. Follow your organization's style guide for internal communications.

4.21 Geographical Terms

Geographical names follow specific capitalization patterns:

Always Capitalize

  • Specific places: Lake Ontario, Rocky Mountains, Prairies
  • Regions: Western Canada, Atlantic Canada, the North
  • Political divisions: Ontario, British Columbia, Yukon
  • Nicknames: the Maritimes, the Rock (Newfoundland)

Generic Terms

Capitalize when part of proper name:

  • Ottawa River
  • Pacific Ocean
  • Mount Logan
  • Lake Superior
  • Bay of Fundy

Lowercase when standing alone:

  • the river
  • the ocean
  • the mountain
  • the lake
  • the bay

Directions and Regions

Regions (Capitalize)

the West, the East Coast, Northern Ontario, the Far North

Directions (Lowercase)

travelling west, eastern shores, northern route, south of Toronto

Special Canadian Terms

  • the North: Canada's territorial and arctic regions
  • Central Canada: Ontario and Quebec
  • Atlantic Canada: the four eastern provinces
  • the Prairies: Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta
  • the Coast: usually means BC's Pacific coast

Buildings and Structures

  • Parliament Hill
  • CN Tower
  • Ch√¢teau Laurier
  • Rogers Centre
  • the Hill (informal for Parliament Hill)

4.29 Publications and Works of Art

Titles of creative works follow specific capitalization rules:

Capitalize in Titles

  • First and last words
  • All nouns, pronouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs
  • Subordinating conjunctions (because, although, since, unless)

Lowercase in Titles

  • Articles (a, an, the) unless first or last
  • Coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet)
  • Prepositions of fewer than four letters (at, by, for, in, of, on, to)
  • The "to" in infinitives

Types of Works

Italicize (Major Works)

  • Books: Life of Pi
  • Newspapers: The Toronto Star
  • Magazines: Maclean's
  • Movies: The Sweet Hereafter
  • TV series: Schitt's Creek

Quotation Marks (Shorter Works)

  • Articles: "Budget Highlights"
  • Short stories: "The Bear Came Over the Mountain"
  • Poems: "In Flanders Fields"
  • Songs: "O Canada"
  • TV episodes: "The One with the Prom Video"

Special Cases

  • The: Capitalize when part of official title: The Globe and Mail
  • Subtitles: Use colon or dash: CanLit: A Short History
  • Series: Capitalize series names: the Harry Potter series
