8.01 Basic Quotation Rules

üí¨ Accurate Attribution

Quotation marks serve to distinguish direct speech, borrowed text, and special uses from your own writing.

When to Use Quotation Marks

üì¢ Direct Quotations

Exact words spoken or written by someone else

The minister said, "We will review the policy."

üìù Cited Text

Direct excerpts from written sources

According to the report, "Climate change affects all regions."

üìë Titles of Short Works

Articles, poems, songs, short stories

Read the article "Economic Trends in Canada."

üéØ Special Meanings

Words used ironically or in a special sense

The "solution" created more problems.

Direct vs. Indirect Quotations

‚úÖ Direct Quotation

Uses the exact words spoken or written:

She said, "I will attend the meeting."
The document states, "All employees must complete training."

‚ùå Indirect Quotation (No Quotes)

Paraphrases or reports what was said:

She said that she would attend the meeting.
The document states that all employees must complete training.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå Mixed Direct/Indirect

She said that "I will be there."
She said, "I will be there." OR She said that she would be there.

‚ùå Unnecessary Quotes

The "meeting" was held yesterday.
The meeting was held yesterday.

‚ùå Missing Attribution

"The economy is improving."
The minister said, "The economy is improving."

Quotation Accuracy

üéØ Exact Reproduction

Direct quotations must reproduce the original text exactly, including any errors.

üìù Indicating Changes

Use square brackets [ ] for clarifications and ellipsis points (...) for omissions.

‚öñÔ∏è Fair Use

Quote only what's necessary. Extensive quotation may require permission.

üí° Attribution Tip

Always make it clear who said or wrote the quoted material. Floating quotations without attribution confuse readers.

8.02 Punctuation with Quotation Marks

üìç Placement Precision

The placement of punctuation marks with quotation marks follows specific Canadian conventions.

Canadian/British Style Rules

‚úÖ Inside Quotation Marks

Periods and Commas

Always go inside the closing quotation marks:

She said, "I'll be there."
"Hello," he replied.
The article "Policy Changes," published last month, explains the new rules.
Question Marks and Exclamation Marks

Go inside when they're part of the quotation:

She asked, "Will you attend?"
He shouted, "Stop immediately!"

‚ùå Outside Quotation Marks

Semicolons and Colons

Always go outside the closing quotation marks:

She said "yes"; however, she seemed uncertain.
Remember the rule about "early birds": they get the worm.
Question Marks and Exclamation Marks

Go outside when they apply to the whole sentence:

Did she say "tomorrow"?
I can't believe he said "maybe"!

Decision Framework for Question/Exclamation Marks

ü§î Ask Yourself:

Is the entire quoted sentence a question/exclamation? ‚Üí Inside: "Are you coming?"
Is your whole sentence a question about the quote? ‚Üí Outside: Did he say "yes"?
Are both the quote and sentence questions? ‚Üí Inside only: Did she ask "Where are you going?"

Complex Punctuation Examples

Multiple Sentences

He said, "The meeting is tomorrow. Please bring your reports."

Interrupted Quotations

"The project," she explained, "will take six months."

Quotations with Citations

"Climate change is accelerating," according to the UN report.

Common Errors and Corrections

Comma Placement

‚ùå She said "hello", and smiled. ‚úÖ She said "hello," and smiled.

Question Mark Placement

‚ùå She asked "Are you ready"? ‚úÖ She asked, "Are you ready?"

Semicolon Placement

‚ùå He said "yes;" I was surprised. ‚úÖ He said "yes"; I was surprised.

‚ö†Ô∏è American vs. Canadian Style

American style puts periods and commas inside quotation marks regardless of logic. Canadian style follows British conventions, which can be more logical but requires careful attention to context.

8.03 Block Quotations

üìÑ Long Quotation Formatting

Block quotations provide a clear, readable format for longer quoted passages.

When to Use Block Format

Use Block Format When:

  • 40+ words in the quotation
  • 4+ lines of typed text
  • Multiple sentences from the same source
  • Dialogue or conversation excerpts
  • Poetry or verse quotations

Example Qualifying Quote:

According to the policy document:
The new environmental regulations will take effect on January 1, 2025. All manufacturing facilities must comply with the updated emission standards within six months of the effective date. Failure to meet these requirements may result in significant penalties and potential facility closure.

Block Quotation Formatting

üìè Indentation

  • Indent the entire block 1 inch (2.5 cm) from the left margin
  • Maintain normal right margin
  • Do not indent the first line further unless it's a paragraph beginning

üìù No Quotation Marks

  • The indentation signals that it's a quotation
  • Only use quotes if they appear in the original text
  • This avoids cluttered appearance

üìê Spacing

  • Double-space before and after the block
  • Single-space within the block quotation
  • Maintain consistent spacing with your document

Block Quotation Examples

Policy Document

The annual report explains the institution's commitment:
Our organization remains dedicated to transparency and accountability in all operations. We will continue to publish quarterly reports, maintain open communication with stakeholders, and ensure that all decisions align with our core values of integrity, excellence, and service to the Canadian public.
This commitment represents a significant shift in organizational culture.

Academic Source

Smith (2023) argues for a comprehensive approach:
Climate change mitigation requires coordinated action across multiple sectors. Government policy, corporate responsibility, and individual behavior change must work in concert to achieve meaningful reduction in greenhouse gas emissions. Without this multi-level approach, efforts will remain fragmented and insufficient to address the scale of the challenge.
This perspective has gained support among environmental policy experts.

Poetry and Verse

üé≠ Special Considerations for Poetry

  • Preserve original line breaks and spacing
  • Center short poems on the page
  • Maintain original capitalization and punctuation
  • Use block format even for short poems to preserve structure

Poetry Example:

The opening stanza of Frost's famous poem sets the contemplative tone:
Two roads diverged in a yellow wood,
And sorry I could not travel both
And be one traveler, long I stood
And looked down one as far as I could
To where it bent in the undergrowth;

Citation with Block Quotes

üìö Citation Placement

  • Place citation after the closing punctuation
  • Do not add a period after the citation
  • Use parenthetical format when appropriate
The research methodology must account for variables that could influence the outcome. Proper controls and randomization are essential for valid results.
(Johnson & Smith, 2023, p. 45)

üí° Readability Tip

Introduce block quotations with a complete sentence ending in a colon. This helps readers understand the context and purpose of the quoted material.

8.04 Quotations Within Quotations

ü™Ü Nested Quotations

When quotations contain other quotations, specific punctuation conventions maintain clarity.

Single vs. Double Quotation Marks

üìè First Level: Double Quotes

Use double quotation marks for the main quotation:

She said, "The meeting starts at 2 p.m."

üìê Second Level: Single Quotes

Use single quotation marks for quotes within quotes:

She said, "Remember the motto: 'Excellence in everything we do.'"

üìè Third Level: Double Quotes Again

Alternate back to double quotes if needed:

He reported, "She told me, 'The sign said "Authorized Personnel Only" at the entrance.'"

Common Scenarios

üó£Ô∏è Reported Speech

When someone quotes what another person said:

The manager explained, "The client said, 'We need this completed by Friday.'"
The manager's words get double quotes; the client's words get single quotes.

üìñ Quoting Written Material

When a quotation includes a title or another quoted phrase:

The review stated, "The article 'Climate Solutions' provides valuable insights."
The review gets double quotes; the article title gets single quotes.

üè¢ Institutional References

When quoting policies or mottos within speech:

She reminded us, "Our policy clearly states 'All visitors must sign in at reception.'"
Her speech gets double quotes; the policy text gets single quotes.

Punctuation with Nested Quotes

üî§ Multiple End Marks

When both the inner and outer quotations end simultaneously:

Both end with periods: He said, "She told me, 'The deadline is tomorrow.'"
Inner quote is a question: She asked, "Did he say, 'Where is the meeting?'"
Outer quote is a question: Did she say, "The answer is 'yes'"?

British vs. American Conventions

üá®üá¶ Canadian/British Style

Single quotes for main quotations, double for nested:

She said, 'Remember our motto: "Excellence first."'

Less common in Canadian business writing

üá∫üá∏ American Style (Recommended for Canada)

Double quotes for main quotations, single for nested:

She said, "Remember our motto: 'Excellence first.'"

Standard in Canadian business and academic writing

Complex Nested Examples

Business Communication

The CEO announced, "Our new slogan will be 'Innovation Today, Success Tomorrow,' replacing the old motto 'Quality Above All.'"
  • CEO's announcement: double quotes
  • New slogan: single quotes
  • Old motto: single quotes

Academic Writing

According to Smith (2023), "The concept of 'sustainable development' differs significantly from earlier notions of 'environmental protection.'"
  • Smith's statement: double quotes
  • Technical terms: single quotes

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid Over-Nesting

More than two levels of quotation can become confusing. Consider paraphrasing or restructuring sentences to avoid excessive nesting. If you must go to a third level, be extra careful with punctuation placement.

8.05 Titles and Special Uses

üè∑Ô∏è Distinguishing Different Works

Quotation marks help distinguish between major works (which get italics) and shorter works or components.

Works That Get Quotation Marks

üì∞ Articles and Essays

  • "Climate Policy in Canada" (journal article)
  • "Economic Trends" (magazine article)
  • "Opinion: Education Reform" (newspaper editorial)
  • "The Future of Work" (blog post)

üìñ Short Literary Works

  • "The Road Not Taken" (poem)
  • "The Lottery" (short story)
  • "The Gift of the Magi" (short story)
  • "Stopping by Woods" (poem)

üéµ Songs and Short Compositions

  • "O Canada" (national anthem)
  • "Imagine" (song)
  • "F√ºr Elise" (short musical piece)
  • "Yesterday" (Beatles song)

üì∫ Episodes and Segments

  • "The One with the Prom Video" (TV episode)
  • "Chapter 3: The New Beginning" (book chapter)
  • "Market Update" (radio segment)
  • "Introduction" (section of a report)

Italics vs. Quotation Marks Decision Guide

ü§î Ask: Is it a complete, independent work?

YES ‚Üí Use Italics
  • Romeo and Juliet (complete play)
  • The Globe and Mail (newspaper)
  • Inception (movie)
NO ‚Üí Use "Quotation Marks"
  • "Act 2, Scene 1" (part of play)
  • "Editorial: Climate Action" (newspaper article)
  • "Behind the Scenes" (movie featurette)

Examples in Context

üìö Academic Writing

In her article "Educational Innovation in Canada," published in the journal Canadian Education Quarterly, Dr. Smith examines...
Article title gets quotes; journal name gets italics

üì∫ Media References

The episode "The Final Countdown" from the series Lost revealed crucial plot details.
Episode title gets quotes; series name gets italics

üé≠ Performance Arts

She performed "Tomorrow" from the musical Annie at the talent show.
Song title gets quotes; musical name gets italics

Special Cases

üåê Digital Content

Website/Blog Names: The Huffington Post (italics)

Individual Posts: "Five Tips for Success" (quotes)

Podcast Series: Serial (italics)

Podcast Episodes: "Episode 12: The Evidence" (quotes)

üìÑ Government Documents

Major Reports: Annual Report 2023 (italics)

Sections: "Executive Summary" (quotes)

Policy Papers: Climate Action Framework (italics)

Chapters: "Implementation Strategy" (quotes)

üèõÔ∏è Artistic Works

Exhibitions: Canadian Landscapes (italics)

Individual Pieces: "Sunset Over Lake Ontario" (quotes)

Concert Series: Symphony Season 2024 (italics)

Individual Performances: "Beethoven's 9th Symphony" (quotes)

üí° Consistency Rule

Be consistent throughout your document. If you put one article title in quotation marks, do the same for all article titles. Consistent formatting helps readers understand your system.

8.06 Words in Special Senses

üé≠ Beyond Literal Meaning

Quotation marks can indicate irony, special terminology, or non-standard usage of words.

Legitimate Uses of "Scare Quotes"

üéØ Technical Terms

When introducing specialized terminology to a general audience:

In computing, "bandwidth" refers to data transmission capacity.
The legal concept of "due process" protects individual rights.

üåç Translated Terms

When providing English equivalents of foreign concepts:

The German concept of "schadenfreude" means taking pleasure in others' misfortune.
In Japan, "nemawashi" refers to behind-the-scenes consensus building.

üó£Ô∏è Slang and Colloquialisms

When using informal language in formal writing:

The project was "dead in the water" after budget cuts.
Students often "cram" for exams instead of studying regularly.

Ironic and Critical Usage

üòè Ironic Emphasis

To indicate skepticism or irony about a claim:

The "solution" actually created more problems.
The meeting was very "productive" ‚Äì nothing was decided.

ü§î Questionable Claims

To distance yourself from dubious statements:

The company claimed the layoffs were "necessary for growth."
The politician promised "real change" in his campaign.

What NOT to Put in Quotation Marks

‚ùå Simple Emphasis

Don't use quotation marks just to emphasize words:

This is "very" important. This is very important. OR This is crucial.

‚ùå Unnecessary Qualification

Don't use quotes around perfectly normal words:

We held a "meeting" yesterday. We held a meeting yesterday.

‚ùå Apologetic Quotes

Don't use quotes to apologize for word choice:

The presentation was "awesome." The presentation was excellent.

Professional Context Guidelines

üè¢ Business Writing

  • Use sparingly ‚Äì overuse suggests insincerity
  • Avoid ironic quotes in formal reports
  • Acceptable for introducing new terminology
  • Can be used to distance from others' claims

üì∞ Journalism

  • Useful for reporting controversial statements
  • Help maintain objectivity when presenting disputed claims
  • Should not reflect reporter's personal opinion
  • Must be balanced and fair

üéì Academic Writing

  • Appropriate for defining terms
  • Useful for discussing language itself
  • Avoid excessive ironic usage
  • Be clear about your intent

Alternative Methods for Special Emphasis

üìù Explicit Statement

The "solution" created problems. The proposed solution actually created more problems.

üó£Ô∏è Attribution

The project was "successful." Management described the project as successful.

üí≠ Qualifying Language

The "expert" gave advice. The self-proclaimed expert gave advice.
