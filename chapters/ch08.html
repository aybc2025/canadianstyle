Quotations and Quotation Marks

Master the proper use of quotations and quotation marks in Canadian writing

8.01 Basic Rules

üí¨ Accurate Attribution

Quotation marks preserve the exact words of speakers and writers while clearly distinguishing them from your own text.

When to Use Quotation Marks

  • Direct speech: Exact words spoken or written
  • Short titles: Articles, poems, songs, chapters
  • Technical terms: Words used in special senses
  • Irony or emphasis: Words used unusually

Basic Quotation Examples

Direct speech: The minister said, "We will review the policy."

Short title: Read "Climate Action" in today's paper.

Special sense: The "solution" created more problems.

Canadian vs. American Style

  • Canadian: Double quotes for main quotations
  • Punctuation: Outside quotes unless part of quoted material
  • Nested quotes: Single quotes inside double quotes

8.02 Punctuation with Quotes

The placement of punctuation with quotation marks follows specific Canadian conventions.

Canadian Punctuation Rules

  • Inside quotes: Only punctuation that's part of the quoted material
  • Outside quotes: Punctuation that belongs to your sentence
  • Question marks: Inside if the quote is a question
  • Periods and commas: Usually outside in Canadian style

Punctuation Placement

‚úì Canadian Style (Correct)
  • She asked, "Are you ready"?
  • He said "yes", then left.
  • Did she say "maybe"?
  • The sign read "Danger".
‚úì When Quote Has Its Own Punctuation
  • "Are you coming?" she asked.
  • "Help!" he shouted.
  • "Wait," she said, "I'm not ready."

‚ö†Ô∏è American vs Canadian

American style puts periods and commas inside quotes regardless of meaning. Canadian style is more logical - punctuation goes where it belongs.

Style Comparison

Did he say "yes"? Did he say "yes"?

8.03 Block Quotations

Long quotations are formatted as separate blocks of text without quotation marks.

Block Quote Guidelines

  • Length: Usually 40+ words or 4+ lines
  • Indentation: Indent from left margin
  • No quotes: Don't use quotation marks
  • Introduction: Introduce with complete sentence and colon

Block Quotation Format

Introduction: The Prime Minister's speech emphasized unity:

Canada's strength has always come from our diversity and our ability to work together. We face challenges that require not just government action, but the collective effort of all Canadians. In the coming years, we must focus on building bridges, not walls, between communities.

Follow-up text continues here...

Block Quote Formatting

  • Font: Same as body text (or slightly smaller)
  • Spacing: Usually single-spaced within quote
  • Margins: Extra space above and below
  • Attribution: Can follow the quote

üí° When to Use Block Format

Use block format for emphasis and readability with longer quotes. It helps readers distinguish quoted material from your analysis.

8.04 Nested Quotations

When quotes contain other quotes, use a specific hierarchy of quotation marks.

Quote-within-Quote Rules

  • Outer quote: Double quotation marks
  • Inner quote: Single quotation marks
  • Third level: Back to double quotes (rare)
  • Punctuation: Follows same rules as regular quotes

Nested Quotation Examples

Basic nested quote:

The teacher said, "Remember that Shakespeare wrote, 'To be or not to be, that is the question.'"

With interruption:

She explained, "The policy states, 'All employees must', and I quote directly, 'report by 5 p.m.'"

Question within statement:

He asked, "Did she really say 'I quit'?"

8.05 Titles & Special Uses

Quotation marks are used for certain types of titles and words with special meanings.

Titles Using Quotation Marks

  • Articles: "Climate Change and Policy"
  • Short stories: "The Gift of the Magi"
  • Poems: "In Flanders Fields"
  • Songs: "O Canada"
  • Episodes: "The Trouble with Tribbles"

Special Word Uses

  • Irony: His "brilliant" idea failed completely
  • Technical terms: The "cloud" refers to online storage
  • Unusual usage: She's very "extra" today
  • Slang: That's totally "lit"

Title and Special Use Examples

  • The word "literally" is often misused. (word as word)

Don't use quotation marks for emphasis alone. Reserve them for genuine quotations, titles, and genuinely special word uses.

8.06 Indirect Speech

Indirect speech reports what someone said without using their exact words. It doesn't require quotation marks.

Direct vs. Indirect Speech

  • Direct: Uses exact words + quotation marks
  • Indirect: Paraphrases meaning + no quotation marks
  • Verb changes: Tense often shifts in indirect speech
  • Pronoun changes: Perspective adjusts for reporter

Direct vs. Indirect Examples

Direct Speech

The minister said, "We will implement the policy next month."

Indirect Speech

The minister said that they would implement the policy the following month.

Direct Question

She asked, "Are you ready to proceed?"

Indirect Question

She asked whether we were ready to proceed.

Direct Command

The supervisor said, "Please submit your reports by Friday."

Indirect Command

The supervisor asked us to submit our reports by Friday.

When to Use Each

  • Direct: For impact, authority, exact wording matters
  • Indirect: For summary, flow, general meaning
  • Legal contexts: Often require direct quotes
  • News writing: Mix both for variety and accuracy

Common Transformations

Direct Speech Indirect Speech
"I am ready." She said she was ready.
"We will go tomorrow." They said they would go the next day.
"Can you help me?" He asked if I could help him.
"Don't be late!" She told us not to be late.
