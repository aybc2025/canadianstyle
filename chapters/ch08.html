8.01 Introduction

Quotations require careful attention to format, punctuation, and accuracy. Proper handling of quoted material shows respect for original sources and helps readers distinguish between your words and those of others.

Two Main Formats

  • Run-in quotations: Integrated into your paragraph, enclosed in quotation marks
  • Block quotations: Set apart from your text, usually indented, no quotation marks

Key Principles

  • Accuracy: Quote exactly as written in the original
  • Attribution: Always identify the source
  • Context: Ensure quotations support your point
  • Integration: Blend quotations smoothly into your writing

8.02 Run-in Format

Short Quotations Within Text

Use run-in format for brief quotations (usually under 50 words) that flow within your paragraph:

Basic Run-in Quotations

‚úì Correct format:

  • The minister stated, "We will review all options before making a decision."
  • According to the report, "Costs have increased by 15 percent since 2020."
  • The policy emphasizes "collaborative approaches to problem-solving."

Run-in Format Rules

  • Enclose the quotation in double quotation marks
  • Integrate the quotation grammatically into your sentence
  • Use appropriate punctuation before the quotation (comma, colon, or none)
  • Place periods and commas inside the quotation marks

Integration Techniques

‚úì Smooth integration:

  • The committee described the proposal as "innovative and cost-effective."
  • Smith argues that "traditional methods are no longer sufficient."
  • The report concludes: "Immediate action is required."

8.03 Punctuation and Grammar in Run-in Quotations

Punctuation Rules

Follow specific rules for punctuation with quotation marks:

Inside Quotation Marks

  • Periods: Always inside ("The meeting ended.")
  • Commas: Always inside ("Yes," she replied.)
  • Question marks: Inside if part of quotation ("Are you ready?")
  • Exclamation marks: Inside if part of quotation ("Stop!" he shouted.)

Outside Quotation Marks

  • Question marks: Outside if not part of quotation
  • Exclamation marks: Outside if not part of quotation
  • Semicolons: Always outside
  • Colons: Always outside

Punctuation Examples

‚úì Correct punctuation:

  • She asked, "What time is the meeting?"
  • Did she say "three o'clock"?
  • The report states, "Funding will increase"; however, details are unclear.
  • He emphasized these points: "efficiency," "effectiveness," and "economy."

8.04 Indirect (Reported) Speech

Paraphrasing vs. Direct Quotation

Use indirect speech to report what someone said without using their exact words:

Direct vs. Indirect Speech

‚úì Direct quotation:

  • The minister said, "We will increase funding by 10 percent."
  • She announced, "The program will begin next month."

‚úì Indirect speech (no quotation marks):

  • The minister said that funding would increase by 10 percent.
  • She announced that the program would begin next month.

When to Use Indirect Speech

  • When the exact words are less important than the meaning
  • To vary your writing style and avoid too many quotations
  • When summarizing lengthy statements
  • To maintain consistent verb tenses in your narrative

üí° Tense Changes

When converting to indirect speech, often change present tense to past: "I am going" becomes "he said he was going."

8.05 Paragraphing: Run-in Format

Multiple Paragraphs in Quotations

When quoting multiple paragraphs in run-in format, use special punctuation:

Multi-paragraph Rules

  • Open each paragraph with quotation marks
  • Close quotation marks only at the very end
  • Do not close quotation marks at the end of intermediate paragraphs

Multi-paragraph Quotation

‚úì Correct format:

The report states: "The program has achieved significant success in its first year of operation. Participation rates exceeded expectations by 25 percent.

"However, several challenges remain. Funding constraints limit our ability to expand services to rural communities.

"We recommend increased federal support to ensure program sustainability."

üí° Consider Block Format

For quotations longer than 50 words or multiple paragraphs, consider using block format instead for better readability.

8.06 Block Format

Long Quotations

Use block format for quotations of 50 words or more, or when you want to emphasize the quoted material:

Block Format Rules

  • Indent the entire quotation (usually 1 cm or 0.5 inches)
  • Do not use quotation marks
  • Single-space the quotation (even if your text is double-spaced)
  • Leave extra space before and after the block

Block Quotation Example

‚úì Proper block format:

The Treasury Board policy clearly states:

Government communications must be clear, objective, and factual. They should provide information that citizens need to understand government policies, programs, and services. All communications should be accessible to diverse audiences and available in both official languages where required by law.

This policy forms the foundation of our communication strategy.

üí° When to Use Block Format

  • Quotations longer than 50 words
  • Multiple sentences that form a complete thought
  • When you want to draw attention to the quoted material
  • Poetry or structured text that benefits from preservation of original formatting

8.07 Paragraphing: Block Format

Multiple Paragraphs in Block Quotations

When using block format for multi-paragraph quotations, maintain the original paragraph structure:

Block Paragraph Rules

  • Preserve the original paragraph breaks
  • Indent the first line of each paragraph within the block
  • Maintain consistent spacing between paragraphs
  • Do not use quotation marks anywhere in the block

Multi-paragraph Block Example

‚úì Correct multi-paragraph block:

The annual report concludes:

The department exceeded most of its performance targets this fiscal year. Employee satisfaction increased by 12 percent, and client service ratings reached an all-time high.

However, budget constraints continue to pose challenges. Several planned initiatives had to be delayed, and staffing levels remain below optimal.

Looking forward, we will focus on efficiency improvements and strategic partnerships to maximize our impact with available resources.

These findings will inform our planning for the upcoming year.

8.08 Quotations Within Quotations

Handling Quotes Within Quotes

When your quotation contains another quotation, use single quotation marks for the inner quotation:

Nested Quotation Rules

  • Use double quotation marks for the main quotation
  • Use single quotation marks for quotations within the main quotation
  • In block quotations, use double marks for any internal quotations
  • Punctuation goes with the quotation it ends

Nested Quotation Examples

‚úì Run-in format with nested quotes:

  • The minister explained, "When I said 'immediate action,' I meant within 30 days."
  • According to the witness, "The suspect shouted, 'I didn't do it!' before running away."
  • She noted, "The policy states that 'all employees must complete training' by year-end."

Block Format with Internal Quotes

‚úì Block quotation containing quotes:

The committee report stated:

Our interviews revealed significant concerns. One employee told us, "The new system is confusing and time-consuming." Another said, "We need better training and support." These comments reflect a broader pattern of implementation challenges.

8.09 Omissions

Using Ellipsis Points

Use ellipsis points (...) to indicate omitted material from quotations:

Ellipsis Rules

  • Use three dots (...) for omissions within sentences
  • Use four dots (....) when omitting material at the end of a sentence
  • Use a full line of dots when omitting entire paragraphs
  • Space before and after ellipsis points

Ellipsis Examples

‚úì Original text:

"The program has been successful in urban areas, though it faces challenges in rural communities due to infrastructure limitations."

‚úì With omissions:

  • "The program has been successful ... though it faces challenges in rural communities."
  • "The program has been successful in urban areas...." (omitting end)
  • "... it faces challenges in rural communities due to infrastructure limitations." (omitting beginning)

‚ö†Ô∏è Caution with Omissions

Never omit material in a way that changes the meaning of the original. Always preserve the author's intent and context.

üí° When Not to Use Ellipsis

  • At the beginning of quotations (unless you're starting mid-sentence)
  • At the end of quotations that naturally end your sentence
  • When integrating short phrases that are obviously incomplete

8.10 Insertions, Alterations and Parentheses

Adding Clarification to Quotations

Use square brackets to add clarifying information or indicate changes to quotations:

Square Bracket Uses

  • Clarification: [Minister of Finance] when the original just says "he"
  • Grammar changes: "[T]he program" when changing capitalization
  • Errors in original: "The program [sic] was successful"
  • Explanatory notes: "We increased funding [by 10%] last year"

Insertion Examples

‚úì Original: "He announced the new policy yesterday."

‚úì With clarification: "He [the Prime Minister] announced the new policy yesterday."

‚úì Grammar change: The report notes that "[t]he new policy will take effect immediately."

‚úì With explanation: "The increase [from $50 to $75 million] was substantial."

Using [sic]

  • Indicates an error or unusual usage in the original
  • Shows you're quoting accurately, not making a mistake
  • Use sparingly and only when necessary for clarity
  • Italicize: [sic]

[sic] Examples

‚úì Indicating original errors:

  • The memo stated, "We will recieve [sic] the report next week."
  • He wrote, "Their [sic] going to implement the policy soon."

8.11 Reference to Words as Such

Discussing Words and Terms

When referring to words as words rather than their meaning, use quotation marks or italics:

Words as Words

  • Use quotation marks: The word "government" appears frequently
  • Or use italics: The word government appears frequently
  • Be consistent within your document
  • Government writing typically prefers quotation marks

Word Reference Examples

‚úì Using quotation marks:

  • The term "stakeholder" has multiple definitions.
  • Replace "shall" with "must" throughout the document.
  • The word "policy" is used 23 times in Chapter 3.

‚úì Using italics:

  • The term stakeholder has multiple definitions.
  • Replace shall with must throughout the document.
  • The word policy is used 23 times in Chapter 3.

Letters and Numbers

  • Use quotation marks for letters: The letter "s" is silent
  • Use quotation marks for numbers: Add a "1" before the area code
  • This prevents confusion with mathematical expressions

8.12 Words Used in an Ironic or Special Sense; Slang and Technical Terms

Quotation Marks for Special Usage

Use quotation marks to indicate words used in a special, ironic, or non-standard way:

When to Use Quotation Marks

  • Irony or skepticism: Their "solution" created more problems
  • Specialized terms: The "firewall" protects our network
  • Informal language: The proposal was "dead on arrival"
  • Coined terms: We need to "right-size" the organization

Special Sense Examples

‚úì Appropriate special usage:

  • The so-called "efficiency" measures actually increased costs.
  • We need to "sunset" this outdated program.
  • The system experienced a "soft" launch with limited users.
  • Employees were asked to "think outside the box."

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Overuse

Avoid excessive use of quotation marks for emphasis or to distance yourself from your own words. This can make your writing appear uncertain or sarcastic.

Technical Terms

  • Use quotation marks when first introducing technical terms
  • After definition, use the term without quotation marks
  • Consider using italics for scientific or foreign technical terms

Technical Term Introduction

‚úì First use: The system uses "cookies" to store user preferences.

‚úì Subsequent use: These cookies help improve the user experience.

8.13 Titles

Quotation Marks vs. Italics for Titles

Use quotation marks for shorter works; use italics for longer, major works:

Use Quotation Marks For

  • Articles: "The Future of Digital Government"
  • Chapters: "Introduction to Policy Development"
  • Short stories: "The Last Spike"
  • Poems: "The Cremation of Sam McGee"
  • Songs: "The Log Driver's Waltz"
  • Episodes: "The Friendly Giant" (TV episode)

Use Italics For

  • Books: Two Solitudes
  • Newspapers: The Globe and Mail
  • Magazines: Saturday Night
  • Films: The Sweet Hereafter
  • TV series: Anne of Green Gables
  • Reports: Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples

Title Examples in Context

‚úì Combining quotation marks and italics:

  • The article "Digital Transformation" appeared in Canadian Government Executive.
  • Chapter 5, "Implementation Strategies," discusses best practices from Managing Change in Government.
  • The film The Corporation featured the segment "Government Regulation."

üí° Government Documents

For government reports and policies, use italics for major documents and quotation marks for sections or chapters within them.

8.15 Abuse of Quotation Marks

Common Misuses to Avoid

Avoid unnecessary or incorrect use of quotation marks that can confuse readers or undermine your credibility:

Don't Use Quotation Marks For

  • Emphasis: Use bold or italics instead
  • Common words: Don't put normal words in quotes
  • Your own opinions: Own your statements
  • Clich√©s: Either use them confidently or find better expressions

Common Mistakes

‚úó Unnecessary quotation marks:

  • We offer "quality" service. (implies doubt about quality)
  • Please "sign" the contract. (unnecessary around normal word)
  • The meeting is "important." (use bold or italics for emphasis)
  • We need to "touch base" regularly. (own the clich√© or find better words)

‚úì Better alternatives:

  • We offer quality service. (confident statement)
  • Please sign the contract. (clear instruction)
  • The meeting is important. (proper emphasis)
  • We need to communicate regularly. (clearer language)

‚ö†Ô∏è Quotation Marks Suggest Skepticism

Unnecessary quotation marks often suggest irony, skepticism, or that you don't really mean what you're saying. This undermines your credibility.

Signs of Quotation Mark Abuse

  • Multiple sets of quotation marks in signs or advertisements
  • Quotation marks around company slogans or mottos
  • Using quotes to distance yourself from common terms
  • Quotation marks for emphasis instead of proper formatting

üí° Professional Writing

In government writing, be particularly careful about quotation mark use. Clear, confident language without unnecessary punctuation conveys authority and competence.
