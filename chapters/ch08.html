Quotations and Quotation Marks

Learn how to properly quote sources and use quotation marks

8.01 Introduction

Quotations bring other voices into your writing. This chapter covers how to format quotations and use quotation marks correctly in Canadian English.

Key Principles

  • Accuracy: Quote sources exactly, including errors
  • Context: Provide enough context for understanding
  • Attribution: Always identify your sources
  • Integration: Blend quotations smoothly into your text

Canadian vs. American Style

Canadian/British

  • Logical punctuation placement
  • Single quotes for quotes within quotes
  • Punctuation outside quotes unless part of quotation

American

  • Periods and commas always inside
  • Double quotes for quotes within quotes
  • Different punctuation rules
Canadian Style

Did she say "I'll be there"?

He called it "remarkable".

8.02 Run-in Format

Short quotations (generally under 40 words or 4 lines) are run into the text:

Direct Quotations

Example 8.02a

The minister stated, "This policy will benefit all Canadians in the coming year."

According to the report, "greenhouse gas emissions decreased by 15% last year."

Partial Quotations

Example 8.02b

The report describes the situation as "critical but not hopeless."

She said the results were "better than expected" given the circumstances.

Integration Techniques

  • Complete sentence: Use a comma or colon before the quotation
  • Partial integration: No punctuation if quotation flows naturally
  • That introduction: No comma after "that"
Example 8.02c

The CEO announced: "We will expand into three new markets."

The CEO said that "expansion is our top priority."

The CEO announced that the company would "expand into three new markets."

Ellipsis for Omissions

Use three spaced dots (...) to show omitted material:

Example 8.02d

Original: "The committee, after extensive deliberation and consultation, reached a decision."

Quoted: "The committee ... reached a decision."

Square Brackets for Changes

Use [square brackets] for editorial changes or clarifications:

Example 8.02e

"[The Prime Minister] will address the nation tonight."

"The temperature reached 30[Â°C] yesterday."

"This policy is essential to our success [emphasis added]."

8.03 Block Format

Long quotations (over 40 words or 4 lines) are set off as block quotations:

Block Quotation Format

  • Start on a new line
  • Indent from left margin (usually 1.25 cm or 0.5")
  • Single-space (if document is double-spaced)
  • No quotation marks
  • Citation follows final punctuation

The minister explained the new policy in detail:

This initiative represents a fundamental shift in how we approach environmental protection. By investing in renewable energy sources and implementing stricter emissions standards, we can achieve our climate targets while creating thousands of new jobs. The transition will not be easy, but it is necessary for the future of our country and our planet. We have consulted extensively with industry, environmental groups, and Indigenous communities to ensure this policy works for all Canadians.

This comprehensive approach has received support from various stakeholders.

Poetry and Verse

Maintain original line breaks and formatting:

The famous opening of "In Flanders Fields" reads:

In Flanders fields the poppies blow
Between the crosses, row on row,
That mark our place; and in the sky
The larks, still bravely singing, fly
Scarce heard amid the guns below.

Multiple Paragraphs

For quotations with multiple paragraphs:

  • Indent first line of each paragraph
  • No closing quotes until the end
  • Opening quotes at start of each paragraph (in run-in format only)

When to Use Block Format

  • Legal documents requiring exact quotes
  • Literary analysis with extended passages
  • Historical documents
  • Interview transcripts
  • Policy statements

Periods and Commas

Inside quotation marks when:

  • The punctuation is part of the quotation
  • The quotation is a complete sentence

Outside quotation marks when:

  • The quotation is a fragment
  • The punctuation belongs to the main sentence
Example 8.04

She said, "I'll be there at noon." (complete sentence)

He described it as "revolutionary". (fragment)

The sign read "No Entry", but we went in anyway. (comma belongs to main sentence)

Question Marks and Exclamation Points

Place according to what is being questioned or exclaimed:

Example 8.05

She asked, "When does the meeting start?" (quoted question)

Did he say "I'll handle it"? (main sentence is question)

Who shouted "Fire!"? (both are questions/exclamations)

Stop saying "whatever"! (main sentence is exclamation)

Colons and Semicolons

Always place outside quotation marks:

Example 8.06

She had one word for it: "disaster".

He called it "brilliant"; I disagreed.

The report mentions three "critical factors": timing, budget, and personnel.

Single Quotation Marks

Use for quotations within quotations:

Example 8.07

The witness testified, "I heard him yell 'Stop!' before the accident."

She said, "The article 'Climate Crisis' changed my perspective."

8.12â8.13 Other Uses of Quotation Marks

Beyond direct quotations, quotation marks serve several other purposes:

Titles of Short Works

Use quotation marks for:

  • Articles: "The Future of Healthcare"
  • Short stories: "The Bear Came Over the Mountain"
  • Poems: "In Flanders Fields"
  • Songs: "The Hockey Song"
  • Episodes: "The One Where Ross Gets Married"
  • Chapters: "Chapter 3: Spelling Rules"

Words Used in Special Senses

Example 8.12a

The term "sustainable" has different meanings in different contexts.

What exactly do you mean by "soon"?

Their idea of "luxury" was quite modest.

Technical Terms on First Use

Example 8.12b

This process is called "photosynthesis."

The "cloud" refers to remote servers accessed via the internet.

Note: After first use, omit quotation marks.

So-called or Alleged

Use quotation marks to show skepticism or distance:

Example 8.12c

The so-called "expert" had no relevant credentials.

This "solution" created more problems.

8.15 Dialogue

When writing dialogue, follow these conventions:

Basic Dialogue Format

Example 8.15a

"I'll meet you at the library," Sarah said.

Tom replied, "That works for me."

"Great!" she exclaimed. "See you at two o'clock."

New Speaker, New Paragraph

"Have you seen the new policy?" asked Michael.

"Yes, I reviewed it yesterday," Lisa responded. "There are some significant changes."

"I noticed that too. Do you think it will affect our department?"

"Almost certainly. We should schedule a meeting to discuss it."

Dialogue Tags

  • Before: She said, "I'll be there."
  • After: "I'll be there," she said.
  • Middle: "I'll be there," she said, "at noon."
  • Action: "I'll be there." She checked her watch. "At noon."

Punctuation in Dialogue

Example 8.15b

Statement: "I'm ready," he said.

Question: "Are you ready?" he asked.

Exclamation: "Watch out!" she shouted.

Interrupted: "I was justâ" He stopped mid-sentence.

Trailing off: "I'm not sure..." Her voice faded.

Internal Thoughts

Options for presenting thoughts:

  • Italics without quotes: This can't be happening, she thought.
  • Regular text: She wondered if this was really happening.
  • Tagged like dialogue: "This can't be happening," she thought.
Tip: Choose one method for thoughts and use it consistently throughout your document.
