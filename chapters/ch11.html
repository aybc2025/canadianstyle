Learn to write clear, professional reports and meeting minutes

11.01 General

Reports and minutes are essential business documents that record information, analyze situations, and preserve institutional memory. This chapter covers the standard formats and best practices for both.

Purpose of Reports

  • Inform: Present facts and findings
  • Analyze: Examine problems or situations
  • Recommend: Suggest courses of action
  • Document: Create official records
  • Persuade: Support decisions or changes

Types of Reports

Informal Reports

  • 1-10 pages
  • Memo or letter format
  • Limited front matter
  • Direct approach

Formal Reports

  • 10+ pages
  • Full front matter
  • Multiple sections
  • Extensive research

Special Reports

  • Annual reports
  • Feasibility studies
  • Progress reports
  • Technical reports
Remember

The same principle applies to all reports: say it clearly and succinctly. Readers should quickly understand who wrote it, for whom, and why.

11.02 Preparation

Before writing, determine your audience and purpose:

Key Questions

  1. Who will read this? Primary and secondary audiences
  2. What do they need? Information, analysis, or recommendations
  3. How will they use it? Decision-making, information, action
  4. What do they already know? Background and context needed
  5. What are their concerns? Address potential objections

Planning Steps

1. Define Purpose

Write a clear purpose statement

2. Gather Information

Research, interviews, data analysis

3. Organize Content

Create outline, group related items

4. Choose Format

Select appropriate report type

Organization Patterns

  • Chronological: Time sequence (progress reports)
  • Geographical: By location or region
  • Functional: By department or function
  • Order of importance: Most to least important
  • Problem-solution: Define problem, present solutions
Tip: Write your executive summary last, after you've completed the full report and know exactly what to highlight.

11.03 Format

Report format depends on length, formality, and organizational standards:

Standard Report Structure

Front Matter

  1. Title page
  2. Letter of transmittal
  3. Table of contents
  4. List of figures/tables
  5. Executive summary

Body

  1. Introduction
  2. Background/Context
  3. Methodology (if applicable)
  4. Findings/Discussion
  5. Conclusions
  6. Recommendations

Back Matter

  1. References/Bibliography
  2. Appendices
  3. Glossary (if needed)
  4. Index (for long reports)

Page Layout

  • Margins: 2.5 cm (1") minimum all sides
  • Font: 12-point serif for body text
  • Line spacing: 1.5 or double
  • Alignment: Left-aligned (not justified)
  • Page numbers: Bottom center or top right

Binding Considerations

  • Left margin: 3.5 cm if binding
  • Print single-sided for formal reports
  • Use quality paper (24 lb or higher)
  • Professional binding for external distribution

11.04 Letter of Transmittal

A formal cover letter that:

  • Accompanies the report
  • Identifies authorization
  • States purpose and scope
  • Highlights key findings
  • Acknowledges assistance

11.07 Executive Summary

A standalone overview that includes:

  • Purpose: Why the report was written
  • Scope: What it covers
  • Methodology: How information was gathered
  • Key findings: Main discoveries
  • Conclusions: What findings mean
  • Recommendations: Suggested actions

⚠️ Common Mistake

Don't confuse an executive summary with an introduction. The summary is a miniature version of the entire report, while the introduction sets context.

11.16 Headings

Headings guide readers through your report and show the relationship between sections:

Heading Hierarchy

LEVEL 1: MAJOR SECTIONS

Centered, all caps, bold

Level 2: Subsections

Flush left, initial caps, bold

Level 3: Sub-subsections

Flush left, initial caps, italics

Level 4: Paragraph headings. Run-in with text, bold, period.

Numbering Systems

Decimal System

1.0 Introduction
  1.1 Background
  1.2 Purpose
    1.2.1 Primary objectives
    1.2.2 Secondary objectives
2.0 Methodology
  2.1 Data collection
  2.2 Analysis

Traditional Outline

I. Introduction
  A. Background
  B. Purpose
    1. Primary objectives
    2. Secondary objectives
II. Methodology
  A. Data collection
  B. Analysis

Heading Guidelines

  • Use parallel grammatical structure
  • Keep headings concise but descriptive
  • Avoid questions as headings
  • Don't use abbreviations
  • Ensure at least two subheadings at each level
  • Leave more space above than below headings
Accessibility Tip: Use built-in heading styles in your word processor. This helps screen readers navigate the document.

11.22–11.27 Minutes

Minutes provide an official record of meetings, decisions, and action items.

11.23 Agenda

A well-planned agenda ensures productive meetings:

ENVIRONMENTAL COMMITTEE MEETING

Date: March 15, 2024
Time: 2:00 p.m. – 4:00 p.m.
Location: Conference Room A

AGENDA
  1. Call to Order (2 min)
  2. Approval of Agenda (3 min)
  3. Approval of Minutes from February 15, 2024 (5 min)
  4. Business Arising from Minutes (10 min)
  5. New Business
    1. Budget Report – J. Smith (15 min)
    2. Project Updates – M. Brown (20 min)
    3. Policy Review – K. Lee (30 min)
  6. Other Business (10 min)
  7. Next Meeting Date
  8. Adjournment

11.25 Writing Minutes

Minutes should be:

  • Objective: Record facts, not opinions
  • Concise: Summarize discussions
  • Complete: Include all decisions and actions
  • Clear: Understandable to absent members

Essential Elements

  1. Meeting identification (date, time, place)
  2. Attendance (present, absent, guests)
  3. Approval of previous minutes
  4. Summary of discussions
  5. Decisions made (including votes)
  6. Action items with responsible parties
  7. Next meeting date
  8. Time of adjournment

11.26 Indirect Speech

Use indirect speech and past tense:

Examples

Direct: "I think we should increase the budget."

Minutes: Ms. Smith recommended increasing the budget.


Direct: "Will this affect our timeline?"

Minutes: Mr. Jones inquired whether this would affect the timeline.

Action Items Format

ACTION: J. Smith to prepare cost analysis by April 1, 2024.

ACTION: M. Brown to circulate revised policy to all members by March 20, 2024.

Motions and Voting

MOTION: Moved by K. Lee, seconded by P. Chen, that the committee approve the proposed budget increase of $50,000.

CARRIED (7 in favour, 2 opposed, 1 abstention)

Best Practice: Distribute draft minutes within 48 hours while memories are fresh. This allows for corrections before the next meeting.
