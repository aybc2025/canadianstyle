11.01 Introduction

Reports and minutes are essential documents in government communication. They convey information, document decisions, and provide accountability. Effective reports are well-structured, clearly written, and professionally formatted.

Key Principle

Every report should have a clear purpose, logical structure, and appropriate level of detail for its intended audience. The format should support, not hinder, communication.

Types of Government Reports

  • Policy reports: Analysis and recommendations for decision-makers
  • Progress reports: Status updates on projects and programs
  • Research reports: Findings from studies and investigations
  • Annual reports: Comprehensive yearly overviews
  • Technical reports: Specialized information for experts
  • Committee reports: Findings and recommendations from committees

Report Planning Considerations

Before writing, consider:

  • Purpose: What decision or action should result?
  • Audience: Who will read and use this report?
  • Scope: What topics will be covered?
  • Timeline: When is the report needed?
  • Distribution: Who should receive copies?

Quality Standards

  • Accuracy: All information must be correct and current
  • Clarity: Writing should be clear and accessible
  • Completeness: Include all necessary information
  • Objectivity: Present balanced, unbiased analysis
  • Relevance: Focus on information that supports the purpose

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Report Problems

Avoid these frequent issues: unclear purpose, poor organization, excessive length, inadequate supporting evidence, and inconsistent formatting.

11.02 Report Structure

A logical structure helps readers navigate your report and find the information they need. Government reports typically follow established conventions that readers expect.

Standard Report Structure

  1. Title Page
  2. Executive Summary
  3. Table of Contents
  4. Introduction
  5. Body (Main Sections)
  6. Conclusions and Recommendations
  7. References
  8. Appendices

Executive Summary Guidelines

Include in executive summary:

  • Purpose and scope of the report
  • Key findings and conclusions
  • Main recommendations
  • Implementation considerations

Length: 1-2 pages for most reports, 10% of total length maximum

Introduction Components

  • Background: Context and history
  • Purpose: Why the report was written
  • Scope: What is and isn't covered
  • Methodology: How information was gathered
  • Limitations: Constraints and assumptions

Body Section Organization

Common organizational patterns:

  • Chronological: Events in time order
  • Topical: Different aspects of the subject
  • Problem-solution: Issues and proposed fixes
  • Comparative: Analysis of alternatives
  • Priority-based: Most to least important

Conclusions and Recommendations

  • Conclusions: What the evidence shows
  • Recommendations: What actions to take
  • Implementation: How to proceed
  • Next steps: Follow-up actions required

Recommendation Format

Effective recommendations are:

  • Specific: Clear about what should be done
  • Actionable: Feasible to implement
  • Prioritized: Indicate importance or sequence
  • Justified: Supported by evidence in the report

Example format:

Recommendation 1: That the Department implement a new training program for staff by March 2025, as outlined in Section 4.2, to address the skills gap identified in this analysis.

üí° Reader-Friendly Reports

Use headings, bullet points, and white space to make your report easy to scan. Busy executives often read only the executive summary and recommendations.

11.03 Headings

Headings organize information and help readers navigate documents. A consistent heading system shows the logical structure of your report and makes it easier to find specific information.

Heading Hierarchy

  • Level 1: Major sections (Introduction, Findings, Recommendations)
  • Level 2: Main subsections within major sections
  • Level 3: Specific topics within subsections
  • Level 4: Detailed subtopics (use sparingly)

Heading Format Examples

Numbered System:

  • 1. Introduction
  • 2. Current Situation
  •   2.1 Background
  •   2.2 Key Issues
  •     2.2.1 Budget Constraints
  •     2.2.2 Staffing Challenges
  • 3. Recommendations

Typography-Based System:

  • LEVEL 1: ALL CAPS, BOLD
  • Level 2: Bold, Title Case
  • Level 3: Italic, Title Case
  • Level 4: Regular text, title case

Heading Writing Guidelines

  • Descriptive: Clearly indicate section content
  • Parallel: Use consistent grammatical structure
  • Concise: Keep to one line when possible
  • Informative: Help readers decide what to read

Good vs. Poor Headings

‚úì Good headings (parallel, specific):

  • 3.1 Analyzing Current Budget
  • 3.2 Identifying Cost Savings
  • 3.3 Recommending Budget Changes

‚úó Poor headings (not parallel, vague):

  • 3.1 Budget Analysis
  • 3.2 How to Save Money
  • 3.3 What We Think Should Happen

Numbering Systems

  • Decimal system: 1.1, 1.2, 1.2.1, 1.2.2
  • Traditional outline: I, A, 1, a
  • Simple numbering: 1, 2, 3 (for short reports)
  • No numbers: Typography alone (for informal reports)

Government Report Headings

Standard government sections:

  • Executive Summary
  • Background and Context
  • Current Situation Analysis
  • Options and Alternatives
  • Risk Assessment
  • Financial Implications
  • Implementation Plan
  • Conclusions and Recommendations

‚ö†Ô∏è Heading Pitfalls

Avoid: Too many heading levels (more than 4), inconsistent formatting, vague headings like "Discussion" or "Other Issues," and headings with no content following them.

üí° Table of Contents

Your heading structure becomes your table of contents. Well-written headings make it easy to generate an accurate and useful table of contents automatically.

11.04 Front Matter

Front matter provides essential information about the report before the main content begins. It helps readers understand the document's purpose, structure, and key findings quickly.

Front Matter Components (in order)

  1. Title Page
  2. Letter of Transmittal (if required)
  3. Executive Summary
  4. Table of Contents
  5. List of Tables and Figures
  6. Acknowledgements (if appropriate)

Title Page Elements

Required information:

  • Report title (descriptive and specific)
  • Subtitle (if needed for clarity)
  • Author(s) or organization
  • Date of publication
  • Department or agency name
  • Report number or file reference

Implementation of Digital Services Strategy

Progress Report for Fiscal Year 2023-24

Prepared by: Digital Transformation Office
Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat
March 2024
Report No. TBS-2024-015

Executive Summary Best Practices

  • Standalone document: Must make sense without the full report
  • Key information only: Purpose, methods, findings, recommendations
  • Decision-focused: Emphasize information needed for decisions
  • Appropriate length: 1-2 pages for most reports

  • Format: Number, title, and page number
  • Order: Separate lists for tables and figures
  • Numbering: Consecutive throughout document

Page Numbering

  • Front matter: Roman numerals (i, ii, iii)
  • Main text: Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3)
  • Appendices: Continue Arabic numbering or use letters
  • Position: Consistent placement throughout document

üí° Letter of Transmittal

A letter of transmittal introduces the report to its recipient. Include it when submitting formal reports to senior officials. Keep it brief and highlight key points.

11.05 Appendices

Appendices contain supporting material that supplements the main report without interrupting its flow. They provide detailed information that some readers need while keeping the main text focused.

What to Include in Appendices

  • Raw data: Detailed statistics, survey results
  • Supporting documents: Legislation, regulations, policies
  • Technical details: Methodology, calculations, specifications
  • Supplementary information: Background material, historical data
  • Correspondence: Letters, emails, responses to inquiries

Appendix Organization

Labeling system:

  • Appendix A: Survey Questionnaire
  • Appendix B: Statistical Analysis
  • Appendix C: Stakeholder Responses
  • Appendix D: Budget Calculations
  • Appendix E: Legislative References

Appendix Guidelines

  • Reference in text: Direct readers to relevant appendices
  • Logical order: Arrange in order of mention in main text
  • Clear titles: Descriptive headings for each appendix
  • Self-contained: Each appendix should make sense alone

Referencing Appendices

In main text:

  • "Detailed survey results are provided in Appendix A."
  • "For complete budget calculations, see Appendix D."
  • "The full text of the regulation appears in Appendix E."
  • "As shown in Appendix B, the statistical analysis confirms..."

Formatting Considerations

  • Page numbering: Continue main document numbering
  • Headers: Include appendix letter and title
  • Original formatting: Preserve format of source documents when possible
  • Consistency: Match main document style when reformatting

Common Government Appendices

Typical appendices in government reports:

  • Appendix A: Terms of Reference or Mandate
  • Appendix B: Methodology and Data Sources
  • Appendix C: Stakeholder Consultation Summary
  • Appendix D: Financial Analysis and Projections
  • Appendix E: Risk Assessment Matrix
  • Appendix F: Implementation Timeline
  • Appendix G: Relevant Legislation and Regulations

‚ö†Ô∏è Appendix Overuse

Don't use appendices as a dumping ground for everything that didn't fit in the main text. Include only material that truly supports your findings and recommendations.

üí° Digital Considerations

In digital reports, consider linking to appendices for easy navigation. For very large appendices, consider separate files with clear naming conventions.

11.06 Tables & Charts

Tables and charts present data clearly and support your written analysis. Well-designed visuals can communicate complex information more effectively than text alone.

When to Use Tables vs. Charts

  • Tables: Exact values, detailed comparisons, reference data
  • Charts: Trends, patterns, relationships, proportions
  • Both: Complex data that benefits from multiple presentations

Table Design Principles

Table 1: Budget Allocations by Department ($ millions)

Department 2022-23 2023-24 Change (%)
Health Canada 4,235 4,456 +5.2
Transport Canada 1,890 2,104 +11.3
Environment Canada 985 1,142 +15.9

Source: Public Accounts of Canada, 2024

Table Formatting Standards

  • Numbering: Sequential throughout document (Table 1, Table 2)
  • Titles: Descriptive and placed above the table
  • Column headers: Clear, concise labels
  • Alignment: Text left-aligned, numbers right-aligned
  • Units: Clearly indicated in headers or title
  • Sources: Always cite data sources

Chart Types and Uses

Common chart types for government reports:

  • Line charts: Trends over time (budget changes, performance indicators)
  • Bar charts: Comparisons between categories (departmental spending)
  • Pie charts: Parts of a whole (budget breakdown by sector)
  • Column charts: Comparisons across time periods
  • Scatter plots: Relationships between variables

Chart Design Guidelines

  • Clear titles: Explain what the chart shows
  • Labeled axes: Include units and scale information
  • Legends: Explain symbols, colors, and patterns
  • Data labels: Include when precision is important
  • Sources: Always cite where data came from

Integration with Text

Effective integration:

  • "As shown in Table 1, budget allocations increased across all departments."
  • "Figure 2 illustrates the steady growth in program participation since 2020."
  • "The data in Appendix B, summarized in Chart 3, reveals..."
  • "Table 4 provides detailed breakdown of the costs outlined above."

Accessibility Considerations

  • Color blindness: Don't rely solely on color to convey information
  • Alternative text: Provide descriptions for charts
  • Data tables: Include underlying data for screen readers
  • High contrast: Ensure text and background contrast is sufficient

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Visualization Errors

Avoid: Misleading scales, unnecessary 3D effects, too many data series in one chart, poor color choices, and charts that don't add value to the text.

üí° Data Integrity

Always verify your data before creating visuals. Double-check calculations, ensure data sources are current, and review charts for accuracy before publication.

11.07 Meeting Minutes

Meeting minutes provide an official record of discussions, decisions, and action items. They serve as both historical documentation and a tool for accountability and follow-up.

Types of Minutes

  • Verbatim minutes: Word-for-word record (rare, usually for legal proceedings)
  • Action minutes: Decisions and action items only
  • Summary minutes: Key points of discussion and outcomes
  • Formal minutes: Detailed record following parliamentary procedure

Standard Minutes Format

Minutes of Digital Transformation Committee

Date: March 15, 2024
Time: 2:00 PM - 4:00 PM
Location: Room 340-S, Centre Block
Chair: Sarah Johnson, Assistant Deputy Minister

Present:
Sarah Johnson (Chair), ADM Digital Policy
Michael Brown, Director, IT Services
Lisa Wong, Senior Analyst, Treasury Board
Robert Lee, Communications Director

Regrets:
Jennifer Smith, Director, Data Management

1. Call to Order
The meeting was called to order at 2:05 PM.

2. Approval of Agenda
The agenda was approved as presented.
Moved by M. Brown, seconded by L. Wong. Carried.

Essential Elements

  • Meeting details: Date, time, location, chair
  • Attendance: Present members and regrets
  • Agenda approval: Acceptance or modifications
  • Previous minutes: Approval and any corrections
  • Discussion items: Key points and decisions
  • Action items: Tasks, responsibility, deadlines
  • Next meeting: Date and location if determined

Recording Decisions and Actions

Decision format:

3.2 Budget Approval
After discussion of the proposed budget for the digital transformation initiative, the committee agreed to approve the allocation of $2.5 million for Phase 1 implementation.
Decision: Approved budget of $2.5M for Phase 1.
Action: M. Brown to prepare detailed budget breakdown by March 30, 2024.

Action item format:

Action Items:
‚Ä¢ L. Wong to draft privacy assessment report (Due: April 15, 2024)
‚Ä¢ R. Lee to develop communication strategy (Due: April 1, 2024)
‚Ä¢ Chair to schedule meeting with Deputy Minister (Due: March 25, 2024)

Writing Style for Minutes

  • Third person: "The committee decided" not "We decided"
  • Past tense: Record what happened
  • Objective tone: Factual, not interpretive
  • Concise language: Capture essence, not every word
  • Formal structure: Consistent formatting throughout

Motion Recording

Standard motion format:

M. Brown moved that the committee recommend approval of the Digital Services Strategy implementation plan as presented, with amendments to the timeline as discussed.
Seconded by L. Wong.
Carried.

Vote recording (when required):

Motion carried: 4 in favour, 1 opposed, 0 abstentions.
Opposed: R. Lee

Distribution and Approval

  • Draft circulation: Send to attendees within 48 hours
  • Review period: Allow time for corrections
  • Final approval: At next meeting or by circulation
  • Official filing: Maintain permanent record

üí° Taking Effective Minutes

Focus on decisions and action items rather than detailed discussion. Use a template for consistency and bring extra copies of the agenda to track progress through items.

‚ö†Ô∏è Confidentiality and Access

Be aware of privacy and confidentiality requirements. Some minutes may be subject to Access to Information requests, while others may contain cabinet confidence.

11.08 Formatting

Consistent formatting enhances readability and gives your reports a professional appearance. Good formatting supports your content and helps readers navigate the document effectively.

Standard Format Elements

  • Fonts: Professional typefaces (Times New Roman, Arial, Calibri)
  • Font sizes: 12pt for body text, 14pt+ for headings
  • Line spacing: 1.5 or double spacing for drafts, single for final
  • Margins: 1 inch (2.5 cm) on all sides minimum
  • Alignment: Left-aligned for most text, justified for formal reports

Page Layout Guidelines

Header information:

  • Document title or abbreviated title
  • Page number
  • Date (for drafts)
  • Security classification (if applicable)

Footer information:

  • Department or organization name
  • Contact information
  • Document version or file name

Heading Styles

  • Consistency: Same style for same level throughout
  • Hierarchy: Clear visual distinction between levels
  • Spacing: More space before headings than after
  • Font treatment: Bold, size changes, or capitalization

Government Document Standards

Canada Wordbook standards:

  • Paper size: 8.5" √ó 11" (letter size)
  • Binding margin: Extra space on left side
  • Page numbers: Bottom center or top right
  • Official languages: Bilingual requirements as applicable

Lists and Bullets

  • Bullet points: Simple bullets (‚Ä¢) for unordered lists
  • Numbered lists: Sequential items or procedures
  • Indentation: Consistent spacing for sub-items
  • Parallel structure: Consistent grammar in list items

White Space and Readability

Effective use of white space:

  • Space between sections for visual separation
  • Adequate margins for notes and binding
  • Paragraph breaks for new ideas
  • Indentation to show document structure

Graphics and Images

  • Resolution: High quality for printed documents
  • Placement: Near relevant text
  • Captions: Descriptive labels for all visuals
  • Sizing: Appropriate scale for readability

Digital Document Considerations

For online distribution:

  • PDF format: Preserves formatting across platforms
  • Bookmarks: Enable navigation in long documents
  • Hyperlinks: Link to appendices and external sources
  • Accessibility: Alt text for images, proper heading tags
  • File size: Optimize for email and web distribution

‚ö†Ô∏è Formatting Pitfalls

Avoid: Overuse of bold and italics, too many font styles, excessive capitalization, poor line spacing, and inconsistent formatting between sections.

üí° Templates and Styles

Create or use existing templates for consistency across reports. Set up paragraph styles in your word processor to ensure uniform formatting throughout your document.
