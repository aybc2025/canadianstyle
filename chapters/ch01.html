Abbreviations

Rules for using abbreviations correctly in government writing

1.01 Introduction

The use of abbreviations has gained greater acceptance as an increasing number of new products and organizations are identified by shorter and more easily recognizable word forms.

In addition to abbreviations in the strict sense (including the short forms of common nouns, Latin expressions and titles), this chapter contains information and recommendations regarding acronyms, initialisms, and symbols such as those for metric units, which are uniform in many languages.

Key Principles

  • Use abbreviations only when they improve readability
  • Ensure consistency throughout your document
  • Consider your audience's familiarity with the abbreviation
  • Follow established conventions for your field

1.02 General Guidelines and Observations

Many abbreviations will not be understood unless the term is written in full at first mention, with the abbreviation given in parentheses. Follow these general rules:

Basic Rules

  • In general, abbreviate words only when the short form will be immediately recognized by the reader, and ensure that the same abbreviation is used elsewhere in your text to represent the word or words involved.
  • Some standard abbreviations such as i.e., AD, IQ, ESR, CBC and MP do not have to be spelled out because they are well known and in many cases occur as dictionary entries.
  • Many commonly used words that are actually abbreviations are now rarely regarded as such, including ad, fridge, phone, exam, memo, photo and math. Most such words should be avoided in formal writing, although cello and bus are exceptions to this rule.
First Mention Rule

Correct: The Canadian Broadcasting Corporation (CBC) announced new programming.

Later references: The CBC will continue this policy.

1.03 Periods

The use of periods in abbreviations follows specific patterns and conventions.

When to Use Periods

Use Periods With:

  • Most lower-case abbreviations: etc., e.g., i.e.
  • Abbreviated titles: Dr., Mr., Ms.
  • Traditional Latin abbreviations: a.m., p.m.
  • Units of measurement in text: ft., in.

Omit Periods With:

  • Metric symbols: kg, cm, km
  • Most acronyms: CBC, RCMP, NATO
  • Scientific symbols: H₂O, CO₂
  • Postal codes: ON, BC, QC

1.04 Plurals

Forming plurals of abbreviations requires careful attention to established conventions.

General Rules for Plurals

  • Add s (no apostrophe) to most abbreviations: MPs, URLs, DVDs
  • Add 's only when confusion might result: A's and B's (grades)
  • Some abbreviations remain unchanged in plural: pp. (pages)
Singular Plural Note
MP MPs Standard plural
p. pp. Traditional form
A (grade) A's Prevents confusion
CEO CEOs No apostrophe needed

1.05 Capital Letters and Hyphens

Capitalization and hyphenation in abbreviations follow specific patterns.

Capitalization Rules

  • Use initial capitals for proper nouns and official titles: UN, NATO, Dr.
  • Use lower case for common abbreviations: etc., i.e., vs.
  • Follow the style of the full form when in doubt

Hyphenation Guidelines

  • Retain hyphens from the full form: vice-president → VP or V-P
  • Use hyphens to avoid confusion: T-shirt, X-ray
  • Compound abbreviations may use hyphens: CD-ROM
Capitalization Examples

Proper nouns: UNESCO, UNICEF, NATO

Common terms: etc., e.g., cf.

Mixed: PhD, MSc, BSc

1.06 Titles Used with Personal Names

Titles and forms of address have specific abbreviation conventions in formal writing.

Academic and Professional Titles

Academic Titles

  • Dr. (Doctor)
  • Prof. (Professor)
  • Rev. (Reverend)

Courtesy Titles

  • Mr. (Mister)
  • Mrs. (Missus)
  • Ms. (Miss/Mrs.)

Military Titles

  • Gen. (General)
  • Col. (Colonel)
  • Maj. (Major)

Usage Guidelines

  • Use periods with abbreviated titles before names
  • Spell out titles when they appear alone
  • Be consistent throughout your document

1.07 Military Abbreviations

Military terms and ranks have established abbreviation forms used in government writing.

Military Ranks

Army Ranks

  • Gen. (General)
  • Col. (Colonel)
  • Maj. (Major)
  • Capt. (Captain)
  • Lt. (Lieutenant)
  • Sgt. (Sergeant)
  • Cpl. (Corporal)

Naval Ranks

  • Adm. (Admiral)
  • Capt. (Captain)
  • Cdr. (Commander)
  • Lt.-Cdr. (Lieutenant-Commander)
  • Lt. (Lieutenant)
  • PO (Petty Officer)

Usage Guidelines

  • Always use periods with military rank abbreviations
  • Capitalize all military abbreviations
  • Use the full rank when first mentioned in formal documents
  • Be consistent with abbreviation style throughout

1.08 University Degrees, Professional Designations, Military Decorations, Honours, Awards and Memberships

Academic degrees and professional designations have specific formatting requirements in government documents.

Academic Degrees

Bachelor's Degrees

  • B.A. (Bachelor of Arts)
  • B.Sc. (Bachelor of Science)
  • B.Ed. (Bachelor of Education)
  • B.Comm. (Bachelor of Commerce)
  • LL.B. (Bachelor of Laws)

Master's Degrees

  • M.A. (Master of Arts)
  • M.Sc. (Master of Science)
  • M.Ed. (Master of Education)
  • M.B.A. (Master of Business Administration)
  • LL.M. (Master of Laws)

Doctoral Degrees

  • Ph.D. (Doctor of Philosophy)
  • Ed.D. (Doctor of Education)
  • D.Phil. (Doctor of Philosophy - Oxford style)
  • LL.D. (Doctor of Laws)

Professional Degrees

  • M.D. (Doctor of Medicine)
  • J.D. (Juris Doctor)
  • D.D.S. (Doctor of Dental Surgery)
  • D.V.M. (Doctor of Veterinary Medicine)

Professional Designations

  • C.A. (Chartered Accountant)
  • P.Eng. (Professional Engineer)
  • C.P.A. (Certified Public Accountant)
  • R.N. (Registered Nurse)
  • Q.C. (Queen's Counsel)

Military Decorations and Honours

  • V.C. (Victoria Cross)
  • C.M. (Member of the Order of Canada)
  • O.C. (Officer of the Order of Canada)
  • C.C. (Companion of the Order of Canada)
  • M.M. (Military Medal)

Formatting Rules

  • Use periods between letters: Ph.D., not PhD
  • List degrees in order of importance, highest first
  • Separate multiple degrees with commas
  • Place after the person's name: John Smith, M.A., Ph.D.
