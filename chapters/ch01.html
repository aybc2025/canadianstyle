Abbreviations

Learn the proper use of abbreviations in Canadian government writing

1.01 Introduction

Abbreviations save space and time in writing, but they must be used carefully to maintain clarity. This chapter covers Canadian government standards for creating and using abbreviations effectively.

Key Principle

Use abbreviations sparingly in formal writing. When in doubt, spell it out completely.

When to Use Abbreviations

  • In technical documents where space is limited
  • For widely recognized terms (e.g., etc., i.e., e.g.)
  • For units of measurement in scientific contexts
  • In tables, charts, and reference lists
  • When the abbreviation is more familiar than the full form

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid Abbreviations When:

  • Writing for a general audience
  • The abbreviation appears only once
  • It creates ambiguity or confusion
  • Starting a sentence (unless unavoidable)

1.02 General Principles

General Principles

  • Use abbreviations sparingly in formal writing
  • Define abbreviations on first use
  • Be consistent throughout the document
  • Consider your audience - avoid technical abbreviations for general readers
  • Maintain readability - too many abbreviations make text hard to read

Defining Abbreviations

‚úì Correct: The Department of National Defence (DND) announced...

Later in document: DND officials confirmed...

‚úó Incorrect: The DND announced... (undefined abbreviation)

Periods in Abbreviations

  • With periods: Dr., Mr., Mrs., vs., etc., i.e.
  • Without periods: NATO, UNESCO, PhD, CBC, HTML
  • Mixed practice: Follow established conventions

üí° Pro Tip

If an abbreviation appears only once or twice in a document, it's usually better to spell out the full term each time.

1.03 Sentence Capitals

Abbreviations that begin sentences should be capitalized according to specific rules.

Rules for Sentence Capitals

  • Capitalize the first letter of an abbreviation that begins a sentence
  • Do not capitalize subsequent letters unless they are proper nouns
  • Avoid starting sentences with lowercase abbreviations
  • Restructure sentences when possible to avoid abbreviations at the beginning

Examples

‚úì Correct: E.g., the following examples illustrate...

‚úì Correct: Dr. Smith will attend the meeting.

‚úì Better: For example, the following illustrations show...

‚úó Incorrect: e.g., the following examples...

‚úó Avoid: etc. appears frequently in casual writing.

Preferred Alternatives

  • Instead of "E.g.," use "For example," or "For instance,"
  • Instead of "I.e.," use "That is," or "In other words,"
  • Instead of "Etc." use "and so on" or list all items

1.04 Proper Nouns

Abbreviations of proper nouns follow specific capitalization and formatting rules in Canadian government writing.

Organization Names

  • International organizations: UN, NATO, UNESCO, WHO
  • Government departments: Always spell out on first use
  • Crown corporations: CBC, VIA Rail, Canada Post
  • Agencies: RCMP, CSIS, CRA

Government Department Examples

First use: The Department of Justice Canada (Justice Canada) released...

Subsequent uses: Justice Canada announced...

First use: Statistics Canada (StatCan) reported...

Subsequent uses: StatCan data shows...

Place Names

  • Provinces: B.C., N.S., P.E.I., N.L. (with periods)
  • Countries: U.S., U.K., U.S.S.R. (with periods)
  • Cities: Generally spell out (avoid abbreviations)

‚ö†Ô∏è Caution with Acronyms

Some government acronyms may not be familiar to all readers. Always consider your audience when deciding whether to use an abbreviation.

1.05 Government Bodies

Government bodies, departments, and agencies have specific abbreviation conventions in Canadian government communications.

Federal Departments

  • Always spell out on first reference
  • Use official short forms when established
  • Avoid creating new abbreviations
  • Maintain consistency within documents

Common Federal Departments

Full Name Acceptable Short Form Abbreviation
Department of Justice Canada Justice Canada DOJ (avoid)
Department of National Defence National Defence DND
Public Safety Canada Public Safety PSC (avoid)
Employment and Social Development Canada ESDC ESDC

Parliamentary Bodies

  • House of Commons: the House (after first reference)
  • Senate of Canada: the Senate (after first reference)
  • Parliament of Canada: Parliament (after first reference)

Provincial and Territorial Bodies

Spell out on first use:

The Government of British Columbia announced...

The Alberta government reported...

The Government of the Northwest Territories confirmed...

üí° Best Practice

When in doubt about government abbreviations, consult the official website or style guide of the specific department or agency.

1.06 Titles & Positions

Professional titles, academic titles, and government positions have established abbreviation conventions.

Personal Titles

  • Dr. (Doctor) - use before name
  • Mr., Mrs., Ms. - use before surname
  • Prof. (Professor) - acceptable in informal contexts
  • Hon. (Honourable) - for certain government officials

Professional Titles

‚úì Correct: Dr. Sarah Johnson, Chief Medical Officer

‚úì Correct: The Honourable Jane Smith, Minister of Health

‚úì Correct: Prof. Michael Brown presented the research

‚úó Avoid: Sarah Johnson, Dr. (title after name)

‚úó Incorrect: Hon Jane Smith (missing period)

Government Positions

  • Prime Minister: PM (only in informal contexts)
  • Minister: Generally spell out
  • Deputy Minister: DM (internal government use)
  • Assistant Deputy Minister: ADM (internal use)

Military and Police Ranks

Full Title Abbreviation Usage
General Gen. Gen. Lisa White
Colonel Col. Col. John Davis
Sergeant Sgt. Sgt. Mary Wilson
Constable Cst. Cst. Robert Lee

‚ö†Ô∏è Formal vs. Informal Usage

In very formal documents, consider spelling out all titles and positions. Abbreviations are more acceptable in internal communications and technical documents.

Canadian Provinces and Territories

  • Acceptable in addresses and technical documents
  • Spell out in formal narrative text

In addresses: Vancouver, B.C.

In narrative: The government of British Columbia announced...

In tables: B.C., Alta., Ont.

1.08 Brand Names

Brand names and corporate identities should be treated with care in government documents to maintain neutrality and accuracy.

Corporate Brand Names

  • Use official company names when possible
  • Avoid branded abbreviations in formal documents
  • Consider generic alternatives for government neutrality
  • Check current corporate branding for accuracy

Technology and Software

Instead of: MS Word ‚Üí Use: Microsoft Word or word processing software

Instead of: PowerPoint ‚Üí Use: presentation software

Instead of: iPhone ‚Üí Use: smartphone or mobile device

When brand specific: If discussing a specific product, use the full brand name initially, then generic terms.

Media and Communications

  • CBC: Canadian Broadcasting Corporation (spell out first use)
  • CTV, Global: Acceptable for broadcast networks
  • Social media: Use platform names as needed

Transportation and Travel

Brand/Company Government Usage Context
Air Canada Air Canada Use full name
VIA Rail VIA Rail Canada Crown corporation
WestJet WestJet or commercial airline Private company

‚ö†Ô∏è Government Neutrality

In policy documents and public communications, consider using generic terms instead of specific brand names to maintain government neutrality and avoid appearing to endorse particular products or services.

üí° Legal Considerations

When using brand names, ensure accuracy and respect trademark conventions. Some companies have specific guidelines for how their names should be written.

1.09 Academic Degrees

Academic degrees and professional designations have established abbreviation formats in Canadian usage.

Degree Abbreviations

  • Use periods in most degree abbreviations
  • Place after the person's name
  • Use sparingly in running text
  • Separate multiple degrees with commas

Common Academic Degrees

Degree Abbreviation Example
Bachelor of Arts B.A. Sarah Johnson, B.A.
Bachelor of Science B.Sc. Mark Davis, B.Sc.
Master of Arts M.A. Lisa Wong, M.A.
Master of Science M.Sc. Robert Lee, M.Sc.
Master of Business Administration M.B.A. Jennifer Smith, M.B.A.
Doctor of Philosophy Ph.D. Michael Brown, Ph.D.
Doctor of Medicine M.D. Dr. Patricia Wilson, M.D.
Juris Doctor J.D. David Thompson, J.D.

Professional Designations

  • C.A.: Chartered Accountant
  • P.Eng.: Professional Engineer
  • R.N.: Registered Nurse
  • C.P.A.: Certified Public Accountant

Usage Guidelines

‚úì First mention: Dr. Elizabeth Chen, Professor of Environmental Science

‚úì Subsequent mentions: Dr. Chen or Professor Chen

‚úì Multiple degrees: James Wilson, B.A., M.A., Ph.D.

‚úó Avoid: The Ph.D. presented her research (degree as noun)

‚úó Redundant: Dr. Sarah Johnson, Ph.D. (use either Dr. or Ph.D.)

Canadian vs. International Formats

  • Canadian: B.A., M.A., Ph.D. (with periods)
  • Some institutions: BA, MA, PhD (without periods)
  • Follow institutional preference when known

üí° Context Matters

In government documents, use academic credentials when they're relevant to the person's role or expertise. In general correspondence, they're often unnecessary.
