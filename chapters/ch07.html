7.01 Introduction

Punctuation serves to clarify meaning and guide readers through the structure of written text. This chapter covers all punctuation marks used in Canadian government publications, following the conventions established in The Canadian Style.

Fundamental Principle

Punctuation should enhance readability and prevent misunderstanding. When in doubt, choose clarity over complexity.

General Guidelines

  • Maintain consistency throughout your document
  • Follow established Canadian government conventions
  • Consider your audience and document type
  • Use punctuation to support, not replace, clear writing

7.02 Spacing

Proper spacing around punctuation marks ensures professional appearance and readability in all government documents.

Standard Spacing Rules

  • One space after periods, question marks, exclamation points
  • One space after commas, semicolons, colons
  • No space before punctuation marks (except opening quotes/parentheses)
  • No space after opening or before closing quotation marks

Correct Spacing Examples

Periods: The meeting ended. We reviewed the results.

Commas: Please bring pens, paper, and notebooks.

Colons: Remember this rule: always proofread carefully.

Quotation marks: She said, "The project is complete."

Incorrect spacing:

The meeting ended.We reviewed the results. ‚ùå

Please bring pens ,paper ,and notebooks. ‚ùå

She said ,"The project is complete". ‚ùå

üí° Modern Practice

Use only one space after periods, not two. This follows current typographic conventions and government style guides.

7.03 The Period - Main Purpose

The period (full stop) marks the end of declarative and imperative sentences and serves as a decimal point in numerical expressions.

Primary Uses

  • Declarative sentences: Statements of fact or opinion
  • Imperative sentences: Commands or requests
  • Abbreviations: Mr., Dr., etc., i.e.
  • Decimal points: 23.5, $1,234.56

Examples

Declarative: The committee approved the budget.

Imperative: Please submit your report by Friday.

Abbreviations: Dr. Smith will attend the 3:00 p.m. meeting.

Decimals: The temperature rose to 25.7¬∞C.

Abbreviations with Periods

  • Titles: Mr., Mrs., Ms., Dr., Prof.
  • Academic degrees: B.A., M.A., Ph.D.
  • Latin terms: etc., i.e., e.g., vs.
  • Time designations: a.m., p.m.

‚ö†Ô∏è Modern Exceptions

Some organizations now omit periods in certain abbreviations (PhD, AM, PM). Follow your organization's style guide consistently.

7.04 Imperatives, Exclamations and Indirect Questions

Periods end imperative sentences and indirect questions, but the tone and context determine when exclamation points might be more appropriate.

Imperatives with Periods

  • Polite requests: Please review the document.
  • Instructions: Complete the form in black ink.
  • Mild commands: Submit your report by Friday.
  • Procedures: Follow these three steps.

Imperatives with Exclamation Points

  • Urgent commands: Stop! Evacuate immediately!
  • Warnings: Danger! Hard hat area!
  • Strong emphasis: Don't forget the deadline!

Examples

Indirect questions (use periods):

She asked what time the meeting would start.

I wonder if the report is ready.

The manager inquired whether we had the necessary resources.

Direct questions (use question marks):

What time will the meeting start?

Is the report ready?

üí° Tone Consideration

In formal government documents, use periods for most imperatives unless genuine urgency or danger requires exclamation points.

7.05 Ellipsis Points

Ellipsis points (three spaced periods) indicate omitted material in quotations and unfinished thoughts in dialogue or informal writing.

Format and Spacing

  • Three periods with spaces between them: . . .
  • Space before and after the ellipsis
  • Fourth period if omission follows complete sentence
  • Brackets in academic citations: [...]

Uses of Ellipsis Points

Omitted text in quotations:

Original: "The policy will benefit employees, contractors, and volunteers across all departments."

With ellipsis: "The policy will benefit employees . . . across all departments."

Unfinished thoughts:

"I was thinking we might . . . well, never mind."

Trailing off:

"The results were quite unexpected . . ."

Special Cases

  • End of sentence: "The meeting was productive. . . . We achieved our goals."
  • Academic style: "The study found [...] significant improvements."
  • Mathematical series: 1, 2, 3, . . . , n

‚ö†Ô∏è Usage Guidelines

Don't use ellipsis points at the beginning or end of quotations unless showing that the original text continues beyond what you've quoted.

7.06 Leaders

Leaders are rows of dots used in tables of contents, indexes, and tabular material to guide the reader's eye across the page.

Purpose and Function

  • Visual guidance: Connect items to page numbers or values
  • Table of contents: Link chapter titles to page numbers
  • Financial tables: Connect line items to amounts
  • Indexes: Connect entries to page references

Examples

Table of contents:

Chapter 1: Introduction ........................... 1
Chapter 2: Methodology .......................... 15
Chapter 3: Results ................................. 34

Financial statement:

Revenue ......................................... $125,000
Expenses ........................................ $98,000
Net Income ..................................... $27,000

Formatting Guidelines

  • Consistent spacing: Use regular intervals between dots
  • Alignment: Ensure right-aligned numbers line up properly
  • Length: Adjust to fill space appropriately
  • Alternatives: Some styles use spaces or tabs instead

üí° Modern Alternatives

Digital documents often use tab stops or table formatting instead of leaders, but leaders remain useful in printed materials.

7.07 Other Uses

Periods serve several specialized functions beyond ending sentences, including use in outlines, run-in headings, and technical contexts.

Run-in Headings

When headings are incorporated into the paragraph text rather than standing alone:

  • Use period after the heading
  • Usually set in bold or italic type
  • Text continues on same line

Run-in Heading Example

Budget considerations. The total project cost is estimated at $2.5 million, with funding allocated from three sources: federal grants (60%), provincial contributions (25%), and private partnerships (15%).

Outline Format

  • Roman numerals: I. II. III.
  • Letters: A. B. C. or a. b. c.
  • Arabic numerals: 1. 2. 3.
  • Legal style: 1.1, 1.2, 1.3

Outline Examples

Standard outline:
I. Introduction
    A. Background
    B. Objectives
II. Methodology
    A. Data collection
    B. Analysis procedures

Technical Uses

  • Decimal classification: 1.1, 1.1.1, 1.1.2
  • Version numbers: Software v.2.1
  • Citations: Section 4.2 of the Act

7.08 Periods Properly Omitted

Certain contexts require or allow the omission of periods, particularly in modern usage, headings, and specific types of abbreviations.

Headings and Titles

  • Main headings: No periods needed
  • Subheadings: No periods needed
  • Chapter titles: No periods needed
  • Captions: Periods only if complete sentences

Headings Without Periods

Budget Analysis and Recommendations

Quarterly Revenue Report

Implementation Timeline

Figure 1: Population growth trends

Modern Abbreviations Without Periods

  • Metric units: km, kg, cm, ml, L
  • Acronyms: NATO, UNESCO, RCMP
  • Technical terms: HTML, PDF, URL
  • Postal codes: ON, BC, QC

Lists and Vertical Displays

  • Bullet points: No periods for fragments
  • Short items: No periods needed
  • Parallel structure: Be consistent within each list

Lists Without Periods

Required documents:

  • Valid passport
  • Proof of residence
  • Completed application form
  • Two passport photographs

üí° Consistency Rule

Whatever style you choose for abbreviations and lists, maintain consistency throughout your document.

7.09 Question Marks - Main Purpose

Question marks end direct questions and indicate uncertainty about facts or information in formal writing.

Primary Uses

  • Direct questions: What time is the meeting?
  • Question series: Who will present? When? Where?
  • Uncertain facts: The document is dated 1995(?)
  • Rhetorical questions: Why would anyone oppose this policy?

Direct Questions

Will you attend the conference tomorrow?

How many employees participated in the survey?

What are the projected costs for this initiative?

When should we expect the final report?

Questions in Series

  • Each question gets its own question mark
  • Can be in same sentence or separate sentences
  • Maintain parallel structure when possible

Question Series

The committee must decide: Who will lead? What resources are needed? When should we begin?

Have you reviewed the proposal? Do you have any concerns? Are there recommended changes?

‚ö†Ô∏è Punctuation with Quotation Marks

Question marks go inside quotation marks if the quoted material is a question, outside if the entire sentence is a question.

7.10 Requests, Indirect Questions and Other Uses

Not all question-like sentences use question marks. Requests, indirect questions, and courtesy questions often use periods instead.

Requests Phrased as Questions (Use Periods)

  • Polite requests: Would you please submit the report.
  • Instructions: Could you ensure all forms are signed.
  • Formal requests: May I ask that you review this document.

Indirect Questions (Use Periods)

  • Reported questions: She asked what time the meeting was.
  • Embedded questions: I wonder if the budget is approved.
  • Statement form: The question is whether we should proceed.

Direct vs. Indirect

Direct questions (question marks):

She asked, "What time is the meeting?"

"Are you ready for the presentation?" he inquired.

Indirect questions (periods):

She asked what time the meeting was.

He inquired whether I was ready for the presentation.

Courtesy Questions

  • May be punctuated with periods or question marks
  • Depends on tone and expectation of response
  • Be consistent within your document

Courtesy Questions

Expecting response (question mark):

Would you be available for a meeting next Tuesday?

Polite instruction (period):

Would you please ensure the doors are locked.

üí° Test Method

If you genuinely expect an answer, use a question mark. If you're making a polite request or giving instructions, use a period.

7.11 Exclamation Marks - Main Purpose

Exclamation marks express strong emotion, surprise, emphasis, or urgency, but should be used sparingly in formal government documents.

Appropriate Uses in Formal Writing

  • Safety warnings: Danger! Construction zone ahead!
  • Urgent notices: Deadline extended to Friday!
  • Emergency instructions: Evacuate the building immediately!
  • Strong interjections: Excellent! The project is approved!

Avoid in Formal Documents

  • General enthusiasm or promotional language
  • Routine announcements
  • Multiple exclamation marks (!!!)
  • Sarcasm or irony

Appropriate vs. Inappropriate

Appropriate:

Warning! This equipment requires specialized training.

Stop! Authorized personnel only beyond this point.

The witness exclaimed, "I saw everything clearly!"

Avoid in formal writing:

We're excited to announce our new initiative! ‚ùå

This is a fantastic opportunity!!! ‚ùå

Thanks for your cooperation! ‚ùå

With Other Punctuation

  • Quotation marks: Inside if exclamation is part of quote
  • No double punctuation: Never use period after exclamation mark
  • In series: Each exclamatory element gets its own mark

‚ö†Ô∏è Professional Tone

Exclamation marks can undermine the authoritative tone expected in government communications. Use judiciously and only when genuinely warranted.

7.12 Miscellaneous

Exclamation marks have specialized uses with interjections, emphasis, and in specific grammatical constructions that require careful handling.

Interjections

  • Strong interjections: Oh! Wow! Alas!
  • Mild interjections: Oh, well, yes (use commas)
  • Context determines punctuation: Tone and emphasis matter

Interjection Examples

Strong (exclamation marks):

Oh! I completely forgot about the deadline.

Excellent! The proposal was approved.

Help! The system has crashed again.

Mild (commas):

Well, I suppose that could work.

Oh, I see what you mean now.

Yes, the report is ready for distribution.

Emphatic Statements

  • Commands: Get that report finished now!
  • Strong opinions: What a waste of resources!
  • Surprise: The budget increased by 40%!

Special Constructions

  • Exclamatory phrases: What a difference! How wonderful!
  • Interrupted exclamations: "What a‚Äî" "Let me finish," he said.
  • Vocatives (strong): Citizens! Your vote matters!

Special Cases

Exclamatory questions:

How could this have happened!

What were they thinking!

Ironic statements (use sparingly):

What a brilliant strategy that was!

üí° Tone Test

Read your sentence aloud. If you would naturally raise your voice or express strong emotion, an exclamation mark may be appropriate.

7.13 The Comma - General

The comma is the most frequently used punctuation mark, serving to separate elements within sentences and clarify relationships between ideas.

Primary Functions

  • Separation: Items in series, clauses, phrases
  • Clarification: Preventing misreading and ambiguity
  • Organization: Structuring complex sentences
  • Emphasis: Setting off important information

Essential Comma Rules

  • Series: Always use serial comma before "and" or "or"
  • Independent clauses: Before coordinating conjunctions
  • Introductory elements: After introductory words, phrases, clauses
  • Non-essential information: Around parenthetical elements

Basic Comma Uses

Series: The report covers budget, timeline, and resources.

Independent clauses: The meeting ended early, but we covered all topics.

Introductory: After careful consideration, we approve the proposal.

Non-essential: The manager, who joined last month, leads the project.

Canadian Government Style

  • Serial comma: Mandatory in all government documents
  • Consistency: Apply rules uniformly throughout documents
  • Clarity: When in doubt, use comma to prevent misreading

‚ö†Ô∏è Comma Splices

Don't join independent clauses with commas alone. Use semicolons, periods, or coordinating conjunctions with commas.

üí° Reading Test

Read your sentence aloud. Natural pauses often indicate where commas should go, but always verify with grammatical rules.

7.14 Restrictive/Non-restrictive

Understanding the difference between restrictive and non-restrictive elements is crucial for proper comma usage and clear communication.

Restrictive Elements (No Commas)

  • Essential information: Necessary to identify the noun
  • Cannot be removed: Without changing the basic meaning
  • Usually introduced by "that": The policy that we discussed
  • No commas: Because information is essential

Non-restrictive Elements (Use Commas)

  • Additional information: Adds detail but not essential for identification
  • Can be removed: Without changing core meaning
  • Usually introduced by "which": The policy, which we discussed
  • Set off with commas: Before and after the element

Restrictive vs. Non-restrictive

Restrictive (no commas):

The employee who submitted the report will present tomorrow.

Students that miss the deadline will lose points.

The policy that addresses privacy concerns is under review.

Non-restrictive (with commas):

Sarah Johnson, who submitted the report, will present tomorrow.

The students, who worked hard all semester, deserve recognition.

The new policy, which addresses privacy concerns, takes effect Monday.

That vs. Which

  • "That": Introduces restrictive clauses (no commas)
  • "Which": Introduces non-restrictive clauses (with commas)
  • Helps readers: Immediately understand the clause type

That vs. Which Examples

That (restrictive):

The report that John prepared is excellent.

Which (non-restrictive):

The report, which John prepared, is excellent.

üí° Identification Test

Ask: "Which one?" If the clause answers this question, it's restrictive (no commas). If it just adds extra information, it's non-restrictive (use commas).

7.15 Co-ordinate Elements

Co-ordinate elements are equal in grammatical rank and function. They require specific comma patterns to maintain clarity and parallel structure.

Items in Series

  • Three or more items: Always use serial comma
  • Two items only: No comma needed (unless for clarity)
  • Complex items: May require semicolons instead of commas
  • Parallel structure: Keep items grammatically consistent

Series Examples

Simple series:

The committee reviewed policies, procedures, and guidelines.

We need to hire, train, and deploy new staff members.

The report analyzes costs, benefits, and implementation challenges.

Two items (no comma):

The system tracks income and expenses.

We reviewed policies and procedures.

Co-ordinate Adjectives

  • Equal weight: Each modifies the noun independently
  • Reversible: Can change order without altering meaning
  • Use commas: Between co-ordinate adjectives
  • Test with "and": If "and" works, use comma

Co-ordinate vs. Cumulative Adjectives

Co-ordinate (use commas):

A comprehensive, detailed analysis ‚úì (detailed, comprehensive analysis)

The efficient, cost-effective solution ‚úì (cost-effective, efficient solution)

Cumulative (no commas):

A large government building ‚ùå (government large building doesn't work)

Three small red cars ‚ùå (red small three cars doesn't work)

Compound Sentences

  • Independent clauses: Use comma before coordinating conjunctions
  • FANBOYS: For, and, nor, but, or, yet, so
  • Both clauses complete: Each must have subject and verb

Compound Sentence Examples

The budget was approved, and implementation will begin next month.

We received the proposal, but several concerns need addressing.

The team worked overtime, yet they missed the deadline.

No comma needed (compound predicate):

The team worked overtime and missed the deadline.

(Same subject "team" - not two independent clauses)

üí° Independence Test

For compound sentences, ensure both parts could stand alone as complete sentences before using a comma.

7.16 Clarity and Emphasis

Commas can prevent misreading, add emphasis, and improve clarity even when not strictly required by grammatical rules.

Preventing Misreading

  • Ambiguous phrases: Use commas to clarify meaning
  • Similar words: Separate potentially confusing elements
  • Complex constructions: Guide reader through sentence structure
  • When in doubt: Choose clarity over rigid rule-following

Clarity Examples

Preventing misreading:

Soon after, the manager called a meeting.

(Without comma: "Soon after the manager called...")

Inside, the building was completely renovated.

(Without comma: "Inside the building was...")

To John, Mary seemed very competent.

(Without comma: "To John Mary seemed...")

Emphasis and Pacing

  • Natural pauses: Commas create breathing space
  • Important information: Set off key points
  • Dramatic effect: Use strategically for impact
  • Reader guidance: Help navigate complex ideas

Emphasis Examples

Adding emphasis:

The decision, unfortunately, cannot be reversed.

We must, however, consider all options.

The results were, quite frankly, disappointing.

Controlling pace:

The project was completed on time, within budget, and exceeded expectations.

Slowly, carefully, methodically, she reviewed each document.

Addresses and Dates

  • Geographic elements: Separate city, province, country
  • Full dates: Comma before and after year
  • Postal codes: No comma before postal code

Addresses and Dates

Addresses:

The conference is in Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada.

Send correspondence to Ottawa, Ontario K1A 0A1.

Dates:

The meeting on March 15, 2024, was very productive.

By December 2023 we had completed the project.

‚ö†Ô∏è Overuse Warning

Don't use commas solely for emphasis if they violate grammatical rules. Effective writing provides clarity without excessive punctuation.

7.17 Omitted Words

Commas can indicate omitted words, particularly in parallel constructions where repetition would be awkward or unnecessary.

Elliptical Constructions

  • Parallel structures: Omit repeated verbs or phrases
  • Use commas: To indicate the omission
  • Maintain clarity: Reader should easily understand missing element
  • Consistent pattern: Apply same structure throughout

Omitted Verb Examples

With comma for omitted verb:

The first proposal was accepted; the second, rejected.

(The second [was] rejected)

Some employees prefer morning shifts; others, evening shifts.

(others [prefer] evening shifts)

The manager reviews policies; the director, budgets.

(the director [reviews] budgets)

Comparative Constructions

  • Parallel comparisons: Than/as constructions
  • Omitted elements: Use commas to indicate missing parts
  • Balanced structure: Maintain grammatical parallelism

Comparative Examples

The budget is more important than the timeline, the timeline than the resources.

John was responsible for research; Mary, for analysis; and Tom, for presentation.

In January we hired five employees; in February, three; in March, seven.

Contrasting Elements

  • But/not constructions: Set off contrasting phrases
  • Emphasis on contrast: Highlight differences
  • Clear opposition: Make contrast obvious

Contrasting Examples

We need action, not discussion.

The solution is simple, but effective.

Focus on results, not process.

The policy helps employees, not management.

When Not to Use

  • Unclear omissions: If meaning becomes ambiguous
  • Simple sentences: Don't create unnecessary complexity
  • Different subjects: When subjects don't match

üí° Clarity Check

Always ensure the omitted word is clear from context. If readers might be confused, include the word rather than using elliptical construction.

7.18 Quotations, etc.

Commas have specific rules when used with quotations, dialogue, and attribution that ensure clarity and proper punctuation hierarchy.

Quotations and Attribution

  • Before quotes: Use comma after attribution
  • After quotes: Replace final punctuation with comma
  • Interrupted quotes: Commas around attribution
  • Inside quotation marks: Commas always go inside

Attribution Examples

Attribution before quote:

The minister said, "This policy will benefit all Canadians."

She replied, "We need more time to review the proposal."

Attribution after quote:

"This policy will benefit all Canadians," the minister said.

"We need more time to review the proposal," she replied.

Interrupted quotes:

"This policy," the minister said, "will benefit all Canadians."

"We need more time," she replied, "to review the proposal thoroughly."

Comma Placement Rules

  • Always inside: Commas go inside quotation marks
  • Replace periods: When quote continues with attribution
  • Keep other marks: Question marks and exclamation points stay

Punctuation Examples

Comma inside quotes:

"The meeting is tomorrow," she announced.

He said, "Please submit the report," and left the room.

Questions and exclamations:

"Are you ready?" she asked.

"That's excellent!" he exclaimed.

"When will this end?" the employee wondered.

Dialogue Formatting

  • New paragraphs: Each speaker gets new paragraph
  • Action tags: Use commas with speech tags
  • Continuous speech: No comma if speech continues

Dialogue Examples

"Have you reviewed the budget?" asked the director.

"Yes, I have several concerns," replied the manager.

"Please outline them in writing." The director made a note.

"I'll have the analysis ready by tomorrow morning."

Integrated Quotations

  • No comma needed: When quote flows grammatically
  • Partial quotes: Often integrate without punctuation
  • Natural flow: Read sentence aloud to test

Integrated Examples

The report describes the results as "highly encouraging."

According to the minister, the policy "will benefit all citizens."

She believes we should "focus on practical solutions."

‚ö†Ô∏è Consistency in Dialogue

Maintain consistent punctuation patterns throughout dialogue sections. Don't mix attribution styles within the same conversation.

7.19 The Semicolon

The semicolon creates a stronger break than a comma but weaker than a period, primarily linking closely related independent clauses.

Primary Uses

  • Independent clauses: Link closely related complete thoughts
  • Complex lists: Separate items containing internal commas
  • Conjunctive adverbs: Before however, therefore, consequently
  • Balance and emphasis: Create deliberate pauses

Independent Clauses

The budget was approved; implementation will begin immediately.

The committee met for three hours; they reached unanimous agreement.

Revenue increased significantly; expenses remained stable.

The proposal has merit; however, timing is problematic.

When NOT to Use Semicolons

  • Dependent clauses: Use commas instead
  • Simple lists: Use commas for simple items
  • After colons: Don't follow colons with semicolons
  • Unrelated ideas: Use periods for separate thoughts

Complex Lists

The conference attendees included Dr. Sarah Johnson, University of Toronto; Mr. Paul Chen, Statistics Canada; and Ms. Lisa Brown, Treasury Board Secretariat.

Regional offices are located in Vancouver, British Columbia; Calgary, Alberta; and Halifax, Nova Scotia.

üí° Independence Test

Both parts of a semicolon-separated sentence should be able to stand alone as complete sentences.

7.20 Semicolons with Conjunctive Adverbs

Conjunctive adverbs require semicolons when connecting independent clauses, creating smooth transitions between related ideas.

Common Conjunctive Adverbs

  • Contrast: however, nevertheless, nonetheless, conversely, still
  • Addition: furthermore, moreover, additionally, besides, also
  • Result: therefore, consequently, thus, accordingly, hence
  • Example: for example, for instance, namely, specifically

Punctuation Pattern

  • Semicolon before: Independent clause; conjunctive adverb
  • Comma after: Conjunctive adverb, independent clause
  • Both clauses complete: Each must stand alone

Conjunctive Adverb Examples

Contrast:

The proposal was well-received; however, budget constraints limit implementation.

Sales increased this quarter; nevertheless, profits remained flat.

Addition:

The report is comprehensive; furthermore, it includes actionable recommendations.

Training is mandatory; moreover, certification is required annually.

Result:

Costs exceeded projections; therefore, we must revise the budget.

The system failed repeatedly; consequently, we upgraded the hardware.

Longer Transitional Phrases

  • On the other hand: Shows contrast
  • As a result: Shows consequence
  • In addition: Shows addition
  • For this reason: Shows causation

Transitional Phrase Examples

The policy benefits employees; on the other hand, it increases administrative costs.

Revenue declined significantly; as a result, we must reduce expenses.

The team worked diligently; for this reason, they completed the project early.

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Confuse with Coordinating Conjunctions

Coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, so) use commas, not semicolons: "The budget was approved, but implementation is delayed."

7.21 The Colon

The colon introduces, explains, or emphasizes what follows, creating anticipation and directing attention to important information.

Primary Functions

  • Introduce lists: Following complete independent clauses
  • Introduce explanations: Clarify or elaborate on preceding statement
  • Introduce quotations: Formal quotations and dialogue
  • Express ratios: Mathematical and statistical expressions

Essential Rule

  • Complete clause before colon: Must be independent sentence
  • Not after verbs alone: "The items are:" is incorrect
  • Not after prepositions: "Including:" is incorrect
  • Complete thought required: Should make sense as standalone sentence

Correct Colon Usage

Introducing lists:

Please bring the following items: passport, insurance card, and completed application.

The committee reviewed three options: merger, acquisition, or strategic partnership.

Introducing explanations:

The solution is clear: increase funding and extend the deadline.

The result was predictable: costs exceeded the budget by 25 percent.

Time expressions:

The meeting starts at 9:30 a.m.

Office hours are 8:00 a.m. to 4:30 p.m.

Incorrect usage:

Please bring: passport, insurance, and application. ‚ùå

The items include: forms, documents, and fees. ‚ùå

We need: more time, more money, and more staff. ‚ùå

Capitalization After Colons

  • Capitalize: Complete sentences following colons
  • Capitalize: Formal statements and rules
  • Don't capitalize: Single words, phrases, or lists
  • Be consistent: Within the same document

7.22 Colons in Titles and References

Colons serve specialized functions in titles, citations, and reference materials, following specific formatting conventions.

Titles and Subtitles

  • Main title: Subtitle format: Space before and after colon
  • Capitalize subtitle: First word of subtitle is capitalized
  • Books and reports: Government publications use this format
  • Article titles: Academic and professional writing

Title Examples

Government reports:

Budget 2024: Building a Stronger Economy

Climate Action: Canada's Path to Net Zero Emissions

Digital Government: Transforming Service Delivery

Academic articles:

"Public Policy Analysis: Methods and Applications"

"Employee Engagement: Measuring Organizational Success"

Biblical and Legal Citations

  • Chapter and verse: Genesis 1:1, Matthew 5:3-12
  • Legal references: Section numbers, subsections
  • Academic citations: Volume and page numbers

Ratios and Proportions

  • Mathematical ratios: 3:1, 5:2 ratio
  • Statistical data: Population ratios, survey results
  • Financial ratios: Cost-benefit analysis
  • No spaces: Around the colon in ratios

Specialized Uses

Ratios:

The vote was 3:1 in favour of the motion.

The cost-benefit ratio improved to 1:4.

Business correspondence:

Dear Minister Johnson:

To: All Department Heads

Re: Budget Review Process

References:

See Journal of Public Administration 45:3 (2023)

Employment Standards Act, section 12:4

üí° Consistency in Documents

Choose one format for titles and references and use it consistently throughout your document or publication series.

7.23 Quotation Marks

Quotation marks enclose direct quotations, indicate titles of short works, and mark words used in special senses or for emphasis.

Primary Uses

  • Direct quotations: Exact words spoken or written
  • Titles of short works: Articles, chapters, songs, poems
  • Special terminology: Technical terms, jargon, slang
  • Words as words: Discussing language itself

Quotation Mark Hierarchy

  • Double marks (" "): Primary quotations
  • Single marks (' '): Quotations within quotations
  • Alternating pattern: Continue alternating for nested quotes

Direct Quotations

Simple quotations:

The minister announced, "The policy takes effect immediately."

"We must address this issue," the director emphasized.

According to the report, the results were "highly encouraging."

Quotations within quotations:

She reported, "The witness said, 'I saw everything clearly.'"

The article stated, "The Prime Minister declared, 'We will not compromise on this issue.'"

Titles in Quotation Marks

  • Articles: Newspaper, magazine, journal articles
  • Chapters: Individual chapters in books
  • Short stories: Individual stories in collections
  • Poems: Individual poems (unless book-length)
  • Songs: Individual song titles

Title Examples

The article "Government Reform in the Digital Age" appeared in Canadian Public Administration.

Chapter 5, "Implementation Strategies," provides detailed guidance.

The poem "In Flanders Fields" commemorates Canadian soldiers.

Special Uses

  • Technical terms: First use or definition
  • Irony or skepticism: "Temporary" tax increases
  • Non-standard usage: Colloquialisms in formal writing
  • Words as linguistic examples: The word "government" has many meanings

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid Overuse

Don't use quotation marks for emphasis or to highlight ordinary words. This practice can appear unprofessional in government documents.

7.24 Punctuation with Quotation Marks

The relationship between quotation marks and other punctuation follows specific rules that ensure consistency and professional appearance.

Always Inside Quotation Marks

  • Periods: Always inside, regardless of logic
  • Commas: Always inside, regardless of logic
  • Canadian/American style: Follows typographical convention

Always Outside Quotation Marks

  • Semicolons: Always outside
  • Colons: Always outside
  • Logic-based: These marks relate to the sentence structure

Periods and Commas

Periods inside:

She said, "The meeting is tomorrow."

The report concludes, "Implementation should begin immediately."

Commas inside:

"The meeting is tomorrow," she announced.

The terms "effective," "efficient," and "economical" guide our decisions.

Semicolons and Colons

Semicolons outside:

He called it "urgent"; I disagreed completely.

The policy is described as "comprehensive"; however, it lacks detail.

Colons outside:

She mentioned three "priorities": budget, timeline, and quality.

The report identifies several "challenges": funding, staffing, and coordination.

Context-Dependent Punctuation

  • Question marks: Inside if quote is question, outside if sentence is
  • Exclamation points: Inside if quote is exclamatory, outside if sentence is
  • No double punctuation: Never use two end marks together

Question Marks and Exclamation Points

Question mark inside (quote is question):

She asked, "When will the project be completed?"

Question mark outside (sentence is question):

Did she really say "The project is impossible"?

Both are questions (use one mark):

Did she ask, "When will this be finished?"

Exclamation examples:

He shouted, "The building is on fire!"

What a ridiculous "solution"!

üí° Memory Device

Periods and commas are "friends" with quotation marks (always together inside). Semicolons and colons are "independent" (stay outside).

7.29 Parentheses - General

Parentheses, or round brackets, are used to enclose additional information serving to explain, amplify or provide comments on adjacent material.

When to Use Parentheses

  • Supplementary information: Less closely related to the main sentence
  • Explanatory material: Background details or clarifications
  • Internal punctuation: When the parenthetic element contains commas or other marks
  • De-emphasis: To downplay the importance of information

Basic Usage

The budget proposal (submitted last month) was approved by the committee.

Three departments (Transport, Finance, and Health) participated in the review.

The new policy will affect all employees (approximately 2,500 people) starting in January.

Parentheses vs. Other Punctuation

  • Commas: For closely related information
  • Dashes: For emphasis or dramatic effect
  • Parentheses: For less important, supplementary details

7.30 Clarification

Parentheses can solve punctuation problems and prevent confusion, particularly when multiple types of commas might appear in the same sentence.

Avoiding Comma Confusion

When apposition commas and enumeration commas appear together, parentheses provide clarity:

Problem Solving Examples

Clear with parentheses:

Carla Tavares (a recent MBA graduate), Lisa Thompson and three students attended the meeting.

Confusing without parentheses:

Carla Tavares, a recent MBA graduate, Lisa Thompson and three students attended the meeting.

More Clarification Uses

The three main issues (budget, timeline, and resources) were addressed separately.

Several provinces (Ontario, Quebec, and British Columbia) have implemented similar policies.

The report covers two fiscal years (2022-23 and 2023-24) in detail.

üí° Readability Test

The sentence should make complete sense even if you remove the parenthetical information entirely.

7.31 Punctuation with Parentheses

Specific rules govern how punctuation marks interact with parentheses, depending on whether the parenthetical content is a complete sentence or fragment.

Complete Sentence in Parentheses

  • Mid-sentence: No initial capital, no final period
  • Between sentences: Initial capital and final period
  • Standalone: Full punctuation required

Complete Sentences

Mid-sentence (no capital, no period):

To achieve the best results, adopt a combination of the CPM and PERT methods (this approach has proven effective in similar projects) and monitor progress carefully.

Between sentences (capital and period):

To achieve the best possible results, adopt a combination of the CPM and PERT methods. (See a model of such a combination in the attached paper.) This will provide you with an effective, low-cost control mechanism.

Other Punctuation Rules

  • Before opening: No punctuation unless for enumeration
  • After closing: Use any punctuation that would normally appear
  • Before closing: Only period, question mark, exclamation mark, or quotation marks

Punctuation Patterns

No punctuation before opening:

I am (I hope) reliably informed that a new president has been appointed.

Punctuation after closing:

I am (I hope), always have been and always will be an honest judge.

Question mark before closing:

I have always been willing (do you not agree?) to hear both sides of the issue.

7.32 Afterthoughts and Asides

Parentheses de-emphasize the words they contain, making them ideal for afterthoughts, asides, and supplementary comments.

Characteristics of Asides

  • De-emphasis: Information is downplayed, not highlighted
  • Supplementary: Adds context but isn't essential
  • Conversational: Often reflects the writer's commentary
  • Flow preservation: Sentence works without the parenthetical content

Afterthoughts and Commentary

The premier (no mean orator himself) was enthusiastic in his praise of the minister's speech.

The policy will be reviewed annually (assuming budget permits) to ensure continued effectiveness.

Three candidates applied for the position (all with excellent qualifications) but only one could be selected.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Afterthoughts

If an afterthought is truly important, consider using a dash or other punctuation instead of parentheses to give it proper emphasis.

Important (use dash):
Finally the Computer Operations Branch agreed to follow through on the auditor's recommendations‚Äîwhich is what it should have done six months earlier if it had had the best interests of the organization at heart.

Special Uses

Speaker identification in transcripts:

The Hon. John Manley (Minister of Industry): Mr. Speaker, I support this initiative.

Additional context:

The meeting (which lasted three hours) concluded without reaching a consensus.

7.33 Nested Parentheses

When parenthetical material appears within other parenthetical material, use square brackets or a combination of parentheses and dashes.

Options for Nested Parentheses

  • Square brackets inside: Round brackets [square brackets]
  • Dash combination: Use em dashes with parentheses
  • Avoid when possible: Consider rewriting for clarity
  • Legal/scholarly exception: More nested punctuation is acceptable

Square Brackets Method

Acadia (from Alqonquin [dwelling here and there]) was the first French colony in North America.

The regulation (see section 14[1][c]) requires annual reporting.

In equation f(x) [see S. 8.3] reaches a maximum when x = 0.

Dash Combination Method

He worked hard‚Äîtwelve hours a day (and no bonus for overtime), seven days a week‚Äîuntil the task was completed.

The new policy‚Äîeffective immediately (pending final approval)‚Äîwill streamline the application process.

üí° Readability First

If you find yourself needing complex nested parentheses, consider rewriting the sentence for better readability. Break long sentences into shorter ones when possible.

Legal Document Style

Acceptable in legal contexts:

The amount owing (one thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine [1999] dollars) shall be paid within thirty (30) days.
