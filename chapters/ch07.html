7.01 Introduction

Punctuation marks are the traffic signals of writing. They tell readers when to pause, stop, or change direction. Proper punctuation ensures your meaning is clear and your writing flows smoothly.

Key Principle

Punctuation should enhance clarity and meaning. When in doubt, choose the punctuation that makes your sentence easier to understand.

Functions of Punctuation

  • Separate ideas - Distinguish between different thoughts
  • Indicate pauses - Show where readers should pause
  • Signal relationships - Show how ideas connect
  • Clarify meaning - Prevent misunderstandings
  • Express tone - Convey questions, exclamations, etc.

Punctuation Impact on Meaning

Without punctuation: "Let's eat grandma"

With comma: "Let's eat, grandma"

A comma can save lives!

7.02 Periods

The period is the most basic punctuation mark, indicating the end of a complete statement.

Uses of Periods

  • End declarative sentences - Complete statements
  • End imperative sentences - Commands or requests
  • Abbreviations - Dr., Mr., etc.
  • Decimal numbers - 3.14, $25.99
  • Initials - J.R.R. Tolkien

Period Examples

✓ Correct:

  • The meeting starts at 2 p.m.
  • Please submit your report by Friday.
  • Dr. Smith will attend the conference.

✗ Incorrect:

  • The meeting starts at 2 p.m.. (double period)
  • Will you attend the meeting. (question needs question mark)

💡 Period Tips

  • Only one period at the end of a sentence, even after abbreviations
  • Use periods with most abbreviations in formal writing
  • No period after headings or titles

7.03 Commas

Commas are the most frequently used—and misused—punctuation mark. They separate elements within sentences and indicate brief pauses.

Main Uses of Commas

  • Separate items in a series - apples, oranges, and bananas
  • Before coordinating conjunctions - and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet
  • After introductory elements - After the meeting, we'll discuss the results
  • Around non-essential information - The report, which was filed yesterday, is ready
  • Between coordinate adjectives - a long, difficult project
  • In addresses and dates - Vancouver, BC or June 15, 2024

Comma Rules in Action

Series (Oxford Comma)

✓ Canadian style: We need pens, paper, and ink.

✗ Avoid: We need pens, paper and ink. (missing Oxford comma)

Coordinating Conjunctions

✓ Correct: The project is complete, but we need to review it.

✗ Incorrect: The project is complete but we need to review it.

Restrictive vs. Non-restrictive

✓ Non-restrictive: My brother, who lives in Toronto, is visiting.

✓ Restrictive: The employee who submitted the report will present it.

⚠️ Common Comma Errors

  • Comma splice: Joining independent clauses with just a comma
  • Missing comma: Before coordinating conjunctions in compound sentences
  • Unnecessary commas: Around essential information

7.04 Semicolons

Semicolons are stronger than commas but weaker than periods. They connect closely related independent clauses and separate complex list items.

Uses of Semicolons

  • Between independent clauses - The meeting was productive; we accomplished all our goals
  • Before transitional expressions - however, therefore, nevertheless, etc.
  • In complex lists - When items contain commas
  • In compound sentences - When no coordinating conjunction is used

Semicolon Examples

✓ Connecting clauses: The project is due tomorrow; I'll work late tonight.

✓ With transitions: The weather was terrible; however, we still went hiking.

✓ Complex lists: We visited Vancouver, BC; Montreal, QC; and Halifax, NS.

✗ Incorrect: Although it was raining; we went for a walk. (dependent clause)

💡 Semicolon Test

If you can replace the semicolon with a period and create two complete sentences, you're using it correctly.

7.05 Colons

Colons introduce information that explains, illustrates, or elaborates on what comes before them.

Uses of Colons

  • Introduce lists - We need three items: pens, paper, and files
  • Introduce explanations - The reason is simple: we ran out of time
  • Introduce quotations - He said: "The meeting is postponed"
  • In time expressions - 2:30 p.m.
  • In ratios - 3:1 ratio
  • After salutations - Dear Mr. Smith: (formal letters)

Colon Examples

✓ Introducing lists: Please bring the following: your passport, tickets, and hotel confirmations.

✓ Explanations: The deadline was missed for one reason: poor planning.

✗ Incorrect: The team includes: John, Mary, and Bob. (incomplete thought before colon)

✓ Corrected: The team includes the following members: John, Mary, and Bob.

Capitalization After Colons

  • Lowercase: When introducing a list or phrase
  • Uppercase: When introducing a complete sentence or formal quotation

Remember this rule: Colons introduce important information.

We have three options: accept, reject, or postpone the proposal.

7.08 Apostrophes

Apostrophes show possession and mark contractions. They're often misused, so understanding the rules is essential.

Apostrophe Rules

  • Possession (singular): Add 's - the cat's toy, James's book
  • Possession (plural ending in s): Add ' - the cats' toys, the workers' union
  • Possession (plural not ending in s): Add 's - the children's books
  • Contractions: Replace missing letters - don't, won't, it's
  • Time/distance expressions: one day's work, five dollars' worth

Apostrophe Examples

Type Correct Incorrect
Singular possession The manager's office The managers office
Plural possession The managers' meeting The manager's meeting
Contractions It's a good idea Its a good idea
Possessive pronoun The decision is yours The decision is your's

⚠️ Common Apostrophe Errors

  • Its vs. It's: "Its" is possessive; "It's" means "it is"
  • Plurals: Never use apostrophes for simple plurals (1990s, not 1990's)
  • Possessive pronouns: yours, hers, ours, theirs (no apostrophes)

7.12 Dashes

Dashes are versatile punctuation marks that can add emphasis, show interruption, or set off explanatory information.

Types of Dashes

  • Em dash (—): Longer dash for breaks in thought, emphasis
  • En dash (–): Shorter dash for ranges, connections
  • Hyphen (-): Shortest, for compound words

Dash Usage

Em Dashes (—)

✓ Emphasis: The solution is simple—work harder.

✓ Interruption: "I think we should—" "No, that won't work."

✓ Explanation: The team—our best performers—will handle this project.

En Dashes (–)

✓ Ranges: pages 15–30, 9:00 a.m.–5:00 p.m.

✓ Connections: Toronto–Montreal flight, cost–benefit analysis

💡 Dash Tips

  • Use em dashes sparingly—too many can make writing choppy
  • No spaces around em dashes in Canadian style
  • Use en dashes for ranges, not hyphens
  • Don't mix dashes with other punctuation unnecessarily
