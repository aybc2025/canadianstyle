Punctuation

Master all forms of punctuation for clear, professional writing

7.01 Introduction

Punctuation marks are the traffic signals of writing. They tell readers when to pause, stop, or change direction. Proper punctuation ensures your meaning is clear and your writing flows smoothly.

Key Principle

Punctuation should enhance clarity and meaning. When in doubt, choose the option that makes your text clearest to readers.

General Guidelines

  • Clarity first: Punctuation should clarify meaning
  • Consistency: Use punctuation consistently throughout your document
  • Open style: Omit optional punctuation when it doesn't affect clarity
  • Simplicity: If a sentence is hard to punctuate, consider rewriting it

7.02 Periods

The period marks the end of declarative sentences and provides the strongest stop in punctuation.

Main Uses

  1. End of sentences: Complete declarative statements
  2. Abbreviations: Dr., etc., i.e.
  3. Mild imperatives: Please submit your report by Friday.
  4. Indirect questions: She asked when the meeting would start.

Examples

✓ Declarative: The committee will meet next Tuesday.

✓ Mild imperative: Please review the attached document.

✓ Indirect question: He wondered whether the proposal would be accepted.

✗ Avoid after strong imperatives: Stop! (use exclamation mark)

✗ Avoid after direct questions: What time is it? (use question mark)

Periods with Abbreviations

Use periods with most abbreviations, but modern style often omits them from well-known organizations and units of measurement.

With periods: Dr. Smith, etc., i.e., Mr., Ms.

Without periods: CBC, NATO, UNESCO, kg, cm, PhD

7.03 Commas

Commas provide pauses within sentences and separate elements for clarity. They are the most frequently used punctuation mark.

Essential Comma Rules

  1. Series: Separate items in a list of three or more
  2. Independent clauses: Before coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or)
  3. Non-restrictive elements: Around additional information
  4. Introductory elements: After phrases that begin sentences
  5. Direct address: Around names when addressing someone

Series (Oxford Comma)

✓ Recommended: We ordered coffee, tea, and juice.

✓ Also acceptable: We ordered coffee, tea and juice.

Note: Use the Oxford comma (final comma) for clarity and consistency in government writing.

Restrictive vs. Non-restrictive

✓ Restrictive (no commas): The report that was submitted yesterday needs revision.

✓ Non-restrictive (with commas): The report, which was submitted yesterday, needs revision.

Restrictive: Essential information that identifies which specific item

Non-restrictive: Additional information that could be removed

Common Comma Errors

✗ Comma splice: The meeting is scheduled for Monday, we should prepare the agenda.

✓ Corrected: The meeting is scheduled for Monday, so we should prepare the agenda.

✗ Unnecessary comma: The manager, and the director attended.

✓ Corrected: The manager and the director attended.

7.04 Semicolons

Semicolons provide a pause stronger than a comma but weaker than a period. They join related independent clauses and separate complex list items.

Main Uses

  1. Join independent clauses: When clauses are closely related
  2. Before conjunctive adverbs: however, therefore, nevertheless
  3. Complex series: When items contain internal punctuation

Joining Independent Clauses

✓ Related ideas: The budget was approved; implementation begins next month.

✓ With adverbs: The project was delayed; however, we expect completion by December.

Complex Series

✓ Complex list: The conference includes participants from Ottawa, Ontario; Vancouver, British Columbia; and Halifax, Nova Scotia.

Use semicolons to separate items when the items themselves contain commas.

Common Semicolon Errors

✗ Before coordinating conjunctions: The report is ready; and we can distribute it.

✓ Use comma instead: The report is ready, and we can distribute it.

✗ With dependent clauses: Because the meeting was cancelled; we postponed the decision.

✓ Use comma instead: Because the meeting was cancelled, we postponed the decision.

7.05 Colons

Colons introduce lists, explanations, quotations, and amplifications. They signal that something follows.

Main Uses

  1. Introduce lists: After complete sentences
  2. Introduce explanations: Clarifying or expanding information
  3. Introduce quotations: Formal or long quotations
  4. Between independent clauses: When the second explains the first

Introducing Lists

✓ Complete sentence: The package includes the following items: manual, software, and warranty card.

✗ Incomplete sentence: The package includes: manual, software, and warranty card.

✓ Corrected: The package includes manual, software, and warranty card.

Explanations and Amplifications

✓ Explanation: The decision was unanimous: all members voted in favour.

✓ Amplification: The solution is simple: increase the budget allocation.

Capitalization After Colons

  • Lowercase: When introducing a list or phrase
  • Uppercase: When introducing a complete sentence

List: We need three items: paper, pens, and folders.

Sentence: Remember this rule: Clarity always comes first.

7.06 Question Marks

Question marks end direct questions and indicate uncertainty about facts.

Main Uses

  1. Direct questions: Questions that expect an answer
  2. Rhetorical questions: Questions for effect
  3. Uncertainty: Doubtful facts (in parentheses)
  4. Series of questions: Related short questions

Direct Questions

✓ Standard: When will the report be completed?

✓ Rhetorical: How can we improve service delivery?

✓ Series: Who will attend? When? Where?

NOT Used With

✗ Indirect questions: She asked when the meeting would start?

✓ Corrected: She asked when the meeting would start.

✗ Polite requests: Would you please submit the report by Friday?

✓ Corrected: Would you please submit the report by Friday.

Question Marks with Quotations

  • Inside quotes: When the quotation is a question
  • Outside quotes: When your sentence is a question

Quotation is question: He asked, "Will you attend the meeting?"

Sentence is question: Did he say "I'll attend the meeting"?

7.07 Exclamation Marks

Exclamation marks express strong emotion, surprise, or emphasis. Use them sparingly in professional writing.

Appropriate Uses

  1. Strong commands: Stop! Wait!
  2. Genuine surprise: What an unexpected result!
  3. Strong emotions: Congratulations on your promotion!
  4. Interjections: Oh! Alas!

Professional Writing

✓ Appropriate: Congratulations! The project was a complete success.

✓ Emergency: Evacuate immediately!

✗ Overuse: The meeting was great! Everyone participated! We achieved our goals!

✓ Better: The meeting was highly successful. Everyone participated actively, and we achieved our goals.

Guidelines for Government Writing

  • Use sparingly: Reserve for genuine emphasis
  • Avoid in formal documents: Reports, policies, procedures
  • Consider alternatives: Strong word choice instead of punctuation
  • Never multiple: Don't use !!! or ?!

Exclamation Marks with Quotations

Same rules as question marks apply:

Quotation has exclamation: She shouted, "Stop the process!"

Sentence has exclamation: I can't believe he said "maybe"!

7.08 Apostrophes

Apostrophes show possession and mark contractions. They are often misused but follow clear rules.

Possession Rules

  1. Singular nouns: Add 's (even if ending in s)
  2. Plural nouns ending in s: Add ' only
  3. Plural nouns not ending in s: Add 's
  4. Joint possession: Add 's to the last noun only
  5. Individual possession: Add 's to each noun

Singular Possession

✓ Regular: the manager's office, the cat's toy

✓ Ending in s: James's report, the boss's decision

✓ Alternative: James' report (also acceptable)

Plural Possession

✓ Regular plurals: the managers' meeting, the cats' toys

✓ Irregular plurals: the children's books, the women's committee

Joint vs. Individual Possession

✓ Joint: John and Mary's project (they share one project)

✓ Individual: John's and Mary's projects (each has their own)

Contractions

Apostrophes replace omitted letters in contractions:

Common contractions: don't, can't, won't, it's, you're, they're

Common Apostrophe Errors

✗ Its vs. it's: The committee reviewed it's findings.

✓ Corrected: The committee reviewed its findings.

Remember: it's = it is; its = possessive

✗ Plural confusion: The manager's are attending.

✓ Corrected: The managers are attending.

7.09 Parentheses & Brackets

Parentheses and brackets set off additional information from the main text. Each serves specific purposes.

Parentheses ( ) Uses

  1. Additional information: Non-essential details
  2. Explanations: Clarifying terms or acronyms
  3. References: Citations or cross-references
  4. Asides: Comments from the author

Parentheses Examples

✓ Explanation: The CBC (Canadian Broadcasting Corporation) reported the news.

✓ Additional info: The meeting (scheduled for 2:00 p.m.) was postponed.

✓ Reference: See the attached report (Appendix A) for details.

Square Brackets [ ] Uses

  1. Within quotations: Added clarifications
  2. Within parentheses: Nested information
  3. Editorial comments: [sic], [emphasis added]

Square Brackets Examples

✓ In quotations: "The Prime Minister [Justin Trudeau] announced the policy."

✓ Editorial: "The goverment [sic] will respond tomorrow."

✓ Nested: (The report [see page 5] contains the data.)

Punctuation with Parentheses

  • Comma after: When parentheses end mid-sentence
  • Period inside: When parentheses contain a complete sentence
  • Period outside: When parentheses contain part of a sentence

Mid-sentence: The report (dated March 15), was comprehensive.

Complete sentence: (The meeting was very productive.)

Partial sentence: The meeting was productive (lasting three hours).

7.10 Ellipsis

Ellipsis points (...) indicate omitted material in quotations and unfinished thoughts. Use them carefully to maintain meaning.

Main Uses

  1. Omitted words: From the middle of quotations
  2. Omitted sentences: From beginning or end of quotations
  3. Trailing off: Unfinished thoughts (informal writing)
  4. Deliberate pauses: For dramatic effect (rare)

Omissions in Quotations

Original: "The government of Canada will implement new policies to address climate change and economic recovery in the coming year."

✓ With ellipsis: "The government of Canada will implement new policies ... in the coming year."

Ellipsis Formatting

  • Three dots: For omissions within sentences
  • Four dots: When omission includes end of sentence (period + three dots)
  • Spacing: Space before and after ellipsis
  • No brackets: Don't use [...] unless existing in original

Correct Ellipsis Usage

✓ Mid-sentence: "The committee reviewed ... and approved the proposal."

✓ End of sentence: "The meeting was productive...." (Four dots)

✓ Beginning omitted: "... the final decision will be announced tomorrow."

Important Guidelines

  • Preserve meaning: Don't change the original intent
  • Use sparingly: Only when necessary for brevity
  • Formal writing: Avoid for trailing thoughts
  • Complete sentences: Maintain grammatical structure

7.11 Quotation Marks

Quotation marks set off direct speech, quotations, and titles of short works. They must be used in pairs and follow specific punctuation rules.

Main Uses

  1. Direct quotations: Exact words spoken or written
  2. Dialogue: Conversations in narrative
  3. Titles: Articles, chapters, short stories, poems
  4. Special terms: Irony, technical terms, slang

Direct Quotations

✓ Complete statement: The minister said, "We will address this issue immediately."

✓ Partial quotation: The minister promised to address the issue "immediately."

✓ Question within: She asked, "When will the project be completed?"

Punctuation with Quotation Marks (Canadian Style)

  • Periods and commas: Outside quotation marks
  • Question and exclamation marks: Inside if part of quotation, outside if not
  • Semicolons and colons: Always outside

Canadian Punctuation Style

✓ Period outside: The report mentions "significant progress".

✓ Comma outside: "The meeting is scheduled for tomorrow", she said.

✓ Question inside: He asked, "Are you ready?"

✓ Question outside: Did she say "maybe"?

Titles in Quotation Marks

Use quotation marks for shorter works; italics for longer works:

Quotation marks: "The Road Not Taken" (poem), "Chapter 5" (chapter)

Italics: The Globe and Mail (newspaper), To Kill a Mockingbird (book)

Single vs. Double Quotation Marks

✓ Primary: "The minister said the policy is 'innovative and necessary'."

✓ Headlines: PM Says Policy is 'Game-Changer'

Note: Use single quotes within double quotes, or in headlines for space.

7.12 Dashes

Dashes provide breaks in text stronger than commas but less final than periods. There are three types: em dash, en dash, and hyphen.

Em Dash (—) Uses

  1. Sudden breaks: Interruptions in thought
  2. Emphasis: Setting off important information
  3. Attribution: After quotations
  4. Lists: Alternative to bullets

Em Dash Examples

✓ Interruption: The budget—despite opposition—was approved.

✓ Emphasis: Only one solution remains—immediate action.

✓ Attribution: "Excellence is not a skill, it's an attitude."—Ralph Marston

En Dash (–) Uses

  1. Number ranges: Pages, dates, times
  2. Connections: Equal relationships
  3. Compound adjectives: With proper nouns

En Dash Examples

✓ Number ranges: pages 15–25, 2020–2025, 9:00 a.m.–5:00 p.m.

✓ Connections: Ottawa–Toronto flight, Liberal–NDP coalition

✓ Compound: post–World War II era, New York–based company

Hyphen (-) Uses

  1. Compound words: Well-known, twenty-one
  2. Prefixes: Before capitalized words
  3. Word breaks: At line endings
  4. Avoiding confusion: Re-creation vs. recreation

Hyphen Examples

✓ Compound words: well-established practice, twenty-first century

✓ Prefixes: pre-Confederation, anti-American, co-worker

✓ Clarity: re-sign (sign again) vs. resign (quit)

Typing Dashes

  • Em dash: Alt + 0151 (Windows) or Option + Shift + Hyphen (Mac)
  • En dash: Alt + 0150 (Windows) or Option + Hyphen (Mac)
  • Alternative: Use two hyphens -- for em dash in plain text

Spacing with Dashes

  • Em dashes: No spaces around (Canadian style)
  • En dashes: No spaces around
  • Hyphens: No spaces around
