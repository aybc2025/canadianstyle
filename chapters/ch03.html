3.01 Introduction

Canadian spelling combines British and American influences, creating a unique system that reflects Canada's linguistic heritage. Government writers must be consistent and follow established Canadian conventions.

Key Principle

When in doubt about spelling, consult Canadian dictionaries first. The Canadian Oxford Dictionary and Gage Canadian Dictionary are authoritative sources for Canadian spelling.

Canadian Spelling Characteristics

  • British influence: colour, flavour, honour (not color, flavor, honor)
  • British influence: centre, theatre (not center, theater)
  • American influence: organization, realize (not organisation, realise)
  • American influence: tire, curb (not tyre, kerb)
  • Mixed usage: Both forms acceptable in some cases

Canadian vs. British vs. American

Canadian British American
colour colour color
centre centre center
organize organise organize
tire tyre tire
cheque cheque check

⚠️ Consistency is Essential

Choose one spelling system and use it consistently throughout your document. Don't mix Canadian and American spellings in the same piece.

3.02 Canadian Spelling

Canadian spelling follows specific patterns that distinguish it from both British and American conventions. Understanding these patterns helps ensure consistency in government documents.

-our endings (British pattern)

Canadian spelling retains the British '-our' endings in most words:

  • colour (not color)
  • flavour (not flavor)
  • honour (not honor)
  • labour (not labor)
  • neighbour (not neighbor)
  • rumour (not rumor)
  • humour (not humor)

Government Usage Examples

✓ Canadian spelling:

  • The Minister of Labour announced new policies
  • Honour guards attended the ceremony
  • The government favoured the new proposal
  • Neighbourhood consultations will begin

-re endings (British pattern)

Canadian spelling uses '-re' endings for certain words:

  • centre (not center)
  • theatre (not theater)
  • metre (not meter)
  • fibre (not fiber)
  • sombre (not somber)

-ize endings (American pattern)

Unlike British spelling, Canadian uses '-ize' not '-ise':

  • organize (not organise)
  • realize (not realise)
  • recognize (not recognise)
  • emphasize (not emphasise)
  • summarize (not summarise)

Exception: Some words always use -ise

Always -ise (never -ize):

  • advertise
  • compromise
  • exercise
  • supervise
  • surprise

Other Canadian Preferences

  • cheque (not check for bank instrument)
  • grey (not gray)
  • doughnut (not donut)
  • catalogue (not catalog)
  • defence (not defense)

💡 Memory Aids

For -our words: Think "Canadian honour" - we keep the 'u' for tradition.
For -ize words: Think "organize" - we follow the 'z' pattern like Americans.

3.03 Spelling Variants

Some words have acceptable alternative spellings in Canadian English. In government writing, choose one form and use it consistently throughout your document.

Acceptable Variants

These words have two acceptable Canadian spellings:

  • adviser/advisor (both acceptable)
  • doughnut/donut (doughnut preferred)
  • enrol/enroll (enrol preferred)
  • grey/gray (grey preferred)
  • jail/gaol (jail preferred)
  • mould/mold (mould preferred)

Government Preferences

Word Preferred Alternative Notes
Financial advisor adviser advisor Both common in government
Student enrollment enrolment enrollment British form preferred
Color description grey gray Grey for colour, Gray for names
Prison facility jail gaol Modern form preferred

Regional and Professional Variations

  • Legal terms: May follow traditional British spellings
  • Medical terms: Often follow American conventions
  • Scientific terms: International standards may apply
  • Technical terms: Industry standards often dictate spelling

Specialized Terminology

Legal (traditional):

  • judgement (legal context)
  • acknowledgement (legal documents)
  • gaol (historical legal documents)

General (modern):

  • judgment (general use)
  • acknowledgment (general use)
  • jail (modern usage)

Compound Word Variants

  • any one/anyone: "any one person" vs. "anyone can apply"
  • every one/everyone: "every one of them" vs. "everyone agrees"
  • some time/sometime: "some time ago" vs. "sometime next week"
  • all right/alright: "all right" preferred in formal writing

⚠️ Avoid Mixing Variants

If you choose "adviser," don't use "advisor" elsewhere in the same document. Consistency within documents is more important than following a particular variant.

💡 Creating a Style Sheet

For long documents or ongoing projects, create a style sheet listing your chosen variants. Share this with all writers on the project.

3.04 Word Endings

Canadian spelling follows specific patterns for word endings that combine British and American influences. Understanding these patterns helps with consistent spelling of related words.

-ment vs. -ement endings

Most words use '-ment' but some require '-ement':

  • -ment endings: government, payment, treatment, assessment
  • -ement endings: acknowledgement, judgement, abridgement

Common Government Terms

-ment endings:

  • The government announced new funding
  • Payment processing will be automated
  • Assessment criteria have been updated
  • Development plans are being reviewed

-ement endings:

  • Acknowledgement of receipt is required
  • The judgement was appealed
  • An abridgement of the report is available

-able vs. -ible endings

More words end in '-able' than '-ible'. Learn the common '-ible' words:

  • -ible words: accessible, admissible, compatible, credible, eligible, flexible, horrible, impossible, incredible, responsible, terrible, visible
  • -able words: Most others (acceptable, available, comfortable, dependable, reasonable, suitable)

-ance vs. -ence endings

These endings sound the same but have different spellings:

  • -ance words: assistance, attendance, compliance, guidance, importance, performance, resistance
  • -ence words: conference, correspondence, difference, excellence, experience, patience, preference, reference

Memory Aids for -ance/-ence

Related Verb/Adjective Noun Ending Example
assist -ance assistance
attend -ance attendance
prefer -ence preference
refer -ence reference

-er vs. -or endings

Most agent nouns end in '-er', but some professions use '-or':

  • -er endings: adviser, manager, officer, planner, researcher, teacher, worker
  • -or endings: administrator, auditor, coordinator, director, supervisor

Double vs. Single Consonants

Canadian spelling generally follows British patterns for doubling:

  • Double 'l': travelled, modelling, counsellor
  • Double 's': focussed (alternative to focused)
  • Single consonant: canceled (alternative to cancelled)

Government Job Titles

Common patterns:

  • Policy adviser (or advisor)
  • Program manager
  • Financial officer
  • Communications director
  • Project coordinator
  • Senior administrator

💡 Learning Strategy

When uncertain about word endings, look for related words you know how to spell. For example, if you know "prefer," you can remember "preference" ends in '-ence'.

3.05 Troublesome Words

Certain words cause frequent spelling errors in government writing. These troublesome words require special attention and memorization.

Commonly Misspelled Government Terms

  • accommodate (double c, double m)
  • beginning (double n)
  • committee (double m, double t)
  • correspondence (double r)
  • definitely (not definately)
  • environment (no 'i' before 'r')
  • foreign (e before i)
  • government (no 'a' between 'n' and 'm')

Memory Aids for Difficult Words

Word Common Error Memory Aid
accommodate accomodate It can accommodate two c's and two m's
definitely definately There's definitely a finite in the middle
separate seperate There's a rat in separate
necessary neccessary One collar (c) and two socks (s) are necessary

Words with Silent Letters

  • debt, doubt (silent 'b')
  • foreign, sovereign (silent 'g')
  • parliament (silent 'i')
  • mortgage (silent 't')
  • knowledge (silent 'w')

Homophones in Government Writing

  • principal/principle: The principal concern vs. the principle of fairness
  • affect/effect: The policy will affect citizens vs. the effect of the policy
  • complement/compliment: This complements the policy vs. pay a compliment
  • councillor/counsellor: City councillor vs. guidance counsellor

Frequently Confused Spellings

✓ Correct spellings:

  • The committee will accommodate all requests
  • Correspondence must be definitely filed
  • Foreign investment affects the environment
  • Parliament passed necessary legislation
  • The principal principle guides our decisions

✗ Common errors:

  • The comittee will accomodate all requests
  • Correspondance must be definately filed
  • Foriegn investment effects the enviroment
  • Parliment passed neccessary legislation
  • The principle principal guides our decisions

Technical and Professional Terms

  • bureaucracy (not beaurocracy)
  • business (not buisness)
  • maintenance (not maintainance)
  • occurrence (double c, double r)
  • privilege (not priviledge)
  • receive (i before e except after c)
  • schedule (sch- beginning)
  • tomorrow (one 'm', two 'r's)

Canadian-Specific Challenges

  • lieutenant (unusual spelling for pronunciation)
  • manoeuvre (British spelling retained)
  • cheque (not check for bank instrument)
  • labour (not labor)

💡 Proofreading Strategy

Keep a personal list of words you frequently misspell. Use spell-check but don't rely on it entirely - it won't catch "principle" when you meant "principal."

⚠️ Spell-Check Limitations

Spell-check programs may not recognize Canadian spellings and might flag correct words like "colour" or "centre." Configure your spell-checker for Canadian English when possible.
