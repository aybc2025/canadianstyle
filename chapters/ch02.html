Hyphenation

Master hyphenation, compounding, and word division in Canadian writing

2.01 Introduction

Hyphenation is one of the most challenging aspects of English writing. The hyphen serves multiple purposes: joining compound words, connecting prefixes and suffixes, and dividing words at line breaks.

Key Principle

Hyphenation practices evolve over time. Words often progress from hyphenated compounds to closed compounds. When in doubt, consult a current Canadian dictionary.

Main Uses of Hyphens

  • Compound adjectives: well-known author, state-of-the-art technology
  • Compound nouns: co-worker, mother-in-law, twenty-one
  • Prefixes: ex-president, self-control, anti-government
  • Word division: Breaking words at syllable boundaries
  • Clarity: Avoiding ambiguity in meaning

Evolution of Compound Words

Stage 1: Separate Stage 2: Hyphenated Stage 3: Closed
data base data-base database
web site web-site website
email e-mail email

2.02 Compound Words

Compound words are formed by joining two or more words to create a new meaning. The treatment of compounds depends on their grammatical function and established usage.

Compound Adjectives

  • Before a noun: Usually hyphenated
  • After a noun: Usually not hyphenated
  • With adverbs ending in -ly: No hyphen needed
  • Permanent compounds: Follow dictionary

Compound Adjectives

Before noun (hyphenated):

  • a well-known author
  • state-of-the-art equipment
  • user-friendly software
  • high-speed internet

After noun (not hyphenated):

  • The author is well known
  • The equipment is state of the art
  • The software is user friendly
  • The internet is high speed

With -ly adverbs (no hyphen):

  • highly qualified candidates
  • recently published book
  • carefully planned strategy

Compound Nouns

  • Family relationships: mother-in-law, sister-in-law
  • Numbers twenty-one to ninety-nine: All hyphenated
  • Fractions: one-third, two-fifths (when used as adjectives)
  • Job titles: editor-in-chief, commander-in-chief

Number Compounds

Numbers (hyphenated):

  • twenty-one delegates
  • thirty-five percent
  • sixty-seven votes
  • ninety-nine problems

Fractions as adjectives:

  • a two-thirds majority
  • one-half cup
  • three-quarter time

Fractions as nouns (not hyphenated):

  • Two thirds of the members voted
  • One half was sufficient
  • Three quarters agreed

üí° Memory Aid

If you can substitute "and" for the hyphen and the meaning stays the same, you probably need the hyphen: "a black-and-white photo" = "a black and white photo".

2.03 Prefixes & Suffixes

Most prefixes are joined directly to the root word without a hyphen, but certain situations require hyphenation for clarity or pronunciation.

When to Hyphenate Prefixes

  • Before proper nouns: anti-Canadian, pro-American
  • Before capital letters: pre-MBA, non-EU
  • To avoid doubling vowels: anti-inflammatory, co-operate
  • With self- prefix: self-control, self-evident
  • With ex- meaning former: ex-president, ex-wife

Common Prefixes

Prefix Usually No Hyphen Hyphen Required
anti- antibiotic, antisocial anti-Canadian, anti-inflammatory
co- cooperate, coordinate co-worker, co-operate (optional)
pre- prewar, predetermined pre-Confederation, pre-MBA
re- rewrite, reorganize re-enter, re-elect
non- nonviolent, nonprofit non-Canadian, non-EU

Special Prefix Cases

  • self-: Always hyphenated (self-control, self-service)
  • ex-: Hyphenated when meaning "former" (ex-minister)
  • all-: Usually hyphenated (all-Canadian, all-purpose)
  • well-: Hyphenated before nouns (well-being, well-known)

Avoiding Ambiguity

Clear with hyphen:

  • re-cover (cover again) vs. recover (get well)
  • re-creation (creating again) vs. recreation (leisure)
  • co-op (cooperative) vs. coop (chicken house)

Suffixes

  • Generally no hyphen: Most suffixes join directly
  • Exception -elect: president-elect, governor-elect
  • Exception -free and -like: sugar-free, child-like
  • With numbers: 20-something, 40-year-old

2.04 Word Division

Word division occurs when a word must be broken at the end of a line. While modern word processing reduces this need, understanding proper division remains important for typesetting and justified text.

Basic Rules for Word Division

  • Divide between syllables: Use dictionary syllable breaks
  • Keep prefixes intact: mis-under-stand (not mi-sunder-stand)
  • Keep suffixes intact: friend-ship (not friends-hip)
  • Divide compound words at the hyphen: mother-in-law
  • Leave at least 3 characters: Don't divide very short words

Correct Word Divisions

‚úì Correct divisions:

  • gov-ern-ment
  • man-age-ment
  • rep-re-sen-ta-tive
  • un-der-stand-ing
  • co-op-er-a-tion

‚úó Incorrect divisions:

  • go-vernment (breaks prefix)
  • manage-ment (breaks suffix)
  • rep-resent-ative (awkward break)
  • a-bout (too few letters)

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid These Divisions

  • Single letters: Don't leave or carry single letters (a-bout, man-y)
  • Two letters: Avoid divisions leaving only two letters (al-so, of-ten)
  • Proper names: Generally don't divide personal or place names
  • Numbers and dates: Keep intact when possible
  • Abbreviations: Never divide abbreviations

Special Considerations

  • Double consonants: Usually divide between them (com-mit-tee)
  • Vowel combinations: Don't separate diphthongs (rea-son, not re-ason)
  • -ing endings: Usually take the whole suffix (work-ing)
  • Silent -e: Usually goes with preceding syllable (hous-es)

üí° Modern Practice

With modern word processing, avoid word division when possible. Use it only when necessary for justified text or narrow columns, and always check syllable breaks in a dictionary.

2.05 Special Cases

Certain hyphenation situations require special attention due to evolving usage, ambiguity concerns, or established conventions in Canadian government writing.

Age and Time Expressions

  • Ages as adjectives: 30-year-old minister, 5-year-old child
  • Ages as nouns: a 30 year old, the 5 year old
  • Time periods: 10-minute break, 3-hour meeting
  • Academic years: 2023-24 school year, 2023-2024 budget

Age and Time Examples

As adjectives (hyphenated):

  • The 45-year-old prime minister addressed the nation
  • A 10-minute presentation is scheduled
  • The 3-day conference was successful
  • A 20-year commitment to climate action

As nouns (not hyphenated):

  • The prime minister, a 45 year old, addressed the nation
  • The presentation lasted 10 minutes
  • The conference ran for 3 days
  • The commitment spans 20 years

Colour Compounds

  • Two colours: blue-green water, red-orange sunset
  • Colour + noun: navy-blue suit, sky-blue eyes
  • Colour modifiers: light-blue shirt, dark-green forest

Direction and Location

Compound directions (hyphenated):

  • north-south highway
  • east-west trade corridor
  • northeast-southwest diagonal

Regional terms:

  • Atlantic Canada (no hyphen)
  • Western Canada (no hyphen)
  • Central Canada (no hyphen)
  • French-Canadian culture (hyphenated adjective)

Technology and Modern Terms

  • Email: email (closed, no hyphen) - modern usage
  • Internet terms: website, online, smartphone
  • E-prefixes: e-commerce, e-government (still hyphenated)
  • Cyber terms: cybersecurity (closed), cyber-attack (hyphenated)

Government-Specific Terms

Term Correct Form Usage
Policy making policy-making (adj.), policy making (noun) policy-making process, engaged in policy making
Decision making decision-making (adj.), decision making (noun) decision-making authority, involved in decision making
Cost effective cost-effective (adj.), cost effective (pred.) cost-effective solution, the solution is cost effective
Long term long-term (adj.), long term (noun) long-term planning, planning for the long term

‚ö†Ô∏è Evolving Usage

Technology terms evolve rapidly. What was "e-mail" is now "email." Check current government style guides and dictionaries for the most up-to-date conventions.

2.06 Technical Terms

Technical, scientific, and specialized terms often have specific hyphenation conventions. Government writing frequently includes technical content that requires precise formatting.

Scientific and Medical Terms

  • Chemical compounds: Follow scientific conventions
  • Medical terms: Check medical dictionaries
  • Units of measure: kilowatt-hour, person-year
  • Statistical terms: t-test, chi-square, p-value

Scientific Measurements

Hyphenated compounds:

  • kilowatt-hour consumption
  • person-year calculations
  • cost-benefit analysis
  • risk-assessment procedures
  • peer-reviewed research

Technical modifiers:

  • state-of-the-art technology
  • evidence-based policy
  • data-driven decisions
  • real-time monitoring

Legal and Regulatory Terms

  • Legal compounds: cease-and-desist, attorney-client
  • Regulatory terms: compliance-related, audit-ready
  • Cross-references: above-mentioned, afore-said
  • Legal status: court-ordered, government-funded

Financial and Economic Terms

Term Type Examples Usage Notes
Ratios debt-to-income, cost-to-benefit Hyphenate ratio expressions
Market terms bull-market conditions, bear-market trends When used as adjectives
Time periods quarter-over-quarter, year-on-year Comparative time expressions
Budget terms across-the-board cuts, line-by-line review Established budget terminology

Information Technology

  • Network terms: client-server, peer-to-peer
  • Security terms: end-to-end encryption, two-factor authentication
  • Development terms: user-friendly, plug-and-play
  • System terms: real-time processing, multi-user environment

Environmental and Climate Terms

Environmental compounds:

  • greenhouse-gas emissions
  • carbon-neutral policies
  • renewable-energy sources
  • climate-change adaptation
  • ecosystem-based management

Social and Policy Terms

  • Social policy: means-tested benefits, income-based assistance
  • Demographics: age-related services, gender-based analysis
  • Public health: evidence-based medicine, population-level interventions
  • Education: competency-based learning, outcome-focused programs

üí° Staying Current

Technical terminology evolves rapidly. Consult current specialized dictionaries, style guides, and professional organizations for the most accurate hyphenation of technical terms.

‚ö†Ô∏è Consistency in Technical Writing

When writing technical documents, create a terminology list at the beginning of your project. Ensure all writers on the team use the same hyphenation conventions for specialized terms.
