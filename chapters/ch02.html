2.01 Introduction

Hyphenation is one of the most challenging aspects of English writing. The hyphen serves multiple purposes: joining compound words, connecting prefixes and suffixes, and dividing words at line breaks.

Key Principle

Hyphenation practices evolve over time. Words often progress from hyphenated compounds to closed compounds. When in doubt, consult a current Canadian dictionary.

Main Uses of Hyphens

  • Compound adjectives: well-known author, state-of-the-art technology
  • Compound nouns: co-worker, mother-in-law, twenty-one
  • Prefixes: ex-president, self-control, anti-government
  • Word division: Breaking words at syllable boundaries
  • Clarity: Avoiding ambiguity in meaning

2.02 Compound Words

Compound words combine two or more words to create new meanings. Hyphenation depends on the word's position and function in the sentence.

Before Nouns vs. After Nouns

‚úì Before Nouns (Hyphenated)

  • well-known author
  • high-quality work
  • up-to-date information
  • part-time position

‚úì After Nouns (Not Hyphenated)

  • The author is well known
  • The work is high quality
  • The information is up to date
  • The position is part time

2.03 Prefixes & Suffixes

Most prefixes are joined directly to the root word without a hyphen, but certain situations require hyphenation for clarity or pronunciation.

When to Hyphenate Prefixes

  • Before proper nouns: anti-Canadian, pro-American
  • Before capital letters: pre-MBA, non-EU
  • To avoid doubling vowels: anti-inflammatory, co-operate
  • With self- prefix: self-control, self-evident
  • With ex- meaning former: ex-president, ex-wife

2.04 Word Division

Word division occurs when a word must be broken at the end of a line. Modern word processors handle this automatically, but understanding the rules helps with manual formatting.

Basic Word Division Rules

  • Between syllables: Break at natural syllable boundaries
  • Double consonants: Usually divide between them (run-ning)
  • Compound words: Break at the natural join (under-stand)
  • Avoid poor breaks: Don't leave single letters alone

Good vs. Poor Word Division

‚úì Good Breaks

  • impor-tant
  • govern-ment
  • under-stand
  • pro-gram

‚úó Poor Breaks

  • a-bout (single letter)
  • import-ant (awkward)
  • th-rough (awkward)
  • stren-gth (unpronounceable)

2.05 French Compounds

French compound words and word division follow different rules from English and require special attention in bilingual Canadian documents.

French Compound Adjectives

When French words are used as compound adjectives in English text, follow these guidelines:

  • Retain French hyphenation: avant-garde, laissez-faire
  • Use English rules for new combinations: French-Canadian relations
  • Consider context and audience: Specialist vs. general readership

French Compound Examples

‚úì Retained French form: "The avant-garde movement influenced Canadian art."

‚úì English adaptation: "French-speaking Canadians comprise 22% of the population."

2.06 Special Cases

Some hyphenation situations require special attention and don't follow standard rules.

Numbers and Measurements

  • Twenty-one to ninety-nine: Always hyphenated
  • Fractions as adjectives: two-thirds majority
  • With units: 10-kilometre race, 30-minute meeting

Suspended Hyphenation

When multiple compounds share a common element:

  • Example: "first- and second-year students"
  • Usage: Saves space and avoids repetition

2.07 Technical Terms

Technical and scientific terms often have specialized hyphenation rules.

Scientific Compounds

  • Chemical names: Follow standard scientific notation
  • Units of measurement: kilowatt-hours, person-days
  • Technical processes: freeze-drying, X-ray
