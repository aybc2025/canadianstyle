02

Hyphenation: Compounding and Word Division

Learn when and how to use hyphens in compound words and word division

2.01 Introduction 2.02 Compound Nouns 2.03 Nouns with Adjectives 2.04 Compound Adjectives 2.07 Prefixes 2.10 Numerals 2.17 Word Division

2.01 Introduction

Hyphenation is one of the most variable aspects of English writing. This chapter provides guidance on when to hyphenate compound words and how to divide words at line endings.

General Principles

  • Clarity: Use hyphens to prevent misreading
  • Evolution: Many hyphenated compounds eventually become solid words
  • Consistency: Follow one style throughout your document
  • Dictionary: Consult a current Canadian dictionary when in doubt
Evolution of Compounds

co-operate → cooperate

e-mail → email

on-line → online

data-base → database

Three Types of Compounds

  1. Open: ice cream, high school
  2. Hyphenated: mother-in-law, up-to-date
  3. Solid: notebook, database

2.02 Compound Nouns

Most compound nouns are written as separate words or as one word. Use hyphens primarily when:

Always Hyphenate

  • Family relationships: mother-in-law, great-grandmother
  • Compounds with prepositions: passer-by, hanger-on
  • Double titles: secretary-treasurer, owner-operator
  • Verb + noun compounds: has-been, write-up
Example 2.02a

The editor-in-chief met with the vice-president.

Several passers-by witnessed the accident.

She works as an owner-operator of a small business.

Usually Separate Words

  • blood pressure
  • income tax
  • life insurance
  • real estate
  • social media

Usually One Word

  • businessperson
  • database
  • healthcare
  • website
  • workplace

⚠️ Watch Out

Some compounds change form when used as modifiers:

  • decision making (noun) → decision-making process (adjective)
  • policy maker (noun) → policy-making role (adjective)

2.04 Compound Adjectives

Hyphenate compound adjectives when they come before the noun they modify:

Before the Noun (Hyphenate)

Example 2.04a

a well-known author

a 10-year-old building

a full-time employee

a high-quality product

a government-funded program

After the Noun (Usually No Hyphen)

Example 2.04b

The author is well known.

The building is 10 years old.

She works full time.

The product is of high quality.

Exceptions: No Hyphen Needed

  • Adverbs ending in -ly: highly qualified person, newly elected official
  • Proper nouns: North American trade, Toronto based company
  • Chemical terms: sodium chloride solution
  • Foreign phrases: bona fide offer, ad hoc committee

Multiple Word Compounds

Use hyphens to link all elements:

Example 2.04c

state-of-the-art technology

pay-as-you-go plan

coast-to-coast coverage

up-to-date information

2.07 Prefixes

Most prefixes attach directly to words without hyphens, but there are important exceptions:

Hyphenate After These Prefixes

All-, Ex-, Self-

  • all-inclusive
  • all-Canadian
  • ex-president
  • ex-employee
  • self-evident
  • self-service

Before Proper Nouns

  • anti-American
  • pre-Confederation
  • post-Victorian
  • trans-Atlantic
  • mid-January
  • non-European

Before Numbers

  • pre-1960s
  • post-2000
  • mid-1980s

Hyphenate to Avoid Confusion

Example 2.07

re-cover (cover again) vs. recover (get better)

re-creation (creating again) vs. recreation (leisure)

co-op (cooperative) vs. coop (chicken house)

re-sign (sign again) vs. resign (quit)

No Hyphen Usually Needed

  • Pre: preview, prehistoric, prearrange
  • Re: rewrite, rebuild, reorganize
  • Un: unhappy, unable, uncertain
  • Over/Under: overdue, understate
  • Inter: international, interpersonal

Special Case: Co-

The prefix "co-" is increasingly written solid, but use a hyphen:

  • Before a vowel to aid pronunciation: co-author, co-operate
  • To avoid confusion: co-op vs. coop
  • In newer formations: co-CEO, co-working

2.17 Word Division

Although word processors handle most line breaks automatically, understanding word division principles helps with layout and design decisions:

Basic Rules

  1. Divide between syllables: know-ledge, impor-tant
  2. Divide after prefixes: pre-view, dis-appear
  3. Divide before suffixes: teach-ing, care-ful
  4. Divide between double consonants: run-ning, suc-cess

Never Divide

  • One-syllable words: through, strength, width
  • Contractions: don't, wouldn't, it's
  • Abbreviations: NATO, CBC, RCMP
  • Numbers: Keep figures together
  • The last word on a page

Avoid Dividing

Poor Word Divisions

  • Words of fewer than six letters
  • After only one or two letters: a-bout ❌
  • Before only one or two letters: month-ly ❌
  • Proper names when possible
  • Hyphenated compounds except at the hyphen

Special Considerations

  • Compound words: Divide at the hyphen only: self-employed, not self-em-ployed
  • URLs and email: Never divide web addresses or email addresses
  • Technical terms: Avoid dividing to prevent confusion
Good Word Divisions

knowl-edge (after consonant)

impor-tant (between syllables)

pre-view (after prefix)

teach-ing (before suffix)
