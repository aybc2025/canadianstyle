9.01 Introduction

Reference matter includes bibliographies, footnotes, endnotes, and indexes—essential components for documenting sources and helping readers locate information in government publications.

Key Purpose: Reference matter allows readers to verify information, follow up on sources, and navigate documents effectively.

9.02 Bibliographies

Bibliographies list sources consulted or cited in a document, arranged alphabetically by author's surname.

Basic Bibliography Format

Books:
Smith, John A. Canadian Government Policy. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2023.
Articles:
Brown, Sarah. "Climate Change Adaptation." Canadian Public Policy 45, no. 2 (2023): 123-145.
Government Documents:
Canada. Environment and Climate Change Canada. National Climate Assessment. Ottawa: ECCC, 2023.

Organization Principles

  • Alphabetical order by author's last name
  • Hanging indent for subsequent lines
  • Consistent formatting throughout
  • Government authors listed by jurisdiction

9.03 Reference Notes

Reference notes can appear as footnotes (bottom of page) or endnotes (end of chapter/document) to provide source information and additional context.

Footnotes vs. Endnotes

  • Footnotes: Immediate reference, easier for readers
  • Endnotes: Less distracting, better for lengthy notes
  • Choose one system and use consistently

Numbering System

  • Sequential numbering throughout document
  • Superscript numbers in text
  • Corresponding numbers in notes section

Sample Reference Notes

1 John A. Smith, Canadian Government Policy (Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2023), 45.
2 Canada, Environment and Climate Change Canada, National Climate Assessment (Ottawa: ECCC, 2023), 12-15.

9.04 Citations

In-text citations provide brief source information within the body of the document, with full details in the bibliography.

Author-Date System

Climate change poses significant challenges (Smith 2023, 45).
As Smith (2023) argues, climate adaptation requires immediate action.

Government Documents

The report recommends immediate action (Canada 2023, 12).
According to Environment and Climate Change Canada (2023), emissions must decrease.

Multiple Authors

  • Two authors: (Smith and Jones 2023)
  • Three or more: (Smith et al. 2023)
  • Multiple sources: (Smith 2023; Jones 2022)

9.05 Indexes

Indexes provide alphabetical access points to information in a document, helping readers locate specific topics quickly.

Index Structure

  • Main entries: Primary topics in alphabetical order
  • Subentries: Related subtopics indented under main entries
  • Cross-references: "See" and "See also" references

Sample Index Entries

Climate change, 12-15, 34
    adaptation, 45-48
    mitigation, 22-25
    policy responses, 67-72
Government departments. See specific departments
Policy development, 89-92. See also Implementation

Indexing Guidelines

  • Use nouns as main entries when possible
  • Invert names: "Smith, John" not "John Smith"
  • Group related pages: Use ranges (12-15) for continuous coverage
  • Avoid single-page entries unless highly significant
