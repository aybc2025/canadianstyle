6.01 Introduction

Italics serve specific purposes in professional writing. They help distinguish certain types of content from regular text and add emphasis when appropriate. Understanding when and when not to use italics ensures your documents maintain a professional appearance.

When to Use Italics

  • Titles: Books, periodicals, films, artworks
  • Foreign words: Non-English terms not yet adopted into English
  • Scientific names: Species and genus names
  • Legal cases: Court case names
  • Emphasis: Sparingly, for important words or phrases

6.02 Emphasis

Using Italics for Emphasis

Italics can provide emphasis, but use them sparingly and strategically:

When to Emphasize

  • Key terms being defined for the first time
  • Words that need special attention in context
  • Contrasting elements in a comparison
  • Words being discussed as words

Emphasis Examples

‚úì Appropriate emphasis:

  • The program focuses on prevention rather than treatment.
  • What does sustainable mean in this context?
  • The word government appears 47 times in the document.

‚úó Overuse of emphasis:

  • This is very important and must be done immediately.
  • The new policy will significantly improve our operations.

üí° Alternatives to Italics

Consider other emphasis methods: bold text, CAPITALS (sparingly), or restructuring sentences to naturally emphasize important points.

6.03 Titles of Works

Publications and Creative Works

Use italics for titles of major works and publications:

Use Italics For

  • Books: The Canadian Style
  • Newspapers: The Globe and Mail
  • Magazines: Maclean's
  • Films: The National Dream
  • TV shows: The Nature of Things
  • Artworks: The Jack Pine

Use Quotation Marks For

  • Articles: "Climate Policy in Canada"
  • Short stories: "The Hockey Sweater"
  • Poems: "In Flanders Fields"
  • Songs: "O Canada"
  • Chapter titles: "Introduction to Grammar"

Title Examples

‚úì Correct formatting:

  • The article "Digital Government Services" appeared in Canadian Public Administration.
  • The film Atanarjuat won international acclaim.
  • Chapter 3, "Effective Writing," discusses clarity.

6.05 Legal Cases and Citations

Court Cases and Legal References

Italicize the names of legal cases and certain legal references:

Use Italics For

  • Case names: R. v. Morgentaler
  • Civil cases: Roncarelli v. Duplessis
  • Constitutional references: Reference re Secession of Quebec
  • Legal publications: Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms

Legal Citation Examples

‚úì Proper legal formatting:

  • In R. v. Oakes, the Court established the proportionality test.
  • The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms protects fundamental rights.
  • The case of Baker v. Canada addressed procedural fairness.

Don't Italicize

  • Statutes: Criminal Code, Income Tax Act
  • Regulations: Privacy Regulations
  • Acts: Official Languages Act
  • Legal concepts: reasonable doubt, due process

üí° Legal Writing Tip

In legal documents, follow your jurisdiction's style guide. Some courts prefer different formatting conventions for case names and citations.

Mixed Legal Text

‚úì Combining italics and regular text:

  • The Supreme Court's decision in R. v. Stinchcombe under the Criminal Code established disclosure obligations.
  • Section 15 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms guarantees equality rights.
