When to Use Italics

6.02 Emphasis

Italics can emphasize key words or phrases, but should be used judiciously to maintain their impact.

Emphasis Guidelines

  • Sparingly: Only for truly important emphasis
  • Alternative methods: Consider sentence structure, word choice
  • Avoid overuse: Too many italics diminish effectiveness
  • Consistency: Use same approach throughout document

6.03 Foreign Words & Phrases

Foreign words and phrases that haven't been fully adopted into English are typically italicized.

When to Italicize Foreign Words

  • Not in English dictionaries: Check standard dictionaries
  • Unfamiliar to readers: Consider your audience
  • First occurrence: Italicize and define if necessary
  • Technical terms: Specialized vocabulary from other languages

Don't Italicize

  • Common borrowed words: caf√©, garage, kindergarten
  • Place names: Montreal, Quebec, Vancouver
  • Common abbreviations: etc., e.g., i.e.
  • Well-known phrases: ad hoc, per capita

Foreign Word Examples

6.04 Titles & Publications

Major works like books, newspapers, and films are italicized, while shorter works use quotation marks.

Italicize These Titles

  • Books: The Canadian Style, Anne of Green Gables
  • Newspapers: The Globe and Mail, La Presse
  • Magazines: Maclean's, Canadian Geographic
  • Films: Bon Cop Bad Cop, The Sweet Hereafter
  • TV series: Corner Gas, Schitt's Creek

Use Quotation Marks

  • Articles: "Canada's Economic Outlook"
  • Short stories: "The Boat" by Alistair MacLeod
  • Poems: "In Flanders Fields"
  • TV episodes: "The Contest" (Seinfeld)

6.05 Technical & Scientific Terms

Scientific and technical writing uses italics for specific purposes, including species names and newly introduced terms.

Scientific Uses of Italics

  • Species names: Homo sapiens, Acer saccharum (sugar maple)
  • Gene names: BRCA1, p53
  • Variables in equations: x + y = z
  • New terminology: When first defining technical terms

6.06 Legal References

Legal writing has specific conventions for italicizing case names and legislation.

Legal Uses of Italics

  • Case names: R. v. Smith, Brown v. Board of Education
  • Legislation: Criminal Code, Charter of Rights and Freedoms
  • Latin legal terms: habeas corpus, res judicata

Legal Citation Examples
