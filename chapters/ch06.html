6

Italics

When and how to use italics effectively

6.01 Introduction 6.02 Titles & Publications 6.03 Foreign Words 6.04 Emphasis 6.05 Technical Terms 6.06 Special Uses

6.01 Introduction

Italics serve specific purposes in Canadian government writing: emphasizing words, indicating foreign terms, marking titles of major works, and highlighting technical terminology. Use italics purposefully and sparingly.

Key Principle: Use italics to clarify meaning and provide visual distinction, but avoid overuse which can make text difficult to read.

6.02 Titles & Publications

Major works such as books, periodicals, newspapers, and films are italicized, while shorter works use quotation marks.

Italicize These

  • Books: The Canadian Style, Anne of Green Gables
  • Magazines & Newspapers: Maclean's, The Globe and Mail
  • Films & TV shows: The English Patient, Corner Gas
  • Long reports: Truth and Reconciliation Commission Final Report

Use Quotation Marks For

  • Articles: "Climate Change in Canada"
  • Chapter titles: "Introduction to Italics"
  • Short stories: "The Hockey Sweater"
  • Song titles: "The Maple Leaf Forever"

6.03 Foreign Words

Foreign words and phrases that have not become established in English are italicized for clarity.

Italicize Foreign Terms

  • Latin phrases: ad hoc, per se, bona fide
  • French terms: raison d'être, fait accompli
  • Other languages: schadenfreude (German), feng shui (Chinese)

Don't Italicize These Established Terms

  • Common Latin: etc., i.e., e.g., via, per capita
  • Naturalized words: café, résumé, kindergarten
  • Place names: Quebec, Montreal (even if French origin)

6.04 Emphasis

Italics can provide emphasis, but use this technique sparingly to maintain effectiveness and readability.

When to Use Italics for Emphasis

  • Subtle stress: "The policy applies to all employees."
  • Clarification: "We need the original document, not a copy."
  • Contrast: "The intended result differs from the actual outcome."

Alternatives to Consider

  • Restructure the sentence for natural emphasis
  • Use strong verbs instead of emphatic formatting
  • Bold text for headings and labels (not body text)
  • Bullet points to highlight key information
Caution: Avoid overusing italics for emphasis, as it can make text appear unprofessional and difficult to read.

6.05 Technical Terms

Scientific names, legal terms, and specialized vocabulary often require italics for proper formatting.

Scientific Names

  • Genus and species: Acer saccharum (sugar maple)
  • Botanical terms: Quercus species
  • First mention only: E. coli, then E. coli

Legal and Government Terms

  • Case names: R. v. Smith, Brown v. Board
  • Latin legal terms: habeas corpus, amicus curiae
  • Specialized terms: when first defined or when clarity is needed

6.06 Special Uses

Italics serve additional purposes in government writing, including references to words as words and specific formatting conventions.

Words as Words

  • Linguistic discussion: "The word government has multiple meanings."
  • Spelling clarification: "Note the e in judgment."
  • Grammar examples: "Avoid using irregardless in formal writing."

Letters and Symbols

  • Referring to letters: "Add an s to make it plural."
  • Mathematical variables: x + y = z
  • Menu items: Click File then Save

Introductory Words

  • See also: See also Chapter 5

  • Note: Note: This applies to all departments
