06

Italics

Learn when to use italics for emphasis, titles, and foreign words

6.01 Introduction

Italics (or underlining in handwritten text) serve several purposes: emphasis, titles of major works, foreign words, and special uses. Use italics sparingly—overuse diminishes their effect.

General Principles

  • Consistency: Apply italic rules uniformly throughout your document
  • Alternatives: In plain text, use underscores _like this_ or quotes
  • Restraint: Too many italics make text hard to read
  • House style: Some organizations have specific italic policies
Key Principle

Italics draw attention. Use them purposefully, not decoratively.

When NOT to Use Italics

  • Quotation marks (use one or the other, not both)
  • Common foreign words now part of English
  • Company names (unless they use italics in their logo)
  • Sacred texts (the Bible, the Quran)

6.02 Emphasis

Use italics sparingly for emphasis. Good writing rarely needs typographical emphasis.

Appropriate Uses

Example 6.02a - Contrast

The question is not when but how.

I said I was hoping to go, not planning to go.

Example 6.02b - Key Terms

The concept of reasonable accommodation is central to this policy.

What we call active voice makes writing more direct.

Avoid Overuse

⚠️ Too Much Emphasis

❌ This is very important and must be done immediately!

✓ This urgent matter requires immediate attention.

Alternatives to Italics for Emphasis

  • Word choice: Use stronger, more precise words
  • Sentence structure: Put important information first or last
  • Bold text: For headings or key terms (use sparingly)
  • White space: Set off important text

Special Uses

  • Stage directions: [Enter Hamlet]
  • Internal thoughts: This can't be happening, she thought.
  • Irony or sarcasm: His help made things worse. (use sparingly)

6.03 Titles of Publications and Works of Art

Use italics for titles of major, self-contained works:

Italicize These Titles

Books & Publications

  • Books: The Handmaid's Tale
  • Newspapers: The Globe and Mail
  • Magazines: Canadian Geographic
  • Journals: Canadian Medical Association Journal
  • Reports: Annual Report 2024

Entertainment

  • Movies: Bon Cop, Bad Cop
  • TV series: Kim's Convenience
  • Plays: Les Belles-sœurs
  • Operas: Louis Riel
  • Albums: Blue by Joni Mitchell

Artworks

  • Paintings: The Jack Pine
  • Sculptures: The Spirit of Haida Gwaii
  • Long poems: The Cremation of Sam McGee

Use Quotation Marks Instead

For shorter works that are part of larger publications:

  • Articles: "Budget Highlights for 2024"
  • Short stories: "The Bear Came Over the Mountain"
  • Songs: "The Hockey Song"
  • TV episodes: "The Trouble with Tracy"
  • Short poems: "In Flanders Fields"

Special Cases

No Italics or Quotes
  • The Bible, the Torah, the Quran (sacred texts)
  • The Constitution Act, 1867 (legal documents)
  • Websites: CBC.ca, Canada.ca (but italicize online magazines)
  • Software: Microsoft Word, Adobe Photoshop

Punctuation with Italic Titles

Include punctuation in italics only if it's part of the title:

Example 6.03

Have you read Who Has Seen the Wind?

I just finished Life of Pi.

(The period is not part of the title)

6.04 French and Foreign Words and Phrases

Italicize foreign words and phrases that haven't been adopted into English:

Italicize Unfamiliar Foreign Terms

Example 6.04a

The concept of joie de vivre permeates Quebec culture.

She had a certain je ne sais quoi about her.

The raison d'être of the organization is community service.

Do NOT Italicize Common Foreign Words

Many foreign words are now part of English:

French Origin (No Italics)

  • café
  • cliché
  • debut
  • elite
  • entrepreneur
  • fiancé/fiancée
  • genre
  • résumé

Other Languages (No Italics)

  • pasta (Italian)
  • kindergarten (German)
  • karaoke (Japanese)
  • taco (Spanish)
  • yoga (Sanskrit)
  • safari (Swahili)

Canadian Context

In Canada, be particularly aware of French terms:

  • Common terms (no italics): poutine, tuque, dépanneur
  • Official terms (no italics): francophone, anglophone, allophone
  • Less common (italicize): pure laine, coureur de bois

Quotations in Foreign Languages

Example 6.04b

Short phrase: The motto Je me souviens appears on Quebec license plates.

Full sentence: As they say in Quebec, "C'est le fun!" (no additional italics needed)

Tip: If you use a foreign term repeatedly, italicize it only on first use, then use regular type.

6.05 Latin Terms and Abbreviations

Latin terms require special attention, as some are italicized while others are not:

Common Latin Abbreviations (NO Italics)

Abbreviation Latin Full Form Meaning
e.g. exempli gratia for example
i.e. id est that is
etc. et cetera and so forth
vs. or v. versus against
et al. et alii and others
ca. or c. circa approximately

Less Common Latin Terms (Italicize)

  • ad hoc (for this purpose)
  • bona fide (in good faith)
  • de facto (in fact)
  • ex officio (by virtue of office)
  • in situ (in its original place)
  • per se (by itself)
  • prima facie (at first sight)
  • status quo (existing state)

Academic Latin Terms

Usually Italicized
  • ibid. (in the same place)
  • op. cit. (in the work cited)
  • passim (throughout)
  • sic (thus—used to indicate an error in quoted material)
  • vide (see)

Scientific Names

Always italicize genus and species names:

  • Homo sapiens (humans)
  • Canis familiaris (domestic dog)
  • Acer saccharum (sugar maple)

⚠️ Remember

When in doubt about Latin terms, check if they appear in a standard English dictionary. If they do, they usually don't need italics.

6.11 Names of Ships, Aircraft, Spacecraft, and Trains

Italicize the proper names of individual vehicles:

Ships and Boats

Example 6.11a

HMCS Halifax (but not HMCS)

The Queen Mary 2

The Bluenose II

MV Confederation

Aircraft and Spacecraft

Example 6.11b

The Spirit of St. Louis

Canadarm (when referring to specific unit)

Space Shuttle Endeavour

The Silver Dart (first Canadian aircraft)

Trains

Example 6.11c

The Canadian (VIA Rail)

The Ocean (Halifax to Montreal)

The Rocky Mountaineer

Do NOT Italicize

  • Types or classes: Boeing 747, Airbus A380
  • Prefixes: USS, HMS, HMCS, SS
  • Generic names: the space shuttle, the ferry
  • Brand names: Air Canada, WestJet
Note: Some publications italicize the prefixes along with the ship names (e.g., HMS Victory). Choose one style and be consistent.
