6.01 Basic Principles of Italics

‚ú® The Power of Emphasis

Italics are a powerful typographical tool for distinction, emphasis, and clarity in professional writing.

Primary Functions of Italics

üéØ Emphasis

Draw attention to important words or phrases

"This is absolutely critical."

üìö Titles

Distinguish titles of major works

Read The Great Gatsby for class.

üåç Foreign Terms

Mark non-English words and phrases

The French term laissez-faire means...

üî¨ Technical Terms

Highlight specialized terminology

The species Homo sapiens evolved...

When NOT to Use Italics

‚ùå Don't Overuse for Emphasis

Excessive italics lose their impact and make text hard to read.

This is very important and absolutely critical. This is absolutely critical.

‚ùå Don't Use for Common Words

Regular English words don't need italics unless emphasized for specific reasons.

The report was written yesterday. The report was written yesterday.

‚ùå Don't Mix with Other Emphasis

Avoid combining italics with bold, underline, or quotation marks for emphasis.

üí° Golden Rule

Use italics purposefully and sparingly. Every italic word should have a clear reason for being emphasized or distinguished.

6.02 Italics for Emphasis

üé™ Strategic Emphasis

Use italics for emphasis sparingly and strategically to maintain their effectiveness.

When to Use Italics for Emphasis

‚úÖ Clarifying Meaning

When the meaning would be unclear without emphasis:

"I said call him, not email him."

‚úÖ Contrasting Ideas

To highlight contrasts or comparisons:

"The policy applies to all employees, not just senior staff."

‚úÖ Unexpected Information

To draw attention to surprising or contrary information:

"Sales actually increased during the recession."

Alternatives to Italics for Emphasis

Sentence Structure

Instead of: "Quality is very important."
Try: "Quality is essential to our success."

Strong Vocabulary

Instead of: "This is really good."
Try: "This is exceptional."

Strategic Placement

Instead of: "The deadline is tomorrow."
Try: "Tomorrow is the deadline."

Professional Context Guidelines

üìÑ Formal Documents

Use italics for emphasis very sparingly in formal documents. Rely more on clear writing and structure.

üìß Email Communication

Occasional emphasis is acceptable in emails, but don't overuse. Consider your audience and relationship.

üìä Presentations

Use italics to highlight key points on slides, but combine with other design elements for maximum impact.

‚ö†Ô∏è Emphasis Fatigue

Too much emphasis creates "emphasis fatigue" where readers start ignoring italicized text. Use emphasis like seasoning - a little goes a long way.

üí° Consistency Rule

Be consistent throughout your document. If you italicize one newspaper name, italicize all newspaper names. Consistency helps readers understand your formatting system.

6.05 Technical and Scientific Terms

üî¨ Scientific Precision

In technical and scientific writing, italics serve specific functions for clarity and convention.

Scientific Names (Binomial Nomenclature)

Always Italicize Genus and Species

Scientific names of organisms follow the binomial system and are always italicized:

Animals
  • Homo sapiens (humans)
  • Canis lupus (gray wolf)
  • Felis catus (domestic cat)
Plants
  • Quercus alba (white oak)
  • Rosa rubiginosa (sweet briar)
  • Acer saccharum (sugar maple)
Microorganisms
  • Escherichia coli (E. coli bacteria)
  • Saccharomyces cerevisiae (baker's yeast)
  • Plasmodium falciparum (malaria parasite)

Formatting Rules for Scientific Names

üìù Capitalization

  • Genus: Always capitalized (Homo)
  • Species: Always lowercase (sapiens)
  • Both: Always italicized

‚úÇÔ∏è Abbreviations

After first mention, genus can be abbreviated:

"First mention: Escherichia coli
Subsequent mentions: E. coli"

üîó Higher Classifications

Higher taxonomic levels are not italicized:

"Family: Hominidae
Genus: Homo
Species: Homo sapiens"

Mathematical and Statistical Terms

Variables and Symbols

Mathematical variables are typically italicized:

  • The equation y = mx + b
  • Where n = sample size
  • The value of x approaches infinity

Statistical Notation

Statistical symbols and test names:

  • p < 0.05 (probability value)
  • t-test, F-test
  • r = 0.75 (correlation coefficient)

Chemical and Technical Terms

üß™ Chemical Names

Generally, chemical formulas and common chemical names are not italicized:

  • H‚ÇÇO (water)
  • NaCl (sodium chloride)
  • glucose, acetone, benzene

‚öôÔ∏è Technical Terms

Technical terms are usually not italicized unless they're foreign or being defined:

  • bandwidth, algorithm, protocol
  • But: The concept of Gestalt in design...

Field-Specific Guidelines

üß¨ Biology

  • Species names: always italicized
  • Gene names: varies by organism (check style guide)
  • Protein names: usually not italicized

üßÆ Mathematics

  • Variables: italicized
  • Functions: italicized (f(x))
  • Constants: may vary (œÄ, e)

üè• Medicine

  • Disease organisms: italicized
  • Drug names: not italicized
  • Anatomical terms: usually not italicized

‚ö†Ô∏è Discipline-Specific Rules

Different scientific disciplines have specific style guides (APA, ACS, AMA, etc.). Always check the appropriate style guide for your field, as rules can vary significantly.

6.06 Legal References

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Documentation Standards

Legal writing has specific conventions for using italics in case names, statutes, and legal documents.

Case Names

Always Italicize Case Names

Legal case names are conventionally italicized in legal writing:

  • Brown v. Board of Education
  • R. v. Morgentaler
  • Reference re Secession of Quebec
  • Donoghue v. Stevenson

Case Name Formatting

Full Citation: R. v. Oakes, [1986] 1 S.C.R. 103
In Text: The court in Oakes established...
Parenthetical: The test was established (R. v. Oakes)

Statutes and Legislation

Statute Names

Full statute names may be italicized, but common abbreviations are not:

Full name: Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms Short form: Charter
Full name: Criminal Code of Canada Short form: Criminal Code

Section References

Section numbers and references are not italicized:

  • Section 15 of the Charter
  • s. 718.2(a) of the Criminal Code
  • Subsection 2(b)

Legal Documents

üìã Court Documents

Names of specific legal documents may be italicized:

  • Statement of Claim
  • Notice of Motion
  • Factum

üìñ Legal Publications

Legal texts and publications follow standard title rules:

  • Canadian Constitutional Law (book)
  • Canadian Bar Review (journal)
  • "Constitutional Interpretation" (article)

Latin Legal Terms

Italicize Latin Phrases

Latin terms commonly used in legal writing are italicized:

stare decisis let the decision stand
res ipsa loquitur the thing speaks for itself
habeas corpus have the body
ultra vires beyond the powers

Established Terms

Some Latin terms are so common in legal writing they may not need italics:

  • per se
  • inter alia
  • prima facie

Check your jurisdiction's style guide for specific preferences.

Citation Formats

Complete Citation

Hunter v. Southam Inc., [1984] 2 S.C.R. 145, 11 D.L.R. (4th) 641.

Subsequent Reference

Hunter, supra note 15 at para. 17.

In Text Reference

The Supreme Court of Canada in Hunter v. Southam held that...

üí° Jurisdiction Matters

Legal citation styles vary by jurisdiction and court system. Always follow the specific citation guide required by your court, law school, or legal organization (e.g., McGill Guide in Canada, Bluebook in the US).

6.07 Special Uses

üé≠ Unique Applications

Italics have several specialized uses in different contexts and types of writing.

Words as Words (Metalinguistic Use)

Italicize Words Being Discussed

When discussing words, letters, or phrases as linguistic objects, use italics:

The word serendipity comes from a Persian fairy tale.
The letter e is the most common in English.
The phrase jumping the shark originated from a TV show.

Common Contexts

  • Grammar and style guides
  • Dictionary definitions
  • Language learning materials
  • Linguistic analysis
  • Editing and proofreading discussions

Stage Directions and Scripts

Italicize Stage Directions

In plays, screenplays, and scripts, stage directions are italicized:

HAMLET: To be or not to be, that is the question.
(pauses, looking at the skull)
Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer...

Other Script Elements

  • Enter MACBETH
  • Lights fade to black
  • Sound of thunder
  • She exits stage left

Introducing New Terms

Highlighting New Terminology

When introducing technical terms or concepts for the first time:

This process is called photosynthesis, the conversion of light energy into chemical energy.
The concept of cognitive dissonance explains the discomfort felt when holding contradictory beliefs.

Subsequent References

After the first introduction, the term typically returns to regular formatting:

"First use: Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence...
Later use: Machine learning algorithms require large datasets..."

Identifying Information

Names of Vehicles and Vessels

Names of specific ships, aircraft, spacecraft, and trains are italicized:

Ships
  • HMCS Halifax
  • Titanic
  • Queen Mary 2
Aircraft
  • Spirit of St. Louis
  • Air Force One
  • Enola Gay
Spacecraft
  • Apollo 11
  • Voyager 1
  • International Space Station

Generic References

Generic vehicle types are not italicized:

  • The aircraft landed safely
  • Several ships were in port
  • The spacecraft was launched

Digital and Modern Usage

Website and App Names

Major websites and applications are typically italicized:

  • Facebook, Twitter, Instagram
  • Google, YouTube, Wikipedia
  • Microsoft Word, Adobe Photoshop

Email and Digital Communication

In plain text emails where italics aren't available, use alternatives:

Underscores: _The Great Gatsby_
Asterisks: *The Great Gatsby*
Capitals: THE GREAT GATSBY
