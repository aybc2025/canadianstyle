16.01 Introduction

Good writing is rewriting. Revision and proofreading are essential steps in producing clear, error-free documents that achieve their intended purpose.

This chapter covers the systematic approach to improving your writing through multiple types of editing, each focusing on different aspects of the document.

The Four Levels of Editing

1. Structural Editing

Organization, content, and audience focus

2. Stylistic Editing

Clarity, flow, and tone

3. Copy Editing

Grammar, spelling, and consistency

4. Proofreading

Final check for errors and formatting

üí° Best Practice

Allow time between writing and editing. Fresh eyes catch more errors and see opportunities for improvement.

16.02 Structural Editing

Structural editing examines the document's foundation: its purpose, audience, and organization.

Key Questions for Structural Editing

  • Is the purpose clear and consistently maintained?
  • Is the content appropriate for the intended audience?
  • Is the information complete and accurate?
  • Is the organization logical and effective?
  • Are the main points clearly stated and well-supported?

Common Structural Issues

Problem Solution
Unclear purpose Add a clear statement of purpose early in the document
Missing information Research and add necessary content
Poor organization Reorganize using logical order (chronological, spatial, importance)
Weak conclusions Strengthen ending with clear recommendations or summary
Structural Editing in Action

Before: Report jumps between topics without clear transitions

After: Report follows logical progression with smooth transitions between related topics

16.03 Stylistic Editing

Stylistic editing improves clarity, flow, and readability while maintaining the author's voice.

Areas of Focus

  • Clarity: Eliminate ambiguity and confusion
  • Conciseness: Remove unnecessary words
  • Flow: Ensure smooth transitions
  • Tone: Match the audience and purpose
  • Voice: Maintain consistency throughout

Stylistic Techniques

Parallel Structure

Use consistent grammatical patterns in lists and series.

Example: "Reading, writing, and arithmetic" (not "Reading, writing, and to do arithmetic")

Varied Sentence Length

Mix short and long sentences for rhythm and emphasis.

Tip: Short sentences emphasize. Longer sentences provide detail and explanation.

Transitional Words

Use transitions to guide readers between ideas.

Examples: However, Furthermore, In contrast, As a result

16.04 Copy Editing

Copy editing focuses on correctness: grammar, spelling, punctuation, and consistency.

Copy Editing Checklist

Grammar

  • Subject-verb agreement
  • Pronoun reference and agreement
  • Verb tense consistency
  • Sentence fragments and run-ons
  • Dangling and misplaced modifiers

Spelling & Usage

  • Spell-check (but don't rely on it alone)
  • Commonly confused words
  • Proper nouns and names
  • Technical terms and jargon
  • Abbreviations and acronyms

Punctuation

  • Comma usage (series, clauses)
  • Apostrophes (possessives, contractions)
  • Quotation marks and citations
  • Semicolons and colons
  • Hyphens and dashes

Consistency

  • Style guide compliance
  • Number formats
  • Date and time formats
  • Capitalization patterns
  • Heading styles

Common Copy Editing Errors

Error Type Incorrect Correct
Subject-verb agreement The team are meeting The team is meeting
Apostrophe misuse It's motor is running Its motor is running
Confused words The effect of the policy The affect of the policy
Comma splice It's raining, I'll stay inside It's raining, so I'll stay inside

16.05 Proofreading

Proofreading is the final stage of editing, focusing on catching any remaining errors and ensuring proper formatting.

Proofreading Strategies

Read Backwards

Start from the end and read sentence by sentence to catch spelling and word errors without being distracted by content.

Read Aloud

Reading aloud forces you to slow down and helps catch awkward phrasing and missing words.

Use a Ruler

Place a ruler under each line to focus your attention and prevent skipping lines.

Print It Out

Many people catch more errors on paper than on screen. Change your medium for a fresh perspective.

Check One Thing at a Time

Make multiple passes, each time looking for specific types of errors (spelling, then punctuation, then formatting).

Fresh Eyes

If possible, have someone else proofread your work. They'll see errors you've become blind to.

What to Look For

Typography

  • Consistent font usage
  • Proper heading hierarchy
  • Correct emphasis (bold, italic)
  • Alignment and spacing

Numbers & Dates

  • Consistent number formatting
  • Correct date formats
  • Accurate page numbers
  • Sequential numbering

Cross-References

  • Accurate page references
  • Correct section numbers
  • Working hyperlinks
  • Matching table of contents

Final Details

  • Headers and footers
  • Widow and orphan lines
  • Image placement and quality
  • Overall visual appearance

Tips for Effective Revision

  • Take breaks: Distance yourself from your writing between drafts
  • Read with purpose: Focus on one type of issue at a time
  • Question everything: Is each word, sentence, and paragraph necessary?
  • Consider your reader: Will they understand and find value in your message?
  • Be ruthless: Cut unnecessary content, even if you like it
  • Verify facts: Double-check all data, quotes, and references
