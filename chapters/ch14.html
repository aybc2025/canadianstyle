14

Elimination of Stereotyping

Write inclusively and avoid bias in your communications

14.01 Introduction 14.02-14.08 Sexual Stereotyping 14.09-14.12 Racial & Ethnic 14.13-14.14 Disabilities

14.01 Introduction

Fair and representative writing treats all people with respect and dignity. Language evolves to reflect society's values, and contemporary writing should avoid stereotypes and promote inclusivity.

This chapter provides guidelines for eliminating bias in government writing, covering gender, race, ethnicity, disability, and other characteristics that should not define how we refer to people.

Key Principles

  • Put the person first, not the characteristic
  • Use language people prefer to describe themselves
  • Avoid assumptions about roles and capabilities
  • Be specific rather than using generalizations
  • Keep current with evolving terminology

14.02-14.08 Sexual Stereotyping

14.02 Avoid Gender Assumptions

Don't assume someone's gender based on their occupation, role, or situation:

❌ Avoid

businessman, chairman, fireman
the nurse... she
the doctor... he

✅ Prefer

businessperson, chair, firefighter
the nurse... they
the doctor... they

14.03 Job Titles

Use gender-neutral job titles:

Instead of Use
manpower staff, workforce, personnel
man-made synthetic, artificial, manufactured
mankind humanity, humankind, people
spokesman spokesperson, representative
workman worker, employee

14.04 Pronouns

When the gender is unknown or irrelevant, use:

  • Plural pronouns: "All employees must submit their reports"
  • Singular "they": "Each student should bring their textbook"
  • Omit the pronoun: "The manager who signs the form"
  • Use "you" when appropriate: "When you apply, include..."

14.05-14.08 Additional Guidelines

Parallel Treatment

Treat people equally in parallel situations. If you use titles for men, use them for women too.

Avoid Diminutives

Avoid terms like "gal," "girl," or "lady" when referring to adult women in professional contexts.

Family References

Avoid mentioning family relationships unless relevant: "Dr. Smith" not "Dr. Smith, mother of two."

14.09-14.12 Racial & Ethnic Considerations

14.09 People-First Language

Avoid defining people by their race or ethnicity unless relevant:

❌ Avoid

the Black lawyer
Asian students
Indigenous protesters

✅ When Relevant

the lawyer, who is Black
students of Asian heritage
Indigenous people protesting

14.10 Preferred Terms

Use the terms people prefer for themselves:

Indigenous Peoples

  • Indigenous peoples (when referring to all)
  • First Nations, Inuit, Métis (when specific)
  • Avoid: "Indian" (except in legal contexts)

Other Groups

  • Use specific terms when known: "Chinese Canadian" rather than "Asian"
  • Respect self-identification
  • Avoid outdated or offensive terms

14.11 Cultural Sensitivity

Be aware of cultural implications:

  • Avoid stereotypical assumptions about cultures
  • Don't assume religious practices
  • Respect naming conventions
  • Be specific rather than using broad generalizations

14.12 Context Matters

Only mention race or ethnicity when:

  • It's relevant to the subject matter
  • It provides necessary context
  • It serves a legitimate communication purpose
  • The person has specifically requested its inclusion

14.13-14.14 Writing About Disabilities

14.13 Person-First Language

Put the person before the disability:

Instead of Use
disabled person person with a disability
wheelchair-bound person who uses a wheelchair
suffers from has, experiences, lives with
normal, able-bodied people without disabilities
mentally retarded person with an intellectual disability
crippled, lame person with a mobility impairment
crazy, insane person with a mental health condition
victim of person who has/experiences
birth defect congenital disability

14.14 Full Range of Human Characteristics

Show people with disabilities as complete individuals:

  • Focus on the person's role or contribution
  • Avoid emotional language ("brave," "courageous," "inspiring")
  • Don't assume limitations or capabilities
  • Mention disability only when relevant
  • Use neutral, factual language

Best Practices

  • Ask how people prefer to be described
  • Avoid metaphors that stereotype ("blind to the truth")
  • Don't use disability terms as general descriptors
  • Remember that disability is one aspect of a person's identity
