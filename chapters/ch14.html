14.01 Introduction

Fair and representative writing treats all people with respect and dignity. Government communications must be inclusive and free from bias.

üåç Why Inclusive Language Matters

Language shapes perception and reflects values. Inclusive writing ensures that all Canadians feel respected and represented in government communications.

Core Principles

  • Courtesy and Respect: Treat all individuals with dignity
  • Equal Treatment: Ensure equal representation in written material
  • Full Participation: Depict all individuals as active members of society
  • Accuracy: Use current, appropriate terminology

Areas of Focus

This chapter covers:

  • Gender-inclusive language
  • Racial and cultural sensitivity
  • Respectful language about disabilities
  • Age-appropriate references
  • General principles for inclusive writing

14.02 Gender-Inclusive Language

14.02 Avoiding Gender-Specific References

Use inclusive language that does not assume or reinforce gender stereotypes.

Strategies for Inclusion

  • Use neutral job titles: firefighter, not fireman
  • Alternate pronouns: "he or she" when specific
  • Restructure sentences: Use plurals to avoid gendered pronouns
  • Use "they": As singular pronoun when appropriate

14.11 Cultural Sensitivity

Be aware of cultural implications:

  • Avoid stereotypical assumptions about cultures
  • Don't assume religious practices
  • Respect naming conventions
  • Be specific rather than using broad generalizations

14.12 Context Matters

Only mention race or ethnicity when:

  • It's relevant to the subject matter
  • It provides necessary context
  • It serves a legitimate communication purpose
  • The person has specifically requested its inclusion

14.05 Age-Related Language

14.16 Avoiding Age Discrimination

Use age-neutral language that doesn't reinforce stereotypes about different age groups.

Guidelines

  • Avoid assumptions: Don't assume capabilities based on age
  • Use specific terms: "person aged 65" rather than "elderly person"
  • Respect dignity: Avoid patronizing language
  • Focus on relevant factors: Mention age only when necessary

Preferred Terms

Instead of Consider
elderly, aged older adults, seniors
old-timer experienced employee
youngster young person
over the hill (avoid - use specific age if needed)

Examples in Context

‚úì Good: "Canadians aged 65 and over are eligible for Old Age Security"

‚úó Avoid: "Elderly Canadians can apply for Old Age Security"

‚úì Good: "Young adults between 18 and 25 may be eligible"

üí° Best Practice

When age is relevant, use specific ranges rather than general terms. Focus on the service or information, not stereotypes about age groups.
