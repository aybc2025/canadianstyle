14

Elimination of Stereotyping

Write inclusively and avoid bias in your communications

14.01 Introduction 14.02-14.08 Sexual Stereotyping 14.09-14.12 Racial & Ethnic 14.13-14.14 Disabilities

14.01 Introduction

Fair and representative writing treats all people with respect and dignity. This chapter provides guidelines for eliminating stereotyping based on sex, race, ethnicity, and disability from your written communications.

Why This Matters

  • Accuracy: Stereotypes are often inaccurate generalizations
  • Respect: Everyone deserves to be represented fairly
  • Legal compliance: Human rights legislation requires non-discrimination
  • Credibility: Inclusive writing enhances your professional reputation
  • Audience reach: Respectful language connects with diverse readers
Key Principle

Focus on individuals' relevant characteristics, abilities, and qualifications—not on irrelevant personal attributes or group membership.

General Guidelines

  1. Relevance: Mention personal characteristics only when relevant
  2. Balance: Ensure equal treatment of all groups
  3. Individuality: Avoid generalizations about groups
  4. Current terms: Use the language groups prefer for themselves
  5. Person-first: Emphasize the person, not the characteristic

Remember: Language evolves. Stay informed about current preferred terminology and be willing to update your usage as societal understanding progresses.

14.02–14.08 Elimination of Sexual Stereotyping

Treat all people equally regardless of gender. Avoid assumptions about roles, abilities, or characteristics based on gender.

14.02 Correspondence: Names and Forms of Address

  • Use "Ms." as the default for women (parallels "Mr." for men)
  • Use professional titles when known: Dr., Prof., Rev.
  • If uncertain about gender, use the full name: Dear Pat Johnson
  • For unknown recipients: Dear Hiring Manager, Dear Customer Service

14.03 Parallel Treatment

Refer to men and women in parallel ways:

Parallel Treatment

Unequal: Mr. Johnson and Linda attended the meeting.

Equal: Mr. Johnson and Ms. Chen attended the meeting.

Equal: Robert Johnson and Linda Chen attended the meeting.


Unequal: Dr. Smith and his wife

Equal: Dr. Smith and Dr. Jones

Equal: John Smith and Maria Jones

14.04 Pronouns

Avoid using "he" as a generic pronoun. Options include:

1. Use Plural

Instead of: Each manager must submit his report.

Use: All managers must submit their reports.

2. Use "You"

Instead of: The applicant should send his resume.

Use: You should send your resume.

3. Repeat the Noun

Instead of: When the auditor arrives, he will need access.

Use: When the auditor arrives, the auditor will need access.

4. Use "They" (Singular)

Modern usage: Each student should submit their assignment.

Note: Singular "they" is increasingly accepted for gender-neutral reference.

14.06 Titles of Occupations

Use gender-neutral job titles:

Avoid Use
businessman businessperson, executive
chairman chair, chairperson
fireman firefighter
mailman letter carrier, mail carrier
mankind humanity, humankind, people
manpower workforce, staff, personnel
policeman police officer

stewardess flight attendant
workman worker, employee

14.08 Full Range of Human Characteristics

Show all genders in diverse roles and situations:

  • Avoid stereotypical examples (male CEOs, female secretaries)
  • Use diverse names in examples
  • Show both genders in caregiving and leadership roles
  • Avoid assumptions about family structures

14.09–14.12 Elimination of Racial and Ethnic Stereotyping

Respect cultural diversity and avoid perpetuating stereotypes about racial or ethnic groups.

14.09 Ethnic Clichés

Avoid expressions that stereotype ethnic groups:

⚠️ Avoid These Stereotypes

  • Attributing specific traits to entire ethnic groups
  • Using outdated or offensive terms
  • Making assumptions about abilities based on ethnicity
  • Using ethnic references as metaphors

14.10 Gratuitous Modifiers

Mention ethnicity only when relevant:

Example 14.10

Unnecessary: The Chinese doctor examined the patient.

Better: The doctor examined the patient.


Relevant: The program seeks Indigenous nurses to serve northern communities.

(Ethnicity is relevant to the program's purpose)

14.12 Identification of Groups

Use the names groups prefer for themselves:

Canadian Context

  • Indigenous Peoples: Includes First Nations, Inuit, and Métis
  • First Nations: Replaces "Indian" (except in legal contexts)
  • Inuit: Not "Eskimo" (singular: Inuk)
  • Métis: People of mixed Indigenous and European ancestry

General Principles

  • Be specific when possible (e.g., Cree, Mi'kmaq, rather than just "Indigenous")
  • Capitalize names of peoples and languages
  • Stay current with evolving terminology
  • When unsure, ask what people prefer

Avoiding Ethnocentrism

  • Don't assume your cultural perspective is universal
  • Be aware of cultural differences in communication styles
  • Avoid "us vs. them" language
  • Recognize Canada's multicultural reality

14.13–14.14 Fair and Representative Depiction of People with Disabilities

Focus on the person, not the disability. Use respectful language that emphasizes abilities and individuality.

14.13 Person-First Language

Put the person before the disability:

Person-First Examples

Not: disabled person, handicapped person

Use: person with a disability


Not: wheelchair-bound, confined to a wheelchair

Use: person who uses a wheelchair


Not: the blind, the deaf

Use: people who are blind, people who are deaf

Respectful Terminology

Avoid Use
suffers from, afflicted with has, lives with
normal, able-bodied people without disabilities
mentally retarded person with an intellectual disability
crippled, lame person with a mobility impairment
crazy, insane person with a mental health condition
victim of person who has/experiences
birth defect congenital disability

14.14 Full Range of Human Characteristics

Show people with disabilities as complete individuals:

  • Diverse roles: Show people with disabilities in various professions and situations
  • Avoid inspiration porn: Don't portray people as "brave" or "inspiring" just for living with a disability
  • Include naturally: Include people with disabilities in examples without making disability the focus
  • Respect autonomy: Don't assume people need help or can't speak for themselves

Accessibility in Writing

Make your documents accessible:

💡 Accessibility Tips

  • Use clear headings and structure
  • Provide alt text for images
  • Ensure sufficient color contrast
  • Use simple, clear language
  • Avoid relying solely on color to convey information
  • Make links descriptive (not "click here")

Remember

Key Takeaway

Disability is just one aspect of a person's identity. Focus on what's relevant to your communication, and always prioritize respect and dignity.
