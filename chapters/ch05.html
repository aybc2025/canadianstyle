5.01 Basic Rules

Consistent number expression improves document clarity and professionalism. Follow these basic guidelines for most numerical expressions.

Fundamental Number Rules

  • One to nine: Spell out (one, two, three...nine)
  • 10 and above: Use numerals (10, 11, 25, 100)
  • Consistency: Use same style throughout a sentence/paragraph
  • Round numbers: May be spelled out (ten million, one billion)

Number Expression Examples

‚úì Correct:

The committee reviewed three reports and 15 recommendations.

‚úó Incorrect:

The committee reviewed 3 reports and fifteen recommendations.

‚úì Round Numbers:

The budget totaled ten million dollars, not $10,000,000.

‚ö†Ô∏è Exception Guidelines

Always use numerals with technical terms, measurements, percentages, and in scientific contexts, regardless of the basic rule.

5.02 Numbers at Sentence Beginning

Numbers that begin sentences should be spelled out, regardless of their size. If this creates awkward phrasing, rewrite the sentence.

Beginning-of-Sentence Rules

  • Always spell out: Numbers at the start of sentences
  • Rewrite if awkward: Very large numbers should prompt sentence revision
  • Consistency: Related numbers in the same sentence follow the spelled-out style

Sentence Beginning Examples

‚úì Correct:

Twenty-five participants attended the conference.

‚úó Incorrect:

25 participants attended the conference.

‚úì Better (rewritten):

The conference attracted 25 participants.

‚úì Awkward large numbers - rewrite:

Instead of: "Three thousand four hundred fifty-seven applications were received..."
Write: "The department received 3,457 applications..."

5.03 Money & Measurements

Money and measurement expressions follow specific conventions in government documents. Always use numerals with currency symbols and measurement units.

Money Expression Rules

  • Currency symbol: $5, $25, $1,000, $1 million
  • Cents alone: 25 cents or 25¬¢
  • Mixed amounts: $5.25 or $5.25 million
  • Even dollars: $5 (not $5.00 in text)

Money Examples

‚úì Correct:

The budget includes $2.5 million for infrastructure and $500,000 for maintenance.

‚úó Incorrect:

The budget includes $2,500,000.00 for infrastructure.

Measurement Rules

  • Always use numerals: with measurement units
  • SI/metric preferred: in Canadian government documents
  • Space between number and unit: 25 kg, 10 m, 5¬∞C
  • No plural units: 5 km (not 5 kms)

5.04 Dates & Time

Date and time expressions require consistency and clarity. Follow Canadian conventions for date formatting.

Date Format Guidelines

  • Standard format: Month Day, Year (January 15, 2024)
  • Numerical format: YYYY-MM-DD (2024-01-15) - ISO standard
  • Avoid: MM/DD/YY format (American style)
  • Time: Use 24-hour clock for formal documents

Date & Time Examples

‚úì Preferred:

The meeting is scheduled for March 15, 2024, at 14:30.

‚úì ISO Format:

Report date: 2024-03-15

‚úó Avoid:

Meeting: 3/15/24 at 2:30 PM

üí° Time Zone Considerations

Always specify time zones for meetings or deadlines that involve multiple locations: "The deadline is 17:00 EST on March 15, 2024."

5.05 Percentages & Statistics

Statistical information and percentages require precise formatting to ensure accuracy and clarity in government communications.

Percentage Guidelines

  • Always use numerals: with percent (5 percent, 25 percent)
  • Symbol in technical contexts: Use % in tables and technical documents
  • Spell out "percent": in formal prose
  • Decimal precision: Use appropriate decimal places (5.2 percent)

Statistical Expression Examples

‚úì Formal writing:

The response rate improved by 15 percent over the previous year.

‚úì Technical/tabular:

Response rates: 2023: 78.5%, 2024: 83.2%

Statistical Reporting Standards

  • Sample sizes: Always report (n = 1,247)
  • Confidence intervals: Include when relevant (95% CI: 2.1-3.7)
  • Rounding: Be consistent throughout document
  • Significant figures: Match precision to measurement accuracy

5.06 SI/Metric System

The International System of Units (SI) is Canada's official measurement system. Understanding proper SI usage is essential for government documents.

SI Fundamental Principles

  • Seven base units: metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, candela
  • Decimal system: All conversions by powers of 10
  • Standard symbols: Lowercase unless derived from proper names
  • No plurals: 5 km, not 5 kms

5.08 Special Applications

Certain contexts require special attention to numerical expression, including technical documents, legal texts, and statistical reports.

Technical Document Guidelines

  • Scientific notation: Use for very large or small numbers
  • Precision: Match significant figures to measurement accuracy
  • Units: Always include appropriate units
  • Ranges: Use appropriate notation (5-10 kg, 5 to 10 kg)

Special Application Examples

Scientific notation:

The distance is approximately 3.84 √ó 10‚Å∏ m.

Legal documents:

The fine shall not exceed five thousand dollars ($5,000).

Statistical reporting:

Response rate: 78.3% (95% CI: 75.1-81.5%, n = 2,847)

Context-Specific Rules

üìä Statistical Reports

  • Include sample sizes
  • Report confidence intervals
  • Use consistent decimal places
  • Indicate statistical significance

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Documents

  • Spell out amounts in contracts
  • Use both figures and words for clarity
  • Follow jurisdictional conventions
  • Ensure precision in penalties/fines

üî¨ Scientific Papers

  • Use scientific notation when appropriate
  • Include measurement uncertainty
  • Follow discipline-specific standards
  • Maintain significant figure consistency
