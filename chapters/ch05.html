5.01 Basic Number Rules

üî¢ The Foundation Rule

Understanding when to spell out numbers versus using numerals is essential for professional writing.

The Basic Rule

Spell Out:

Numbers one through nine

  • five reports
  • nine participants
  • three meetings

Use Numerals:

Numbers 10 and above

  • 15 departments
  • 25 employees
  • 100 applications

Consistency Within Sentences

‚úÖ Consistent "We ordered 5 computers, 15 chairs, and 25 desks." All numerals because one number is 10+
‚ùå Inconsistent "We ordered five computers, 15 chairs, and 25 desks." Mixed styles in same context

Large Numbers

Combine Words and Numerals for Clarity

  • 2.5 million (not 2,500,000)
  • $4.2 billion
  • 15.6 thousand

Use Commas in Full Numerals

  • 1,250 employees
  • 15,000 applications
  • 2,500,000 voters

üí° Memory Device

If you can count it on your fingers (1-9), spell it out. If you need more than your fingers (10+), use numerals.

5.02 Numbers at Sentence Beginning

‚úèÔ∏è Starting Strong

Numbers at the beginning of sentences require special handling for readability.

The Cardinal Rule

Always spell out numbers that begin a sentence

This applies regardless of the basic number rule.

Examples and Solutions

Preferred: Spell Out

Fifteen participants attended the workshop.
Twenty-five percent of respondents agreed.
One hundred applications were received.

Alternative: Rewrite the Sentence

Instead of: "25 people attended"
Write: "A total of 25 people attended"
Instead of: "1,500 responses were collected"
Write: "We collected 1,500 responses"

Special Cases

Very Large Numbers

For very large numbers, rewriting is often clearer:

Awkward: "Two million five hundred thousand dollars was allocated" Better: "The allocation was $2.5 million"

Years and Dates

Avoid starting sentences with years:

Avoid: "2023 was a challenging year" Prefer: "The year 2023 was challenging"

‚ö†Ô∏è Never Do This

Never start a sentence with a numeral: "25 people attended" ‚ùå

Always spell out or rewrite: "Twenty-five people attended" ‚úÖ or "A group of 25 people attended" ‚úÖ

5.03 Money and Measurements

üí∞ Precision Matters

Money and measurements follow specific rules that override the basic number guidelines.

Money Rules

Always Use Numerals for Money

Regardless of amount, monetary values use numerals:

  • $5 (not five dollars)
  • $15.50
  • $1,250
  • $2.5 million

Currency Formatting

Canadian Dollars: $125.50, $1,250
Other Currencies: ‚Ç¨100, ¬•500, ¬£75
No Decimals for Whole Amounts: $25 (not $25.00)

Measurement Rules

Metric Measurements

Always use numerals with metric units:

  • 5 metres (not five metres)
  • 25 kilometres
  • 3.5 litres
  • 150 grams

Imperial Measurements

Also use numerals with imperial units:

  • 5 feet 6 inches
  • 25 pounds
  • 3 miles
  • 2 gallons

Unit Abbreviations

Common Metric Abbreviations

kilometre km
metre m
centimetre cm
kilogram kg
litre L

Abbreviation Guidelines

  • No periods in metric abbreviations: km, not km.
  • Same abbreviation for singular and plural: 1 kg, 5 kg
  • Space between number and unit: 25 km (not 25km)

üí° Professional Tip

In technical writing, always use numerals with units of measurement, even for numbers under 10. This ensures precision and consistency.

Time Formats

Standard Time Format

  • 9:30 a.m. (with periods)
  • 2:15 p.m.
  • 12:00 noon (or simply "noon")
  • 12:00 midnight (or simply "midnight")

24-Hour Format

  • 09:30 (9:30 a.m.)
  • 14:15 (2:15 p.m.)
  • 23:45 (11:45 p.m.)

Common in military, medical, and international contexts

Ordinal Numbers in Dates

‚ùå Avoid Ordinals in Standard Dates

‚úÖ Use Ordinals in Text

‚ö†Ô∏è International Considerations

Be aware that DD/MM/YYYY format is common internationally, while MM/DD/YYYY is North American. Always clarify when ambiguity is possible: 03/04/2024 could be March 4 or April 3.

5.05 Percentages and Statistics

üìä Data Communication

Statistical information requires precise formatting to communicate data clearly and professionally.

Percentage Rules

Always Use Numerals with Percentages

Regardless of the basic number rule, percentages always use numerals:

  • 5% (not five percent)
  • 25.5%
  • 0.3%
  • 100%

Percent vs. % Symbol

Use % Symbol When:
  • In technical documents
  • In tables and charts
  • In statistical reports
  • Space is limited
Spell Out "Percent" When:
  • In formal writing
  • In narrative text
  • Following organizational style

Decimal Places in Statistics

Choose Appropriate Precision

General reporting: 25% (whole numbers)
Detailed analysis: 25.3% (one decimal)
Scientific data: 25.34% (two decimals)

Maintain Consistency

Within a document, use the same level of precision:

‚úÖ Consistent: 25%, 30%, 45% ‚ùå Mixed: 25%, 30.0%, 45.2%

Statistical Expressions

Ratios and Proportions

  • 3:1 ratio (or 3 to 1)
  • 2 out of 3 respondents
  • 1 in 4 participants

Ranges and Approximations

  • between 25% and 30%
  • approximately 50%
  • more than 75%
  • fewer than 10%

Fractional Expressions

  • one-half (50%)
  • one-quarter (25%)
  • two-thirds (67%)
  • three-quarters (75%)

Large Numbers in Statistics

Combine Numerals and Words for Clarity

2.5 million people not 2,500,000 people
$4.2 billion budget not $4,200,000,000 budget
15.6 thousand responses not 15,600 responses

üí° Data Presentation Tip

When presenting multiple statistics, use parallel structure and consistent formatting to help readers compare data easily.
