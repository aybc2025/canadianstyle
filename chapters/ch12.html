12.01 Introduction

Correct word usage enhances clarity and credibility in professional writing. This chapter addresses commonly confused words and expressions in Canadian English.

Usage Principles

  • Choose precise words that convey your exact meaning
  • Understand subtle differences between similar terms
  • Use Canadian conventions and spelling
  • Avoid common errors that weaken your message
  • Consider your audience and context

üí° When in Doubt

Keep a current Canadian dictionary handy. Usage evolves, and staying current with accepted conventions helps maintain professional credibility.

Location and Direction

Specific Contexts

  • At vs In: At the meeting (event), In the room (location)
  • To vs Into: Go to the office, Walk into the building
  • Between vs Among: Between two options, Among several choices
  • On vs Upon: On the table (simple location), Upon reflection (formal)

12.03 Affect vs Effect

Basic Distinction

Affect is usually a verb; Effect is usually a noun:

‚úÖ Affect (Verb)

The new policy will affect all employees.
Budget cuts affected our timeline.
How did the change affect productivity?

‚úÖ Effect (Noun)

The effect of the policy was immediate.
What effect did the cuts have?
The effects were far-reaching.

Memory Aids

Remember:

  • Affect = Action (verb)
  • Effect = End result (noun)
  • Cause and effect: The cause affects; the effect follows
  • Test: Can you substitute "influence"? Use affect

Less Common Uses

‚úÖ Effect as Verb

The new director will effect change.
(meaning: bring about, cause)

‚úÖ Affect as Noun

The patient showed little affect.
(psychology term: emotional response)

12.04 Compose vs Comprise

Key Distinction

Compose means "to make up"; Comprise means "to include" or "to consist of":

‚úÖ Compose

Ten provinces compose Canada.
(The parts make up the whole)
Five chapters compose this section.

‚úÖ Comprise

Canada comprises ten provinces.
(The whole includes the parts)
This section comprises five chapters.

Memory Aid

Direction Test:

  • Parts compose the whole (upward direction)
  • Whole comprises the parts (downward direction)
  • Never: "comprised of" (redundant)
  • Alternative: Use "consists of" instead of comprise

Common Errors

‚ùå Incorrect

Canada is comprised of ten provinces.
The team is comprised of experts.

‚úÖ Correct

Canada comprises ten provinces.
Ten provinces compose Canada.
The team consists of experts.

12.05 Less vs Fewer

Count vs Non-Count

Use fewer with countable items; use less with uncountable quantities:

‚úÖ Fewer (Countable)

Fewer employees attended
Fewer meetings were scheduled
Fewer applications were received
Fewer than 20 people

‚úÖ Less (Uncountable)

Less time was available
Less money was spent
Less information was provided
Less than $100

Test Method

Quick Check:

  • Can you count it? Use fewer
  • Is it a mass/quantity? Use less
  • Would you use "many"? Use fewer
  • Would you use "much"? Use less

Special Cases

‚úÖ Time and Distance

Less than three hours
Less than 50 kilometres
(Time and distance are measured as quantities)

‚úÖ Money

Less than $50
Fewer dollars
(Money as amount vs. individual units)

12.06 That vs Which

Restrictive vs Non-Restrictive

Use that for essential information (no commas); use which for additional information (with commas):

‚úÖ That (Restrictive)

The report that you requested is ready.
(Specifies which report)
Policies that affect safety are prioritized.

‚úÖ Which (Non-Restrictive)

The report, which took weeks to complete, is ready.
(Adds extra information)
The new policy, which affects all staff, starts Monday.

Test Method

Essential Information Test:

  • Can you remove the clause? If yes, use "which" with commas
  • Is the clause essential for meaning? If yes, use "that" without commas
  • Does it identify which one? Use "that"
  • Does it add extra details? Use "which"

Who vs That

‚úÖ People

Employees who work overtime...
(Preferred for people)
Employees that work overtime...
(Acceptable but less preferred)

‚úÖ Things

Documents that require approval...
Equipment which needs maintenance...

12.07 Common Usage Errors

Frequently Confused Words

‚úÖ Accept vs Except

Accept: receive willingly
Except: excluding
"I accept all terms except clause 5."

‚úÖ Advice vs Advise

Advice: noun (recommendation)
Advise: verb (to recommend)
"I advise you to follow this advice."

‚úÖ Principal vs Principle

Principal: main, chief, head person
Principle: rule, belief
"The principal reason follows this principle."

‚úÖ Compliment vs Complement

Compliment: praise
Complement: complete, go well with
"The data complements the report nicely."

Redundant Expressions

Avoid These Redundancies:

  • Instead of "past history": history
  • Instead of "future plans": plans
  • Instead of "advance warning": warning
  • Instead of "end result": result
  • Instead of "close proximity": proximity

Wordy Expressions

12.08 Canadian Idioms and Expressions

Some expressions differ between Canadian and American English:

‚úÖ Canadian Preferences

Government and Legal Terms

Canadian Conventions:

  • First Nations (preferred over Native or Indian)
