12.01 Introduction to Usage

Proper usage ensures clarity, precision, and professionalism in government writing. This chapter addresses common usage problems and provides guidance for choosing the right words and expressions in Canadian government communications.

Usage Principles

  • Precision in word choice and meaning
  • Standard Canadian English conventions
  • Clarity for diverse audiences
  • Consistency within documents and departments

Common Usage Challenges

  • Word confusion: affect/effect, less/fewer
  • Preposition choice: different from/than
  • Verb agreement: none is/are
  • Pronoun reference: that/which

Canadian Context

  • Balance British and American influences
  • Recognize regional variations
  • Reflect official bilingualism
  • Serve diverse populations

12.02 Prepositional Usage

Common Prepositional Challenges

Prepositions show relationships between words, but the correct choice often depends on context and idiomatic usage.

Frequently Confused Prepositions:

Different from vs. Different than
Preferred (Canadian):
  • This policy is different from the previous one
  • Our approach differs from theirs
  • The results were different from expectations
Acceptable (before clauses):
  • The outcome was different than we expected
  • Results were different than predicted
Between vs. Among
Between (two items or distinct groups):
  • Agreement between Canada and the US
  • Choose between the two options
  • Negotiations between the three provinces
Among (three or more in a group):
  • Distribute the funds among the departments
  • Discussion among committee members
  • Consensus among stakeholders
In vs. Into
In (location, position):
  • The minister is in the office
  • Files are in the cabinet
  • We are in agreement
Into (motion, change):
  • Walk into the conference room
  • Translate the policy into action
  • Divide into three groups

Preposition Guidelines

  • Avoid redundancy: "Where is it at?" ‚Üí "Where is it?"
  • Don't strand unnecessarily: "This is what I'm referring to" is acceptable
  • Use standard combinations: comply with, different from, independent of
  • Consider the relationship: time, place, manner, cause

Affect vs. Effect

The Basic Distinction

Affect is usually a verb (action). Effect is usually a noun (result).

AFFECT (verb) = to influence or change
  • The new policy will affect all employees
  • Budget cuts may affect program delivery
  • Weather conditions affected the schedule
  • How will this decision affect our timeline?

Memory aid: Affect = Action (verb)

EFFECT (noun) = result or consequence
  • The effect of the policy was positive
  • Budget cuts had a negative effect
  • The weather had no effect on attendance
  • What is the effect of this decision?

Memory aid: Effect = End result (noun)

Less Common Uses:

Effect as a verb (to bring about):
  • The new minister hopes to effect change
  • We need to effect reforms quickly
Affect as a noun (psychology - emotional state):
  • The patient showed a flat affect
  • Her affect was appropriate to the situation

Quick Test:

  • Try substituting "influence" ‚Üí use affect
  • Try substituting "result" ‚Üí use effect
  • Look for "the" before the word ‚Üí usually effect
  • Look for "will" before the word ‚Üí usually affect

Compose vs. Comprise

The Key Distinction

Compose = to make up, form. Comprise = to include, contain.

COMPOSE = to make up or form (parts ‚Üí whole)
  • Ten provinces compose Canada
  • Three departments compose the ministry
  • These elements compose the final report
  • Five members compose the committee

Alternative: "Canada is composed of ten provinces"

COMPRISE = to include or contain (whole ‚Üí parts)
  • Canada comprises ten provinces
  • The ministry comprises three departments
  • The report comprises five chapters
  • The committee comprises five members

Avoid: "comprised of" (redundant)

Memory Aids:

Direction of Relationship:
  • Compose: Parts ‚Üí Whole (multiple things make one thing)
  • Comprise: Whole ‚Üí Parts (one thing contains multiple things)
Substitution Test:
  • Compose: Can substitute "make up" or "form"
  • Comprise: Can substitute "include" or "contain"

Common Errors to Avoid:

‚úÖ The committee comprises five members

‚úÖ The committee is composed of five members

‚ùå Five members comprise the committee

‚úÖ Five members compose the committee

‚úÖ The committee comprises five members

Less vs. Fewer

The Countability Rule

Fewer for things you can count. Less for things you can't count or measure as quantity.

FEWER = countable items (discrete units)
  • Fewer employees attended the meeting
  • We received fewer applications this year
  • Fewer than 20 people responded
  • The department has fewer resources

Use with: people, dollars, hours, pages, departments, applications

LESS = uncountable quantities (mass nouns)
  • We have less time than expected
  • Less funding is available this year
  • The project requires less effort
  • There was less interest in the proposal

Use with: time, money, water, information, support, interest

Special Cases and Exceptions:

Distance, time, and money (as units):
  • Less than 10 kilometres (distance as unit)
  • Less than 30 minutes (time as unit)
  • Less than $100 (money as amount)
Percentages and fractions:
  • Less than 50 per cent
  • Less than half the budget
  • Less than one-third of participants
Emphasis on quantity vs. number:
  • "Fewer people" (counting individuals)
  • "Less staff" (thinking of staff as collective resource)

Quick Test:

The Plural Test:

If you can make the noun plural, use fewer

Example: application ‚Üí applications = use "fewer applications"

The "Amount of" Test:

If you would say "amount of," use less

If you would say "number of," use fewer

That vs. Which

The Essential vs. Non-essential Rule

That introduces essential information (no commas). Which introduces non-essential information (with commas).

THAT = essential/restrictive (no commas)

Essential information that restricts or defines which specific thing you're talking about.

  • The policy that was approved yesterday takes effect immediately
  • Employees that work overtime receive compensation
  • Reports that contain errors must be revised
  • The department that handles immigration is very busy
WHICH = non-essential/non-restrictive (with commas)

Additional information that could be removed without changing the meaning.

  • The new policy, which was approved yesterday, takes effect immediately
  • Our employees, which number over 500, are highly skilled
  • The annual report, which contains detailed statistics, is now available
  • Parliament Hill, which attracts many tourists, houses our government

Compare These Examples:

Restrictive (that):

"Policies that violate privacy rights will be reviewed."

‚Üí Only policies that violate privacy rights (not all policies)

Non-restrictive (which):

"Our policies, which violate privacy rights, will be reviewed."

‚Üí All our policies happen to violate privacy rights

Restrictive (that):

"The meeting that starts at 2 p.m. is mandatory."

‚Üí Specifies which meeting (the 2 p.m. one)

Non-restrictive (which):

"The staff meeting, which starts at 2 p.m., is mandatory."

‚Üí Additional detail about the already-identified meeting

Memory Aids:

The Comma Test:

If you can put the clause in parentheses or remove it entirely without changing the basic meaning, use which with commas.

The "By the way" Test:

If the clause feels like "by the way" information, use which with commas.

The Essential Test:

If removing the clause would change which thing you're talking about, use that without commas.

Common Usage Errors

Frequently Confused Words

These word pairs are often confused in government writing, leading to unclear or incorrect communication.

Commonly Confused Pairs:

Ensure vs. Insure vs. Assure
  • Ensure = to make certain: "We will ensure the policy is implemented"
  • Insure = to provide insurance: "Insure your property against damage"
  • Assure = to convince or promise: "I assure you the funds are available"
Principal vs. Principle
  • Principal = main, primary: "The principal reason for the change"
  • Principle = rule, standard: "The principle of fairness guides our decisions"
Complement vs. Compliment
  • Complement = to complete: "This policy complements existing regulations"
  • Compliment = to praise: "The minister complimented the team's work"
Discreet vs. Discrete
  • Discreet = careful, tactful: "Handle this matter with discreet diplomacy"
  • Discrete = separate, distinct: "The program has three discrete phases"

Agreement Problems:

Subject-Verb Agreement
  • Collective nouns: "The committee is meeting" (as unit) vs. "The committee are divided" (as individuals)
  • None: "None of the reports is complete" (formal) or "None of the reports are complete" (informal)
  • Data: "The data shows" (commonly accepted) or "The data show" (traditional)
Pronoun Agreement
  • Everyone/somebody: "Everyone should submit their report" (modern usage)
  • Each: "Each department will receive its allocation"
  • Either/neither: "Neither option meets its objectives"

Redundancy and Wordiness:

‚úÖ Gift, plans, history

‚ùå In order to implement the new policy

‚úÖ To implement the new policy

‚ùå The reason why is because

‚úÖ The reason is or Because
