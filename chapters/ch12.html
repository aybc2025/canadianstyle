Affect vs. Effect

This is one of the most commonly confused pairs in English.

The Basic Rule

AFFECT (verb)

To influence or make a change

  • The weather affects our mood.
  • How did the changes affect you?
  • The medicine affected her sleep.

EFFECT (noun)

A result or consequence

  • The effect was immediate.
  • What are the effects of climate change?
  • Cause and effect are related.

Less Common Uses

Effect as a Verb

Meaning "to bring about" or "to cause":

  • The new CEO hopes to effect change. (bring about change)
  • We must effect a solution. (create/implement)

Affect as a Noun

In psychology, meaning "emotion" or "mood":

  • The patient showed a flat affect. (emotional state)

Memory Tricks

üí° Remember

  • Affect = Action (verb)
  • Effect = End result (noun)
  • If you can substitute "influence," use AFFECT
  • If you can substitute "result," use EFFECT

Practice Examples

Test Yourself

1. The new policy will _____ all employees. (affect)

2. The _____ of the policy was positive. (effect)

3. How did the medication _____ you? (affect)

4. The side _____ were minimal. (effects)

Compose vs. Comprise

These words describe the relationship between parts and wholes, but from opposite directions.

The Key Difference

COMPRISE

The whole comprises the parts

Whole ‚Üí Parts

  • Canada comprises ten provinces and three territories.
  • The committee comprises five members.
  • The report comprises three sections.

COMPOSE

The parts compose the whole

Parts ‚Üí Whole

  • Ten provinces and three territories compose Canada.
  • Five members compose the committee.
  • Three sections compose the report.

Common Forms

  • Is composed of: Canada is composed of ten provinces...
  • Comprises: Canada comprises ten provinces...
  • Consists of: Canada consists of ten provinces...

Never Say

‚ùå "is comprised of" (This is always wrong!)

Say instead:

  • ‚úì comprises
  • ‚úì is composed of
  • ‚úì consists of

Memory Aid

üí° Think of It This Way

Comprise = Include

You wouldn't say "is included of," so don't say "is comprised of"

Less vs. Fewer

This distinction relates to countable versus uncountable nouns.

The Basic Rule

FEWER

For things you can count (count nouns)

  • Fewer people attended
  • Fewer than 50 responses
  • Fewer mistakes this time
  • Fewer cars on the road

LESS

For things you can't count (mass nouns)

  • Less time available
  • Less money in the budget
  • Less patience today
  • Less traffic than usual

Exceptions and Special Cases

  • Time and money as units: Use "less"
    • Less than five minutes (time as a whole)
    • Less than $20 (money as amount)
  • Percentages and fractions: Use "less"
    • Less than 50 percent
    • Less than half

Common Mistakes

‚ö†Ô∏è Watch Out For

  • ‚ùå "10 items or less" ‚Üí ‚úì 10 items or fewer
  • ‚ùå "less people" ‚Üí ‚úì fewer people
  • ‚ùå "less opportunities" ‚Üí ‚úì fewer opportunities

Quick Test

üí° Can You Count It?

If you can put a number before it, use FEWER:

  • Three students ‚Üí fewer students
  • Five dollars ‚Üí BUT: less money

That vs. Which

This distinction parallels the restrictive/non-restrictive rule for commas.

The Essential Difference

THAT

For restrictive (essential) clauses

No commas needed

  • The report that you requested is ready.
  • Books that are overdue incur fines.
  • The policy that we discussed takes effect Monday.

WHICH

For non-restrictive (extra info) clauses

Use commas

  • The report, which took months to prepare, is ready.
  • These books, which are all new, are available.
  • The policy, which passed unanimously, takes effect Monday.

Test the Difference

Meaning Changes

That: The cars that are red must be moved. (only red cars)

Which: The cars, which are red, must be moved. (all cars happen to be red)

Canadian vs. British Usage

Canadian English follows the that/which distinction less strictly than American English but more than British English:

  • Canadian: Flexible, but distinction appreciated in formal writing
  • American: Strict adherence to the rule
  • British: Often uses "which" for both types

Quick Guide

üí° Remember

  • If you can remove it without changing meaning ‚Üí WHICH (with commas)
  • If removing it changes what you're talking about ‚Üí THAT (no commas)
  • When in doubt, "that" is usually safer

Common Usage Errors

Here are frequently misused words and phrases in Canadian business writing:

Words Often Confused

Alternate / Alternative

  • Alternate: Every other one (verb or adjective)
  • Alternative: Another choice (noun or adjective)

Continual / Continuous

  • Continual: Repeated with breaks
  • Continuous: Without interruption

Disinterested / Uninterested

  • Disinterested: Impartial, unbiased
  • Uninterested: Not interested, bored

Farther / Further

  • Farther: Physical distance
  • Further: Abstract degree, additionally
