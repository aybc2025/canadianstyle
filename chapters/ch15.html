Geographical Names

Use correct spellings and forms for Canadian place names

15.01 Introduction

In a bilingual country such as Canada, questions arise as to the designations to be used in official documents for cities, towns, villages, lakes, rivers, mountains and other geographical entities.

Key Principles

  • The official form of a geographical name is the one adopted by the provincial or federal authorities
  • This name can be found in the Gazetteer of Canada
  • Certain geographical names of pan-Canadian significance have official forms in both English and French
  • All other geographical names have only one official form
Background

On November 23, 1983, the Treasury Board issued Circular No. 1983-58 to implement the policy adopted by the Canadian Permanent Committee on Geographical Names (CPCGN) regarding the linguistic treatment of geographical names.

15.02 Canadian Names

Official Geographical Names

Official geographical names (toponyms) are those approved by a provincial, territorial or federal toponymic authority.

Names of Inhabited Places

Only two municipalities in Canada have two official forms of their names, one in English and one in French:

  • Grand Falls / Grand-Sault (New Brunswick)
  • Caissie Cape / Cap-des-Caissie (New Brunswick)

Important: All other municipalities have only one authorized form. Thus Montréal and Québec (the city) retain their accents in English.

Generic and Specific Components

Geographical names typically consist of:

  • Generic: The portion that indicates the nature of the entity (e.g., "Lake" in "Arrow Lake")
  • Specific: The portion that names the entity (e.g., "Arrow" in "Arrow Lake")

Translation Rules

Permissible Translation

In documents, it is permissible to translate the generic portion of names of geographical features, but NOT the specific portion:

  • ✓ Arrow Lake → lac Arrow
  • ✗ Arrow Lake → lac Flèche

15.03 Foreign Names

General Rules for Foreign Place Names

When referring to foreign geographical names in Canadian documents:

  • Use the English form if one is well-established (e.g., Munich not München)
  • Retain the local form if no established English equivalent exists
  • Be consistent throughout the document
  • Consider your audience's familiarity with the names

Common Foreign Place Names

Local Form English Form Usage
Roma Rome Use English form
München Munich Use English form
Praha Prague Use English form
São Paulo São Paulo Retain local form

💡 Tip

When in doubt, consult authoritative sources such as:

  • The Canadian Oxford Dictionary
  • The Columbia Gazetteer of the World
  • Official government style guides

15.04 Adjectives and Nouns of Nationality

Forming Adjectives from Place Names

When creating adjectives or referring to inhabitants of places:

Place Adjective Inhabitant
Quebec Quebec (adj.) Quebecer
Montreal Montreal (adj.) Montrealer
Toronto Toronto (adj.) Torontonian
Halifax Halifax (adj.) Haligonian
Winnipeg Winnipeg (adj.) Winnipegger

Provincial and Territorial Demonyms

  • Albertan (from Alberta)
  • British Columbian (from British Columbia)
  • Manitoban (from Manitoba)
  • New Brunswicker (from New Brunswick)
  • Newfoundlander (from Newfoundland)
  • Nova Scotian (from Nova Scotia)
  • Ontarian (from Ontario)
  • Prince Edward Islander (from Prince Edward Island)
  • Quebecer (from Quebec)
  • Saskatchewanian (from Saskatchewan)
  • Yukoner (from Yukon)

Note: Some adjectives are identical to the place name (e.g., "Quebec culture," "Toronto skyline").

Usage Guidelines

  • Use two-letter postal codes (no periods) in addresses
  • Use traditional abbreviations (with periods) in running text
  • Spell out province names in formal documents
  • Be consistent within a document
