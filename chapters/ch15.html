15.01 Introduction

Geographical names present special challenges in writing. They must be treated consistently and with respect for local preferences and official designations.

This chapter covers the proper treatment of place names, from small communities to countries, and the adjectives and nouns derived from them.

Key Principles

  • Use official names when available
  • Respect local spelling preferences
  • Be consistent within a document
  • Consider your audience's familiarity
  • Use current, not historical names

15.02 Canadian Names

Official Sources

For Canadian place names, consult:

  • The Geographical Names Board of Canada
  • Provincial geographical names authorities
  • Canada Gazetteer Atlas
  • Official municipal websites

Special Considerations

Indigenous Names

  • Use current official spellings
  • Many names have been changed from colonial versions
  • Respect Indigenous pronunciation guides
  • Be aware of ongoing name changes

French Names

  • Preserve accents and special characters
  • Don't translate unless common practice
  • Use official bilingual versions when available

Transliteration

When transliterating from other alphabets:

  • Use established English spellings when they exist
  • Follow consistent transliteration systems
  • Consider your audience's familiarity
  • Provide pronunciation guides when helpful

Special Cases

Ukraine: Use "Ukraine" not "the Ukraine"

Netherlands: "the Netherlands" (always with "the")

Philippines: "the Philippines" (always with "the")

Czech Republic/Czechia: Both are acceptable

15.04 Adjectives and Nouns

Forming Adjectives

Geographical adjectives (demonyms) can be tricky:

Place Adjective/Noun Example
Canada Canadian Canadian policies
Nova Scotia Nova Scotian Nova Scotian lobster
Calgary Calgarian Calgarian residents
Montreal Montrealer Montrealer attitudes
Prince Edward Island Prince Edward Islander Prince Edward Islander traditions

Capitalization Rules

Always Capitalize

  • Proper adjectives: "American foreign policy"
  • Nouns referring to people: "Canadians believe..."
  • Languages: "She speaks French fluently"

Don't Capitalize

  • Common nouns not referring to people: "french fries"
  • Compass directions used descriptively: "northern Ontario"
  • Generic geographical terms: "the river" (when not part of proper name)
