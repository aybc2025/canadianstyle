9.01 Introduction

Reference matter provides readers with the sources, explanations, and additional information they need to understand and verify your work. This chapter covers footnotes, bibliographies, citations, indexes, and specialized reference formats used in government documents.

Types of Reference Matter

üìù Footnotes & Endnotes

Additional information and source citations

üìö Bibliography

Complete list of sources consulted

üîó Citations

In-text references to sources

üìñ Indexes

Alphabetical guide to document content

General Principles

  • Be consistent in formatting throughout the document
  • Provide complete information for all sources
  • Use standard formatting conventions
  • Consider your audience's needs
  • Verify all references before publication

9.02 Footnotes

Footnotes provide additional information or cite sources without interrupting the main text. They appear at the bottom of the page where the reference occurs.

Footnote Formatting

  • Numbering: Use superscript numbers in consecutive order
  • Placement: After punctuation, not before
  • Spacing: Single space within footnotes
  • Separator: Use a short horizontal line above footnotes

Footnote Examples

First Reference:

¬π John Smith, Canadian Policy Analysis (Ottawa: Government Publishing, 2023), 45.

Subsequent Reference:

¬≤ Smith, Canadian Policy Analysis, 67.

Ibid (same source, consecutive notes):

¬≥ Ibid., 72.

Avoid "op. cit." in modern usage. Instead, use a shortened version of the title for subsequent references to the same work.

9.03 Bibliography

A bibliography lists all sources consulted in preparing a document. Entries are arranged alphabetically by author's last name or by title for works with no author.

Bibliography Format

  • Books: Author. Title. Place: Publisher, Year.
  • Articles: Author. "Title." Journal Volume, no. Issue (Year): pages.
  • Government Documents: Department. Title. Place: Publisher, Year.
  • Online Sources: Author. "Title." Website. Date. URL.

Bibliography Examples

Book:

Wilson, Sarah. Policy Implementation in Canada. Toronto: Academic Press, 2023.

Journal Article:

Brown, Michael. "Fiscal Policy Trends." Canadian Public Administration 67, no. 2 (2023): 145-162.

Government Report:

Statistics Canada. Canadian Economic Indicators. Ottawa: Government Publishing, 2023.

üí° Bibliography Tips

  • Alphabetize by first significant word (ignore "A," "An," "The")
  • Use hanging indent for each entry
  • Include all sources cited in text
  • Verify publication details

9.04 Citations

In-text citations acknowledge sources within the body of your document. They must correspond to entries in your bibliography or reference list.

When to Cite

  • Direct quotations of any length
  • Paraphrased ideas or information
  • Statistical data or figures
  • Specific facts not commonly known
  • Expert opinions or interpretations

Citation Styles

Author-Date System (APA style):

Recent studies show improved outcomes (Wilson, 2023, p. 45).

Numbered System:

Economic indicators suggest growth [1].

Note System:

Policy implementation has improved significantly.¬π

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Citation Errors

  • Missing page numbers for quotations
  • Incomplete publication information
  • Inconsistent formatting style
  • Citations not matching bibliography

9.05 Indexes

An index is an alphabetical list of names, places, and subjects mentioned in a document, with page numbers where they can be found. A well-constructed index significantly improves a document's usability.

Index Construction Principles

  • Selectivity: Include only significant references
  • Consistency: Use uniform terminology and formatting
  • Cross-referencing: Guide readers to related topics
  • Hierarchy: Use main entries and subentries appropriately

Index Entry Types

Simple Entry:

Budget planning, 45, 67, 89-91

Entry with Subentries:

Government departments
    Finance, 23, 45-47
    Health, 67, 89
    Transport, 102-105

Cross-Reference:

Federal government. See Government, federal

Advanced Indexing Techniques

Personal Names

List by surname: "Smith, John, 45, 67"

Compound Terms

Choose the most significant word: "Policy, economic" not "Economic policy"

Acronyms

Include both forms: "CBC (Canadian Broadcasting Corporation), 34"

Multiple References

Use commas for individual pages, hyphens for ranges: "23, 45-47, 89"

9.06 Electronic Sources

Electronic sources require special consideration in citations and bibliographies. Include all information necessary for readers to locate the source.

Electronic Source Elements

  • Author: Individual or organization
  • Title: Document or page title
  • Website: Name of the site
  • Date: Publication or update date
  • URL: Complete web address
  • Access Date: When you viewed the source

Electronic Source Citations

Government Website:

Statistics Canada. "Population Estimates." Statistics Canada. Last modified March 15, 2023. https://www.statcan.gc.ca/population.

PDF Document:

Department of Finance. "Budget 2023 Highlights." 2023. PDF file. https://www.fin.gc.ca/budget23/highlights.pdf.

Online News Article:

Johnson, Mary. "New Economic Policies Announced." CBC News. June 10, 2023. https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/economic-policies.

üí° Electronic Source Best Practices

  • Use DOI (Digital Object Identifier) when available
  • Include retrieval date for sources that may change
  • Verify URLs are complete and functional
  • Use archived versions for important sources

9.07 Legal References

Legal documents require specialized citation formats. Follow established legal citation styles consistently throughout your document.

Legal Citation Elements

  • Statutes: Title, jurisdiction, year
  • Cases: Case name, citation, court, year
  • Regulations: Title, regulation number, jurisdiction
  • Treaties: Full name, parties, date, source

Legal Citation Examples

Federal Statute:

Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, Part I of the Constitution Act, 1982, being Schedule B to the Canada Act 1982 (UK), 1982, c 11.

Supreme Court Case:

R. v. Oakes, [1986] 1 S.C.R. 103, 1986 CanLII 46 (SCC).

Federal Regulation:

Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations, SOR/2002-227.

‚ö†Ô∏è Legal Citation Guidelines

  • Use official case names and citations
  • Include court level and jurisdiction
  • Verify current status of cited laws
  • Follow consistent citation style

9.08 Advanced Indexing

Advanced indexing techniques help create comprehensive, user-friendly indexes for complex documents. Master these skills to produce professional reference tools.

Complex Index Structures

  • Multiple levels: Main entries, subentries, sub-subentries
  • Cross-references: "See" and "See also" references
  • Continued headings: For entries spanning multiple columns
  • Special formatting: Italics for emphasis, bold for definitions

Advanced Index Examples

Multi-level Entry:

Economic policy
    development, 23-25
    implementation
        federal level, 45-47
        provincial level, 67-69
    international aspects, 89-91

Cross-Reference Network:

Budget. See also Fiscal policy; Government spending
Fiscal policy, 23, 45-47, 89. See also Budget; Economic policy
Government spending. See Budget

Professional Indexing Techniques

üéØ Term Selection

  • Choose words readers will look for
  • Include synonyms and variants
  • Consider different user perspectives

üìä Entry Analysis

  • Significant discussion: bold page numbers
  • Definitions: italic page numbers
  • Brief mentions: regular page numbers

üîÑ Quality Control

  • Check alphabetical order
  • Verify page numbers
  • Eliminate duplicate entries
