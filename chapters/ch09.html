9.01 Introduction

Proper documentation of sources is essential for academic integrity and credibility. This chapter covers the main systems for citing sources in Canadian government and academic writing.

Why Document Sources?

  • Credit: Acknowledge others' work
  • Verification: Allow readers to check sources
  • Further reading: Help readers find more information
  • Legal protection: Avoid plagiarism

Main Documentation Systems

Footnotes/Endnotes

Traditional academic style

Numbers in text link to notes

Author-Date (In-text)

Common in sciences and social sciences

(Smith 2024) in text

Author-Number

Used in some scientific fields

Numbers refer to bibliography

Key Principle

Choose one documentation system and use it consistently throughout your document. Follow your organization's or publisher's preferred style.

9.23 Footnotes

Footnotes appear at the bottom of the page where the reference occurs:

Footnote Format

  • Superscript number in text¹
  • Corresponding note at page bottom
  • Separated from text by short line
  • Single-spaced, smaller font
  • First line indented

The policy has been widely criticized¹ for its environmental impact.

1 Jane Smith, Environmental Policy in Canada (Toronto: University Press, 2023), 45.

First Reference (Full Citation)

Book

¹ Margaret Atwood, The Handmaid's Tale (Toronto: McClelland & Stewart, 1985), 23.

Journal Article

² David Suzuki, "Climate Change and Canadian Policy," Canadian Environmental Review 45, no. 3 (2023): 12-28.

Government Document

³ Statistics Canada, Census Profile, 2021 Census (Ottawa: Statistics Canada, 2022), Table 1.2.

Subsequent References

  • Ibid.: Same source, immediately following
  • Short form: Author's last name and page
  • Op. cit.: Same work cited earlier (avoid)
Example Sequence

¹ Atwood, Handmaid's Tale, 23.

² Ibid., 45.

³ Suzuki, "Climate Change," 15.

⁴ Atwood, Handmaid's Tale, 67.

Footnote Numbering

  • Number consecutively through chapter
  • Or restart each page (less common)
  • Place after punctuation
  • Use Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3)

9.24 Endnotes

Endnotes collect all references at the end of a chapter or document:

Advantages of Endnotes

  • Cleaner page layout
  • Easier to format
  • Better for lengthy notes
  • Simpler for digital documents

Endnote Format

Notes to Chapter 2

1. M. Fleming and W. H. Levie, eds., Instructional Message Design: Principles From the Behavioral and Cognitive Sciences, 2nd ed. (Englewood Cliffs, N.J.: Educational Technology Publications, 1993), 34-57.

2. Fleming and Levie, 66.

3. B. Joyce, B. Showers, and C. Rolheiser-Bennett, "Staff Development and Student Learning: A Synthesis of Research on Models of Teaching," Educational Leadership 45, no. 2 (1987): 11-23.

Endnote Placement

  • Chapter end: Before bibliography
  • Book end: After all chapters
  • Article end: Before references

Converting Between Systems

Modern word processors can easily convert between footnotes and endnotes. Choose based on:

  • Publisher requirements
  • Document length
  • Reader preferences
  • Number of notes

9.25 In-text Notes (Author-Date System)

The author-date system places brief citations directly in the text:

Basic Format

In-text Citations

Climate change poses significant risks (Suzuki 2023).

According to Suzuki (2023), climate change poses significant risks.

Suzuki (2023) argues that climate change poses significant risks.

Multiple Authors

  • Two authors: (Smith and Jones 2023)
  • Three or more: (Smith et al. 2023)
  • Organizations: (Statistics Canada 2023)

Page Numbers

With Page References

Direct quote: (Atwood 1985, 23)

Specific reference: (Suzuki 2023, 45-47)

Multiple pages: (Smith 2023, 12, 34, 56)

Multiple Citations

Separate multiple sources with semicolons:

Example

Several studies confirm this finding (Brown 2022; Green 2023; White 2023).

Reference List Format

References

Atwood, Margaret. 1985. The Handmaid's Tale. Toronto: McClelland & Stewart.

Suzuki, David. 2023. "Climate Change and Canadian Policy." Canadian Environmental Review 45 (3): 12-28.

Statistics Canada. 2023. Labour Force Survey, September 2023. Ottawa: Statistics Canada.

Advantages

  • Less disruptive to reading
  • Easy to add/remove sources
  • Clear author attribution
  • Standard in many fields

9.27–9.62 Bibliographies

A bibliography lists all sources consulted, whether cited or not. A reference list includes only cited sources.

Bibliography Format

  • Alphabetical by author's last name
  • Hanging indent (first line flush left)
  • Single-spaced entries
  • Double-space between entries

Book Entries

Single Author

Atwood, Margaret. The Handmaid's Tale. Toronto: McClelland & Stewart, 1985.

Multiple Authors

Smith, John, and Jane Doe. Canadian Politics Today. 3rd ed. Vancouver: UBC Press, 2023.

Edited Book

Brown, Sarah, ed. Essays on Canadian Literature. Montreal: McGill-Queen's University Press, 2022.

Chapter in Edited Book

Green, Michael. "Urban Planning in Toronto." In Canadian Cities, edited by Lisa White, 45-67. Ottawa: Carleton University Press, 2023.

Periodical Entries

Journal Article

Suzuki, David. "Climate Change and Canadian Policy." Canadian Environmental Review 45, no. 3 (2023): 12-28.

Magazine Article

Johnson, Kate. "The Future of Healthcare." Maclean's, March 15, 2023, 22-25.

Newspaper Article

Wilson, Robert. "Budget Highlights Infrastructure Spending." The Globe and Mail, April 1, 2023, A1.

Government Documents

Canada. Parliament. House of Commons. Debates, 44th Parliament, 1st Session, vol. 151, no. 156, February 15, 2023.

Statistics Canada. Census Profile, 2021 Census. Statistics Canada Catalogue no. 98-316-X2021001. Ottawa: Statistics Canada, 2022.

Special Considerations

  • No author: Begin with title
  • Same author: Use 3-em dash (———)
  • Multiple works same year: Add a, b, c (2023a, 2023b)
  • Forthcoming: Use "Forthcoming" instead of date

9.63 Electronic Sources

Electronic sources require special attention to ensure readers can locate them:

Key Elements for Online Sources

  • Author (if available)
  • Title of page or article
  • Title of website
  • Publication/update date
  • URL or DOI
  • Access date (if no publication date)

Website Citations

General Website

Government of Canada. "Climate Action Plan." Last modified January 15, 2023. https://www.canada.ca/climate-action.

News Website

CBC News. "Federal Budget 2023: Key Highlights." CBC, March 28, 2023. https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/federal-budget-2023.

Blog Post

Smith, Jane. "Writing Better Reports." Government Communications Blog, February 20, 2023. https://blog.gc.ca/writing-reports.

Digital Documents

PDF Report

Environment Canada. State of Canada's Environment 2023. PDF. Ottawa: Environment Canada, 2023. https://publications.gc.ca/environment-2023.pdf.

E-book

Brown, Michael. Digital Government. Toronto: Tech Press, 2023. Kindle edition.

Social Media

Twitter/X

@JustinTrudeau. "Proud to announce our new climate initiative..." Twitter, March 15, 2023, 2:30 p.m. https://twitter.com/JustinTrudeau/status/123456789.

DOI vs URL

  • Prefer DOI: More stable than URLs
  • Format: https://doi.org/10.1234/5678
  • No period: After URLs/DOIs

⚠️ Important

Always test URLs before submitting. Include access dates for sources that may change or disappear.
