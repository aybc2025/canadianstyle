Reference Matter

Master footnotes, citations, bibliographies, and reference formatting

9.01 Introduction

Reference matter helps readers locate sources, verify information, and explore topics further. Proper documentation enhances credibility and allows others to build upon your work.

Key Principle

All reference matter must be accurate, complete, and consistently formatted. The goal is to enable readers to locate the exact sources you used.

Types of Reference Matter

  • Footnotes: Explanatory notes or source citations at bottom of page
  • Endnotes: Notes collected at the end of a chapter or document
  • Bibliography: Complete list of sources consulted
  • References: List of sources actually cited in the text
  • Indexes: Alphabetical guide to topics and names
  • Appendices: Supplementary material

When to Use Reference Matter

Required for:

  • Research reports and policy papers
  • Academic or scholarly documents
  • Legal documents requiring precedent
  • Technical manuals with external standards
  • Documents making statistical claims

Optional for:

  • Internal memos and routine correspondence
  • Press releases and public announcements
  • Procedural instructions
  • Meeting minutes and agendas

⚠️ Legal and Ethical Requirements

Proper attribution is not just good practice—it's often legally required. Failure to cite sources can constitute plagiarism or copyright infringement.

9.02 Footnotes

Footnotes provide citations, explanations, or additional information without disrupting the main text. They appear at the bottom of the page where the reference occurs.

Footnote Numbering

  • Sequential numbering: Start with 1 and continue consecutively
  • Superscript format: Small raised numbers in text and notes
  • Placement: After punctuation marks, not before
  • Restart options: Each page, chapter, or continue throughout document

Footnote Format Examples

In text:

The policy was implemented in 2023,¹ resulting in significant changes.² However, some critics argued that the timeline was too ambitious.³

At bottom of page:

¹ Department of Justice Canada, Policy Implementation Guide (Ottawa: Government of Canada, 2023), 15.

² Statistics Canada, "Policy Impact Analysis," accessed March 15, 2024, https://www.statcan.gc.ca/policy-analysis.

³ Sarah Johnson, "Timeline Concerns in Policy Implementation," Canadian Public Administration 45, no. 3 (2023): 234.

Types of Footnotes

  • Source citations: Identify where information came from
  • Explanatory notes: Provide additional context or clarification
  • Cross-references: Direct readers to related sections
  • Translations: Provide English versions of foreign terms

Government Document Citations

Source Type Format
Government Report Department, Title (Location: Publisher, Year), page.
Legislation Act Title, S.C. Year, c. Chapter.
Regulation Regulation Title, S.O.R./Year-Number.
Parliamentary Debate Canada, Parliament, House of Commons, Debates, Date (Speaker).

Formatting Guidelines

  • Font size: Smaller than main text (usually 9-10 point)
  • Single spacing: Within footnotes, double space between them
  • Indentation: First line indented, subsequent lines flush left
  • Separator: Horizontal line between text and footnotes

💡 Best Practices

Keep footnotes concise. If a note becomes lengthy, consider moving the information to an appendix or incorporating it into the main text.

9.03 Bibliography

A bibliography is an alphabetical list of sources consulted during research. It may include sources not directly cited in the text but that informed the author's understanding.

Bibliography vs. References

  • Bibliography: All sources consulted, including background reading
  • References/Works Cited: Only sources actually cited in the text
  • Further Reading: Recommended sources for additional information
  • Select Bibliography: Most important or relevant sources only

Bibliography Entry Formats

Books:

Author, Title. Place: Publisher, Year.

Example: Smith, John. Canadian Government Policy. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2023.

Journal Articles:

Author. "Article Title." Journal Name Volume, no. Issue (Year): pages.

Example: Wilson, Sarah. "Policy Implementation Challenges." Canadian Public Administration 67, no. 2 (2023): 145-162.

Government Documents:

Government Department. Document Title. Place: Publisher, Year.

Example: Statistics Canada. Census Profile, 2021 Census. Ottawa: Statistics Canada, 2022.

Alphabetical Arrangement

  • By author's last name: Letter by letter, ignoring spaces
  • No author: Alphabetize by title (ignore "A," "An," "The")
  • Same author: Arrange chronologically by publication date
  • Government sources: Often listed separately or by issuing department

Special Cases in Government Writing

Legislation:

Charter of Rights and Freedoms. Part I of the Constitution Act, 1982, being Schedule B to the Canada Act 1982 (UK), 1982, c 11.

Court Cases:

R. v. Smith, 2023 SCC 15.

Parliamentary Debates:

Canada. Parliament. House of Commons. Debates. 44th Parliament, 1st Session, Vol. 151, No. 45 (March 15, 2023).

Online Government Sources:

Health Canada. "COVID-19 Guidelines." Last modified March 10, 2024. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/covid-guidelines.

Formatting Conventions

  • Hanging indent: First line flush left, subsequent lines indented
  • Single spacing: Within entries, double space between entries
  • Italics: Book titles, journal names, newspaper names
  • Quotation marks: Article titles, chapter titles

⚠️ Currency and Accuracy

Ensure all bibliographic information is current and accurate. Verify URLs and access dates for online sources. Government websites and documents change frequently.

9.04 Citations

Citations identify the specific sources of information, quotations, and ideas used in your document. Government writing often requires precise citation to support policy decisions and maintain accountability.

When to Cite

  • Direct quotations: Any text copied word-for-word
  • Paraphrased ideas: Information restated in your own words
  • Statistical data: Numbers, percentages, research findings
  • Specific facts: Historical events, dates, technical specifications
  • Expert opinions: Professional judgments and recommendations

In-Text Citation Methods

Footnote/Endnote System:

The report concluded that "implementation timelines were unrealistic."¹

Studies show varying success rates² in similar jurisdictions.

Author-Date System (APA):

The report concluded that "implementation timelines were unrealistic" (Johnson, 2023, p. 45).

Studies show varying success rates (Smith & Wilson, 2023) in similar jurisdictions.

Numbered Reference System:

The report concluded that "implementation timelines were unrealistic" [1].

Studies show varying success rates [2,3] in similar jurisdictions.

Government Citation Formats

  • Legal citations: Follow Canadian Guide to Uniform Legal Citation
  • Parliamentary materials: Use official parliamentary citation format
  • Regulations: Include specific regulation numbers and dates
  • Departmental reports: Include full department name and publication details

Canadian Government Citation Examples

Federal Legislation:

Privacy Act, R.S.C., 1985, c. P-21.

Canadian Human Rights Act, R.S.C., 1985, c. H-6, s. 7.

Regulations:

Privacy Regulations, S.O.R./83-508.

Cabinet Documents:

Privy Council Office, Memorandum to Cabinet, "Economic Recovery Plan" (Ottawa: PCO, 2023).

Parliamentary Committees:

Canada, Parliament, House of Commons, Standing Committee on Finance, Report on Budget Implementation (Ottawa: House of Commons, 2023).

Online Government Sources

  • Include access date: Government websites change frequently
  • Use permanent URLs: When available (often ending in .gc.ca)
  • Archive links: Consider using web archive services for stability
  • Version control: Note if document has version numbers or dates

Digital Source Citations

Government Website:

Environment and Climate Change Canada, "Climate Action Plan," last modified February 15, 2024, accessed March 10, 2024, https://www.canada.ca/en/environment-climate-change/climate-action.

Statistical Data:

Statistics Canada, "Labour Force Survey, February 2024," The Daily, March 8, 2024, accessed March 10, 2024, https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/daily-quotidien/240308/dq240308a-eng.htm.

PDF Documents:

Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat, Directive on Automated Decision-Making (Ottawa: TBS, 2023), PDF, accessed March 10, 2024, https://www.tbs-sct.gc.ca/pol/doc-eng.aspx?id=32592.

💡 Citation Management

Use citation management software like Zotero or EndNote to track sources and generate consistent citations. Many tools have Canadian government templates.

9.05 Indexes

Indexes provide alphabetical access to topics, names, and concepts in a document. They are essential for longer government documents that readers need to search for specific information.

Types of Indexes

  • Subject index: Topics, concepts, and themes
  • Name index: People, organizations, and places
  • Combined index: All entries in one alphabetical list
  • Specialized indexes: Legal cases, legislation, tables, figures

Index Entry Examples

Simple entries:

  • Budget, 15, 23, 67-72
  • Employment insurance, 45
  • Privacy protection, 12, 89

Main entries with subentries:

  • Government departments
    • Environment and Climate Change Canada, 34
    • Health Canada, 56, 78
    • Transport Canada, 23, 91

Cross-references:

  • EI. See Employment insurance
  • Public servants. See also Civil service
  • COVID-19. See also Public health; Pandemic response

Alphabetization Rules

  • Letter-by-letter: Ignore spaces and punctuation (New York before Newark)
  • Word-by-word: Consider spaces (New York before Newark)
  • Numbers: Spell out or place before/after alphabetical entries
  • Abbreviations: Alphabetize as if spelled out

Government-Specific Index Entries

Legislation and Regulations:

  • Access to Information Act, 23, 45
  • Charter of Rights and Freedoms, 12, 67, 89
  • Privacy Act, 34, 56
  • Regulations (general), 15-18

Government Officials:

  • Deputy Ministers, 25, 78
  • Minister of Finance, 34
  • Prime Minister, 12, 23, 89
  • Public Service Commission, 45

Programs and Services:

  • Canada Child Benefit, 56
  • Employment Insurance, 23, 67
  • Old Age Security, 45
  • Universal Child Care, 78

Page Number Conventions

  • Individual pages: 12, 34, 56
  • Page ranges: 23-27, 45-49
  • Scattered references: 12, 15, 23, 67
  • Main discussion: 34-38 (bold or italic)
  • Illustrations: 45 (fig.), 67 (table)

Creating Effective Indexes

  • Think like a reader: What terms would they search for?
  • Use consistent terminology: Match the language in the text
  • Provide multiple access points: Include synonyms and variants
  • Keep entries concise: Use the shortest form that's clear

💡 Digital Indexing

Modern word processors can generate basic indexes automatically, but human review is essential for quality. Consider searchable PDFs as an alternative to traditional indexes for digital documents.

9.06 Electronic Sources

Electronic sources present unique challenges for citation and reference. Government documents are increasingly digital, requiring special attention to permanence, accessibility, and accurate citation.

Types of Electronic Sources

  • Government websites: Departments, agencies, Parliament
  • Digital databases: Statistical data, legal databases
  • Online reports: PDF documents, HTML pages
  • Social media: Official government accounts
  • Email and digital correspondence: Official communications
  • Digital archives: Historical documents online

Electronic Citation Elements

Essential elements for all electronic sources:

  • Author or organization name
  • Title of document or webpage
  • Publication or last modified date
  • URL or DOI
  • Access date

Additional elements when available:

  • Version number
  • File format (PDF, HTML)
  • Database name
  • Publisher information

Government Website Citation

Format: Organization. "Page Title." Website Name. Last modified Date. Accessed Date. URL.

Examples:

Health Canada. "Vaccine Safety." Canada.ca. Last modified March 5, 2024. Accessed March 10, 2024. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/vaccine-safety.html.

Statistics Canada. "Consumer Price Index, February 2024." The Daily. March 8, 2024. Accessed March 10, 2024. https://www150.statcan.gc.ca/n1/daily-quotidien/240308/dq240308a-eng.htm.

Special Considerations for Government Sources

  • URL stability: Government URLs may change during website updates
  • Bilingual content: Specify language version (English/French)
  • Version control: Documents may be updated without notice
  • Archive policies: Older content may be moved or removed

Specialized Electronic Sources

Parliamentary Proceedings (online):

Canada. Parliament. House of Commons. Debates. 44th Parliament, 1st Session, No. 45 (March 15, 2024). Accessed March 16, 2024. https://www.ourcommons.ca/DocumentViewer/en/44-1/house/sitting-45/hansard.

Legal Database:

R. v. Smith, 2024 SCC 8. CanLII. Accessed March 10, 2024. https://canlii.ca/t/k2x34.

Digital Archive:

Library and Archives Canada. "Cabinet Conclusions, 1968." Digital Archives. Accessed March 10, 2024. https://www.bac-lac.gc.ca/eng/discover/politics-government/cabinet-conclusions/Pages/item.aspx?IdNumber=1968.

Social Media (Official Account):

Prime Minister's Office (@CanadianPM). "Climate action announcement." Twitter, March 8, 2024, 2:30 PM. https://twitter.com/CanadianPM/status/1766123456789.

Ensuring Long-term Access

  • Permalink preference: Use permanent URLs when available
  • Web archiving: Consider using Wayback Machine links
  • PDF preservation: Save important documents locally
  • Multiple formats: Note if content exists in different formats

⚠️ Digital Preservation Challenges

Electronic sources can disappear or change without notice. For critical sources, consider citing archived versions or maintaining local copies with appropriate permissions.

💡 Best Practices

  • Check links regularly and update as needed
  • Include access dates for all online sources
  • Use shortened URLs cautiously—they may break
  • Consider including DOIs when available for academic sources
