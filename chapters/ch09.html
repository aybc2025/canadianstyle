9.01 Introduction to Reference Matter

Reference matter helps readers locate sources, verify information, and explore topics further. This chapter covers Canadian government standards for footnotes, bibliographies, citations, and indexes.

Purpose of Reference Matter

  • Provide source verification for readers
  • Support credibility and transparency
  • Enable further research and fact-checking
  • Meet academic and professional standards

Types of Reference Matter

  • Footnotes: Additional information at page bottom
  • Endnotes: Notes collected at document end
  • Bibliography: List of sources consulted
  • Citations: In-text source references
  • Indexes: Alphabetical topic finder

Canadian Government Context

  • Transparency and accountability requirements
  • Public accessibility of information
  • Consistency across departments
  • Professional presentation standards

9.23 Footnotes

Footnote Numbers

Place footnote numbers after punctuation marks, using superscript numerals that run consecutively through the document.

Placement of Numbers

  • After periods: The report was comprehensive.1
  • After commas: According to Smith,2 the data shows...
  • After quotation marks: She called it "essential".3
  • After question marks: Who made that decision?4

Multiple References

  • Sequential: The studies1,2,3 all agree
  • Range: Several reports4-7 confirm this
  • Separate: The data8 and analysis9 support...
  • Same source: As noted earlier2 (see note 2)

Footnote Content

Footnotes can contain:

  • Source citations: Author, title, publication details
  • Additional information: Explanatory comments
  • Cross-references: "See also section 3.2"
  • Translations: Original language versions

Sample Footnotes:

The new policy framework1 addresses three key areas: economic development, environmental protection,2 and social equity.

1 Department of Finance, Policy Framework for Sustainable Development (Ottawa: Government of Canada, 2024), 15-18.

2 For detailed environmental guidelines, see Environment and Climate Change Canada, Environmental Assessment Guidelines, available at canada.ca/environment.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Use smaller font size than main text
  • Separate from main text with a line
  • Single-space within footnotes, double-space between
  • Start each footnote with superscript number

9.35 Bibliography

Purpose of Bibliography

A bibliography lists all sources consulted during research, even if not directly cited in the text.

Types of Bibliographies

  • Works Cited: Only sources directly referenced
  • Works Consulted: All sources examined
  • Selected Bibliography: Key sources only
  • Annotated Bibliography: With brief descriptions

Organization Methods

  • Alphabetical: By author's last name
  • Chronological: By publication date
  • Subject areas: Grouped by topic
  • Source type: Books, articles, websites

Sample Bibliography Entry Formats:

Books:

Smith, John A. Canadian Public Policy: A Comprehensive Guide. 3rd ed. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2024.

Wilson, Sarah, and Michael Brown. Government Communications in the Digital Age. Vancouver: Douglas & McIntyre, 2023.

Journal Articles:

Jones, Mary. "Policy Implementation in Federal Agencies." Canadian Public Administration 67, no. 2 (2024): 145-162.

Government Documents:

Canada. Department of Finance. Budget 2024: Fairness for Every Generation. Ottawa: Government of Canada, 2024.

Statistics Canada. "Labour Force Survey, March 2024." Ottawa: Statistics Canada, 2024. Accessed April 15, 2024. https://statcan.gc.ca.

Online Sources:

Environment and Climate Change Canada. "Climate Action Plan." Government of Canada. Last modified March 10, 2024. https://canada.ca/climate-action.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Use hanging indent for each entry
  • List entries alphabetically by author's last name
  • Use consistent citation style throughout
  • Include all necessary publication details

9.50 In-Text Citations

Citation Systems

Canadian government documents typically use author-date or numbered citation systems, depending on the context and audience.

Author-Date System

  • Single author: (Smith, 2024)
  • Multiple authors: (Smith & Jones, 2024)
  • No author: (Department of Finance, 2024)
  • Page numbers: (Smith, 2024, p. 45)
  • Multiple works: (Smith, 2024; Jones, 2023)

Numbered System

  • First reference: (1)
  • Subsequent references: (1)
  • Multiple sources: (1-3)
  • Page specific: (1, p. 45)
  • Updated reference: (1, updated 2024)

Integration Examples:

Author as Part of Sentence:

According to Smith (2024), the new regulations will improve efficiency by 25%.

The Department of Finance (2024) reported a budget surplus for the first time in five years.

Author in Parentheses:

The new regulations will improve efficiency by 25% (Smith, 2024).

Budget projections show a surplus for the first time in five years (Department of Finance, 2024).

Direct Quotations:

The minister stated that "immediate action is required" (Press Release, 2024, p. 2).

As noted in the report, "policy coordination remains a challenge" (Interdepartmental Committee, 2024, para. 15).

Government Document Citations

  • Department as author: (Health Canada, 2024)
  • No specific author: (Government of Canada, 2024)
  • Multiple departments: (Health Canada & Public Safety Canada, 2024)
  • Updated documents: (Treasury Board, 2024, updated March 15)

Online Sources

  • With date: (Statistics Canada, 2024)
  • No date: (Environment Canada, n.d.)
  • Webpage: (Canada.ca, 2024)
  • Database: (CANSIM, Table 14-10-0287, 2024)

Legal Documents

  • Acts: (Official Languages Act, 1985)
  • Regulations: (Privacy Regulations, SOR/2000-13)
  • Cases: (R. v. Smith, 2024 SCC 15)
  • Orders: (Order in Council P.C. 2024-123)

9.60 Indexes

Purpose of Indexes

Indexes help readers quickly locate specific topics, names, and concepts within documents. They are essential for longer government publications.

Types of Indexes

  • Subject index: Topics and concepts
  • Name index: People and organizations
  • Geographic index: Places and regions
  • Combined index: All types together

Index Features

  • Main entries: Primary topics
  • Subentries: Related subtopics
  • Cross-references: "See" and "See also"
  • Page ranges: 15-18, 45-52

Sample Index Entries:

A

Accessibility, 45-48, 67

accommodation requirements, 47

web standards, 48, 102

Accountability, 12-15, 89-92

financial, 89-90

performance measurement, 91-92

See also Transparency

B

Budget process, 156-175

appropriations, 160-165

estimates, 156-159

supplementary estimates, 170-175

P

Policy development, 23-45

consultation process, 35-40

implementation, 41-45

research phase, 23-30

Prime Minister's Office, 78, 134, 201

Privacy protection, see Information security

Indexing Guidelines

  • Alphabetical order: Letter-by-letter or word-by-word
  • Consistent formatting: Indentation, fonts, punctuation
  • Specific entries: Useful, not overly general
  • Cross-references: Help readers find related information

Cross-Reference Types

  • "See": Directs to preferred term
  • "See also": Points to related entries
  • "See under": Points to subentry
  • "Compare": Highlights differences

Page Number Conventions

  • Single pages: 45, 67, 89
  • Ranges: 15-18 (continuous coverage)
  • Multiple mentions: 23, 35, 67-70
  • Bold: 45 (main discussion)
  • Italics: 67 (illustrations)
