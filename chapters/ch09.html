9.01 Introduction

Reference matter provides readers with the sources, explanations, and additional information they need to understand and verify your work. They provide detailed information that some readers need while keeping the main text focused.

Types of Reference Matter

  • Footnotes: Brief explanations at bottom of pages
  • Endnotes: Detailed notes at end of sections or document
  • Bibliography: Complete list of sources consulted
  • Index: Alphabetical guide to document contents
  • Appendices: Supporting documents and data

Reference Matter Organization

Document structure:

  • Main text with footnotes
  • Appendices (A, B, C, etc.)
  • Bibliography
  • Index

Reference example: This guide includes Appendix A: French Words in English Text as an example of specialized reference material for Canadian bilingual documents.

9.03 Bibliography

A bibliography lists all sources consulted during research, whether cited directly in the text or not.

Bibliography Organization

  • Alphabetical order: By author's surname
  • Consistent format: Same style throughout
  • Complete information: All publication details
  • Indentation: Hanging indent for each entry

Bibliography Formats

Book: Smith, John. Canadian Government Policy. Ottawa: Government Press, 2023.

Journal Article: Brown, Sarah. "Policy Implementation Challenges." Canadian Public Administration 45, no. 2 (2023): 123-145.

9.04 Citations

Citations acknowledge sources and allow readers to verify information.

Citation Styles

  • Author-Date: (Smith, 2023, p. 45)
  • Numbered: [1] or Â¹
  • Legal: Case citations and statutes

Citation Examples

In-text citation: Recent studies indicate improvement (Johnson, 2023, p. 45).

Numbered system: Economic indicators suggest growth [1].

9.05 Indexes

An index is an alphabetical list of names, places, and subjects mentioned in a document, with page numbers where they can be found.

Index Guidelines

  • Alphabetical order: Strict alphabetical sorting
  • Cross-references: "See" and "See also" entries
  • Subentries: Indented related topics
  • Page ranges: Use en dash for ranges (15-18)

Index Entry Examples

Simple entry: Government policy, 45-48, 67

With subentries:
Cabinet, 23-25
  decisions, 24
  meetings, 23, 25

9.06 Electronic Sources

Digital sources require special citation formats and considerations.

Electronic Citation Elements

  • URLs: Full web addresses when stable
  • Access dates: When content may change
  • DOI numbers: When available for academic sources
  • Version information: For databases and software

Electronic Source Examples

Website: Statistics Canada. "Population Growth Rate." https://www.statcan.gc.ca/population-growth (accessed March 15, 2024).

Online report: Treasury Board Secretariat. Digital Government Strategy. PDF. Ottawa: TBS, 2023. https://www.tbs-sct.gc.ca/digital-strategy.

9.07 Legal References

Legal documents require specialized citation formats.

Legal Citation Types

  • Statutes: Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46
  • Cases: R. v. Smith, [2023] SCC 15
  • Regulations: SOR/2023-123
  • Treaties: International agreements

Legal Citation Examples

Court case: Reference re Secession of Quebec, [1998] 2 S.C.R. 217.

Statute: Official Languages Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 31 (4th Supp.).

9.08 Advanced Indexing

Advanced indexing techniques help readers navigate complex documents efficiently.

Advanced Techniques

  • Multiple indexes: Subject, author, geographical
  • Cross-referencing: Connecting related concepts
  • Hierarchical entries: Main topics with subtopics
  • Special symbols: Indicating types of references

Cross-Reference Examples

See reference: Automobile insurance. See Vehicle insurance

See also reference: Policy implementation, 45-67. See also Government procedures
