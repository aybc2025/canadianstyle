Plain Language

Write clearly and concisely for your readers

13.01 Introduction

Plain language is writing that your intended readers can understand and use the first time they read it. It's not about "dumbing down" your contentâ€”it's about clear communication that respects your reader's time and needs.

Benefits of Plain Language

  • Saves time: Readers understand your message quickly
  • Reduces errors: Clear instructions prevent mistakes
  • Improves accessibility: More people can understand your content
  • Builds trust: Transparency shows respect for readers
  • Saves money: Fewer inquiries and clarifications needed
Before and After

Complex: "Prior to the commencement of the project implementation phase, it is imperative that all stakeholders convene for the purpose of establishing parameters."

Plain: "Before starting the project, all stakeholders must meet to set guidelines."

Plain Language Principles

  1. Know your audience
  2. Organize for your reader
  3. Use familiar words
  4. Write short, clear sentences
  5. Use active voice
  6. Design for easy reading
  7. Test your document

13.02 Focusing on the Reader

Successful plain language writing starts with understanding your audience. Before you write, ask yourself:

Key Questions About Your Readers

  • Who are they? Age, education, cultural background
  • What do they know? Their familiarity with your topic
  • What do they need? Information, instructions, or decisions
  • How will they use it? Reference, action, or understanding
  • What concerns them? Time, cost, complexity, or risk

Use "You" and Other Pronouns

Personal pronouns make your writing more direct and engaging:

Example 13.02a

Impersonal: "Applicants must submit their forms by the deadline."

Personal: "You must submit your form by the deadline."


Impersonal: "The department will process applications within 30 days."

Personal: "We will process your application within 30 days."

Address Reader Concerns First

Lead with what matters most to your reader:

Example 13.02b

Writer-focused: "The department has revised its policies following extensive consultation..."

Reader-focused: "Your benefits will increase by 15% starting January 1..."

Answer the "So What?" Question

Always explain why information matters to the reader:

  • How does this affect them?
  • What should they do?
  • When do they need to act?
  • What happens if they don't?

13.04 Vocabulary

Choose words your readers know and use. Simple, familiar words communicate more effectively than complex alternatives.

Use Everyday Words

Replace formal or technical terms with plain alternatives:

Instead of... Use...
commence start, begin
utilize use
prior to before
subsequent to after
in the event that if
notwithstanding despite, even though
ameliorate improve
expeditious quick, fast

Avoid Jargon and Acronyms

Technical terms and acronyms can exclude readers:

Example 13.04a

Jargon: "The SMEs will conduct a gap analysis of the KPIs."

Plain: "Subject matter experts will analyze which performance measures we're missing."

If you must use technical terms:

  • Define them on first use
  • Include a glossary for multiple terms
  • Use them consistently

Be Concrete and Specific

Vague language confuses readers. Be precise:

Example 13.04b

Vague: "Submit your application in a timely manner."

Specific: "Submit your application by March 31."


Vague: "A majority of respondents agreed."

Specific: "73% of respondents agreed."

13.05 Sentences

Clear sentences are the building blocks of plain language. Keep them short, simple, and direct.

Keep Sentences Short

Aim for an average of 15-20 words per sentence:

Example 13.05a

Long (42 words): "The committee, after extensive deliberation and consultation with various stakeholders including community members, business owners, and municipal officials, has determined that the proposed development, subject to certain conditions being met, should proceed as planned."


Short and clear: "The committee consulted stakeholders and decided to approve the development. The approval includes specific conditions."

Use Active Voice

Active voice makes your writing more direct and easier to understand:

Active vs. Passive

Passive: "The report was written by the consultant."

Active: "The consultant wrote the report."


Passive: "Your application will be reviewed by our staff."

Active: "Our staff will review your application."

Put the Main Idea First

Don't bury important information in the middle of sentences:

Example 13.05b

Buried: "Although there are several options available, and while cost is certainly a factor, the safety considerations, which have been thoroughly evaluated, indicate that Option B is recommended."


Clear: "We recommend Option B for safety reasons. While other options exist and cost is important, safety is our priority."

Use Positive Language

Tell readers what to do, not what to avoid:

  • Negative: "Do not submit incomplete forms."
    Positive: "Submit complete forms."
  • Negative: "You cannot park here except on weekends."
    Positive: "You can park here on weekends only."

Avoid Noun Strings

Break up chains of nouns that modify each other:

Example 13.05c

Noun string: "employee workplace safety training program evaluation"

Clear: "evaluation of the safety training program for employees"

13.06 Layout and Design

Good design makes your content easier to read and understand. Use visual elements to guide readers through your document.

Use Descriptive Headings

Headings help readers find information quickly:

Effective Headings

Vague: "Section 3: Procedures"

Descriptive: "How to Apply for Benefits"


Vague: "Important Information"

Descriptive: "Deadlines You Must Meet"

Create Visual Hierarchy

  • Use consistent heading levels
  • Make headings stand out with size and weight
  • Leave white space around important elements
  • Use bullet points for lists
  • Highlight key information with boxes or sidebars

Use Lists Effectively

Lists make information easier to scan and remember:

When to Use Lists

  • Numbered lists: For sequential steps or ranked items
  • Bullet lists: For items of equal importance
  • Keep parallel structure: Start each item the same way

Design for Scanning

Most readers scan before reading in detail. Help them by:

âœ“ Design Checklist

  • Using short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max)
  • Adding subheadings every 2-3 paragraphs
  • Bolding key terms (sparingly)
  • Using tables for complex comparisons
  • Adding visuals to clarify concepts

Choose Readable Fonts

  • Use 12-point font minimum for body text
  • Choose sans-serif fonts for online reading
  • Maintain high contrast (black on white)
  • Avoid ALL CAPS except for short headings

13.07 Testing

Testing ensures your document works for your readers. Never assumeâ€”always verify that your message is clear.

Test with Real Readers

The best test is with people who represent your audience:

  1. Find test readers: 3-5 people similar to your audience
  2. Ask them to use the document: Don't explainâ€”let them figure it out
  3. Observe and ask questions:
    • What's confusing?
    • What's missing?
    • Can they complete the task?
    • Would they recommend changes?
  4. Revise based on feedback: Fix problem areas

Readability Formulas

While not perfect, readability scores provide quick feedback:

Common Readability Tests

  • Flesch Reading Ease: Aim for 60+ (plain English)
  • Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level: Aim for grades 6-8
  • Gunning Fog Index: Aim for 12 or lower

Most word processors include readability statistics in their spelling/grammar tools.
