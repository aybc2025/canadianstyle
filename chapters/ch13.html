Plain Language

Write clearly and concisely for your readers

13.01 Introduction 13.02 Reader Focus 13.03 Organization 13.04 Vocabulary 13.05 Sentences 13.06 Layout & Design 13.07 Testing

13.01 Introduction

Plain language is writing that your intended readers can understand and use the first time they read it. It's not about "dumbing down"—it's about clear communication that works.

Key Principle

Write for your readers, not for yourself. Every word, sentence, and paragraph should serve your reader's needs.

Benefits of Plain Language

  • Saves time - for both readers and writers
  • Reduces errors - clear instructions prevent mistakes
  • Improves compliance - people follow what they understand
  • Builds trust - transparency through clarity
  • Reduces costs - fewer questions and clarifications needed

Plain Language vs. Complex Language

Complex Plain Language
"Due to the fact that..." "Because..."
"In order to..." "To..."
"At this point in time" "Now"
"Utilize" "Use"

13.02 Reader Focus

Everything you write should be designed around your readers' needs, knowledge level, and goals.

Know Your Readers

  • Who are they? - Job roles, education, experience level
  • What do they know? - Technical knowledge, context, background
  • What do they need? - Information, action steps, decisions
  • How will they use it? - Reference, step-by-step, overview
  • When will they read it? - Under pressure, at leisure, repeatedly

Writing for Different Audiences

For Experts

"Implement multi-factor authentication protocols to enhance security posture."

For General Users

"Use two-step login to keep your account safe. This means entering both your password and a code from your phone."

💡 Reader-Focused Questions

  • Will my readers understand this term?
  • Have I given them enough context?
  • Is this the level of detail they need?
  • Can they act on this information?

13.03 Organization

Organize information in a logical order that matches how your readers think and work.

Organizational Strategies

  • Chronological - Step-by-step processes, procedures
  • Order of Importance - Most critical information first
  • General to Specific - Big picture, then details
  • Problem to Solution - What's wrong, how to fix it
  • Question and Answer - Address readers' concerns directly

Document Structure

1. Purpose Statement

Tell readers immediately why this document exists and what they'll learn.

2. Key Information

Put the most important information where readers will see it first.

3. Supporting Details

Provide background, explanations, and additional information.

4. Next Steps

Tell readers what to do with this information.

Organization Checklist

  • Does the opening tell readers what to expect?
  • Is the most important information easy to find?
  • Do headings clearly indicate content?
  • Does the order make sense to readers?
  • Are related ideas grouped together?

13.04 Vocabulary

Choose words that your readers know and use. Prefer common, concrete words over complex or abstract ones.

Word Choice Guidelines

  • Use familiar words - Choose words readers know
  • Be concrete - Specific words are clearer than vague ones
  • Avoid jargon - Unless you're sure readers understand it
  • Define technical terms - When you must use them
  • Use consistent terminology - Don't vary words for the same concept

Plain Language Word Choices

Instead of... Use... Why Better
Facilitate Help, make easier More common, concrete
Endeavour Try Shorter, simpler
Subsequently Later, then More familiar
Methodology Method, way Less pretentious
Approximately About Shorter
Commence Start, begin More natural

⚠️ Avoid Unnecessary Complexity

Government writing often includes unnecessary formal language. Remember: clarity serves citizens better than complexity.

13.05 Sentences

Write clear, direct sentences that express one main idea. Vary sentence length for rhythm, but keep most sentences under 20 words.

Sentence Guidelines

  • Average 15-20 words - Easier to follow
  • Use active voice - More direct and engaging
  • Put the action in verbs - Not in nouns
  • Use positive statements - Instead of negative ones when possible
  • Break up long sentences - One idea per sentence

Sentence Improvements

Complex: "The implementation of the new policy requires the completion of training by all employees prior to the commencement of the new fiscal year."

Plain: "All employees must complete training before the new fiscal year starts."

Passive: "Mistakes were made in the processing of applications."

Active: "We made mistakes when processing applications."

Eliminate Redundancy

  • "Free gift" → "Gift"
  • "End result" → "Result"
  • "Past experience" → "Experience"
  • "Future plans" → "Plans"
  • "Advance planning" → "Planning"

13.06 Layout & Design

Good design makes information accessible. Use visual elements to guide readers and highlight important information.

Design Principles

  • Use white space - Give readers' eyes a rest
  • Write helpful headings - Tell readers what's coming
  • Use lists - Break up dense paragraphs
  • Highlight key information - But don't overdo it
  • Choose readable fonts - Simple, clean typefaces

Visual Design Checklist

  • Headings: Do they tell readers what to expect?
  • Lists: Are they used for series and steps?
  • Paragraphs: Are they short (3-5 sentences)?
  • Font size: Is it large enough to read easily?
  • Contrast: Can readers with vision problems read it?
  • Layout: Does it look organized and professional?

Before and After: Layout

Before: Wall of Text

To apply for benefits you must complete form ABC-123 and submit it with supporting documents including proof of income tax returns for the previous year medical records if applicable and two pieces of identification to the office located at 123 Main Street during business hours Monday through Friday 9 AM to 4 PM or you can mail it to PO Box 456.

After: Organized Information

To apply for benefits:

  1. Complete form ABC-123
  2. Gather supporting documents:
    • Proof of income
    • Previous year's tax returns
    • Medical records (if applicable)
    • Two pieces of identification
  3. Submit in person or by mail

Office hours: Monday-Friday, 9 AM-4 PM

Address: 123 Main Street or PO Box 456

13.07 Testing

Test your writing with real users to ensure it actually works. The best measure of plain language is whether readers can use it successfully.

Testing Methods

  • User testing - Watch people use your document
  • Readability tests - Check grade level and complexity
  • Expert review - Have plain language experts review it
  • Field testing - Try it in real situations
  • Feedback collection - Ask users what works and what doesn't

Testing Questions

Understanding
  • Can readers find the information they need?
  • Do they understand what you're asking them to do?
  • Are there terms that confuse them?
Usability
  • Can they complete the task successfully?
  • How long does it take them?
  • Where do they get stuck?

💡 Simple Testing Tips

  • Read your writing out loud
  • Ask a colleague to review it
  • Use readability tools
  • Time how long it takes to complete tasks
  • Track where people ask for help

Remember

Plain language isn't about dumbing down—it's about opening up. When you write clearly, you show respect for your readers' time and intelligence.
