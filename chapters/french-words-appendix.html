French Words in English Text

Guidelines for incorporating French words, phrases, and expressions in Canadian English documents

A.01 Introduction A.02 General Principles A.03 Italicization A.04 Accent Marks A.05 Plural Forms A.06 Capitalization A.07 Common Examples A.08 Decision Guide

A.01 Introduction

As Canada's official languages are English and French, Canadian English frequently incorporates French words and phrases. This appendix provides guidance on proper usage, spelling, and presentation of French expressions in English documents.

Key Principles

  • Italics: Use italics for French words not fully adopted into English
  • Accents: Retain French accent marks when they appear in the source language
  • Plurals: Follow French plural rules for French words, English rules for adopted words
  • Context: Consider your audience's familiarity with French expressions
  • Consistency: Apply formatting rules consistently throughout documents

Canadian Bilingual Context

Canadian government documents frequently use French terms like vis-à-vis, raison d'être, and place names like Québec, Montréal.

A.02 General Principles

The integration of French words into English text follows specific principles that balance accuracy with readability.

Integration Guidelines

  • Dictionary check: Consult English dictionaries for adoption status
  • Audience consideration: Consider readers' familiarity with French
  • Consistency: Use same treatment throughout document
  • Explanation: Define unfamiliar terms on first use

Levels of Integration

Fully adopted: café, garage, boutique (no italics needed)

Partially adopted: avant-garde, liaison (italics recommended)

Not adopted: raison d'être, savoir-vivre (italics required)

A.03 Italicization Rules

Italicization helps readers identify French words and understand their special status in English text.

Always Italicize

  • Unfamiliar phrases: raison d'être, coup de grâce
  • Technical terms: mise en scène, tête-à-tête
  • Complete sentences: Direct quotations in French
  • First occurrence: When introducing French terms

Don't Italicize

  • Common borrowed words: café, depot, elite
  • Place names: Montreal, Quebec, Vancouver
  • Proper nouns: Names of people, organizations
  • Well-established terms: à la carte, rendez-vous

Italicization Examples

✓ Correct: The exhibition featured avant-garde Canadian artists.

✓ Correct: We met at the local café for lunch.

✗ Incorrect: We met at the local café for lunch.

A.04 Accent Marks

French accent marks should be retained in English text to maintain accuracy and respect for the source language.

French Accent Marks

  • Acute accent (é): café, résumé, cliché
  • Grave accent (è, à, ù): très, à la carte, où
  • Circumflex (ê, î, ô, û, â): crêpe, maître d'hôtel
  • Diaeresis (ë, ï): naïve, Noël
  • Cedilla (ç): français, façade

Accent Usage Examples

✓ Retain accents: The résumé was très impressive.

✓ Place names: The conference was held in Québec.

✗ Don't drop accents: The resume was tres impressive.

Technical Considerations

  • Word processing: Use proper Unicode characters
  • Web content: Ensure proper character encoding
  • Email: Test accent display in different systems
  • Print: Verify accent marks appear correctly

A.05 Plural Forms

French words in English text may follow either French or English pluralization rules, depending on their level of integration.

French Plurals

Use French plural forms for words not fully adopted into English:

  • -eau → -eaux: bureau → bureaux
  • -eu → -eux: adieu → adieux
  • -al → -aux: journal → journaux
  • Silent -s: corps → corps (same form)

English Plurals

Use English plural forms for adopted words:

  • restaurant → restaurants
  • garage → garages
  • boutique → boutiques
  • technique → techniques

Plural Examples

French plural: Several bureaux were involved in the decision.

English plural: The restaurants in Ottawa offer diverse cuisine.

A.06 Capitalization

French capitalization rules differ from English and should be maintained when using French text within English documents.

French Title Capitalization

  • Only first word: Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns
  • Proper nouns: Names of people, places, organizations
  • After colon: Capitalize word after colon if it starts new sentence
  • Articles: Don't capitalize articles, prepositions (except when first word)

Capitalization Examples

French title: Le guide du rédacteur

English equivalent: The Writer's Guide

Place names: Rivière-du-Loup, Sault Ste. Marie

Direction Words

Capitalize French direction words when part of proper names:

  • Capitalized: Amérique du Nord, Caroline du Sud
  • Lowercase: 30 kilomètres à l'est de Gatineau

A.07 Common Examples

Here are frequently used French words and phrases in Canadian government writing with proper formatting.

Government and Politics

vis-à-vis: The department's position vis-à-vis the new regulations

raison d'être: Public service is the raison d'être of government agencies

fait accompli: The merger was presented as a fait accompli

Business and Culture

entrepreneur: The program supports young entrepreneurs

avant-garde: The museum featured avant-garde Canadian artists

connoisseur: She is a connoisseur of Canadian wine

Common Errors to Avoid

✗ Missing accents: The cafe serves excellent coffee

✓ Correct: The café serves excellent coffee

✗ Wrong plural: Several bureaus were involved

✓ Correct: Several bureaux were involved

A.08 Decision Guide

Use this step-by-step guide to determine how to format French words in your English documents.

📋 Decision Tree for French Words

  1. Is the word in a standard English dictionary?
    • Yes → Use normal (non-italic) formatting
    • No → Continue to step 2
  2. Is it a proper noun (name of place, person, institution)?
    • Yes → Use normal formatting, retain accents
    • No → Continue to step 3
  3. Is it a complete phrase or expression?
    • Yes → Use italics, retain accents
    • No → Continue to step 4
  4. Is it commonly used in your field or context?
    • Yes → May use normal formatting
    • No → Use italics

Quick Reference Examples

Dictionary word: café, garage, boutique

Proper noun: Québec, Jean-Baptiste, Université Laval

Complete phrase: raison d'être, coup de grâce

Field-specific: mise en scène (theatre), terroir (wine)
